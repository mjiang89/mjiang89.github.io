Mandarin Primer — Lesson 19

Renting a House

Lesson 19 moves from factories and public institutions to private domestic life. The speakers spot a rental notice from a streetcar, inspect a large traditional courtyard residence, debate comfort and convenience, negotiate the rent, and then discover that the bargain may be connected to the house’s reputation as a haunted house. The lesson is especially rich for teaching housing vocabulary, spatial description, facilities, bargaining, and the clash between practicality and superstition.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so the page stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow the full sequence of renting a house: noticing the sign, getting off the streetcar, inspecting the property, asking about utilities, discussing price, paying a deposit, and reacting to the address.
  • Understand vocabulary for house-hunting: rental signs, keeper, rooms, courtyards, main rooms, gatekeeper’s room, kitchen, servants’ quarters, backyard, pond, vegetable garden, wiring, tap water, bathroom, bathtub, flush toilet, deposit, receipt, and lease folder.
  • Recognize descriptive comparison patterns such as one courtyard being larger than the next, and how hair ... ne keeps adding “more” aspects of what is being examined.
  • See how the dialogue balances practical concerns like light, water, shopping access, and transportation against emotional concerns like haunted-house rumors.
  • Learn how Mandarin handles approval, surprise, contempt, and teasing through particles and exclamations such as Ee, E, Eh, and Ch!.
  • Understand the humorous ending in which cheap rent is suddenly reinterpreted as suspiciously cheap.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 19 teaches that describing a house is never just about architecture. It is also about comfort, family imagination, urban convenience, money, status, and beliefs.

For high-school students, this lesson is highly engaging because it feels like a real argument: one person sees opportunity, the other sees vegetables, then suddenly ghosts enter the conversation. It is also one of the best lessons for teaching how detailed physical description supports decision-making.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Streetcar hook
Begin with the cry of discovery: “For Rent!” and the argument over whether such a thing could even exist now.
2
Entering the house
Teach the keeper, room counts, courtyards, reception hall, main rooms, and echoes.
3
Living conditions
Teach height, coolness, winter cold, kitchen, servants’ quarters, pond, fish, garden, and vegetables.
4
Utilities and facilities
Focus on electric wiring, tap water, bathroom, bathtub, flush toilet, and reinstallation.
5
Rent and decision
Teach price, central location, deposit, receipt, landlord, contract, and address.
6
Cheapness and ghosts + quiz
End with the haunted-house twist, teasing about superstition, and the final decision to take the house anyway.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source’s “To the Student” guidance still applies: utterances first, then notes, then fluency through repetition and exercises. Lesson 19 becomes especially teachable when students can visualize the house as they walk through it.
  1. Draw a courtyard-house floor plan as the keeper introduces each space.
  2. Use color coding for “advantages” and “problems”: big rooms, cool summer, pond, garden, central location versus winter cold, missing fixtures, and ghost reputation.
  3. Pause at each new discovery and ask students: would this make you more likely or less likely to rent the house?
  4. Teach hair ... ne as a discourse move for adding surprising new good features during inspection.
  5. Act out the ending as comedy so students hear the shift from practical negotiation to emotional suspicion.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students may focus only on vocabulary lists, but the deeper challenge is noticing how the speakers gradually reinterpret the same house: first impressive, then convenient, then suspiciously cheap.
  • hair ... ne repeatedly signals “there is still more to notice,” not just a simple “still.”
  • jian in Beijing housing counts bays between columns, not always what English speakers would count as full rooms.
  • farngjian vs. utz matter: one is room as usable space, the other more like the thing or structure.
  • -hao in kann-hao means reaching a satisfactory conclusion, not simply “good.”
  • pyanyi jiow pyanyi tzay... is a special explanatory frame meaning “as for its cheapness, that is exactly where the cheapness lies.”
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 19 as discovery, inspection, and reinterpretation

This lesson becomes clearer when divided into four scenes.

Scene 1 'Jau-tzu,' 'Jau-tzu,' 'Jyifarng jau-tzu'! ... Chiing nii dawle shiah-i-jann tyng i-tyng!
“For rent,” “For rent,” “vacancy for rent”! Please stop at the next station!
Scene 1 Jiann-goei le ... jeh niantourl naal yeou jau-tzu de farngtz? ... Jende me, woo chinyean kannjiann de me.
You must be seeing apparitions ... where nowadays can there be houses for rent? ... It is true, I saw it with my own eyes.
Scene 1 Tzeem mei ren a? Nii tzay duo enn leang.shiall! ... Yee sheu diannliengl huay le ba? Tzarmen chiaux men kann.
Why is there nobody here? Ring some more! ... Perhaps the bell is broken? Let us knock on the door.
Scene 2 Igonq sanshyr'ell-jian ... Jeybial sh menfarngl, neybial sh chefarng, yuanntz neybial sh ig san-kaijian de dah-kehting.
Thirty-two bays altogether ... this side is the gateman’s room, the other side is the ricksha room, and across the courtyard is a large three-bay reception hall.
Scene 2 Shanqfarng tzay liitou i-jinn, liitou hair yeou leangg yuanntz ne ... Hao dah de farngjian. E, hair yeou hweisheng ne. Uai!
The main rooms are one court farther in, and there are two more courtyards inside ... What big rooms. And there are even echoes too. Yoohoo!
Scene 2 Jeh farngdieengl jen gau. ... Gau hao, shiahtian liang.kuay. ... Keesh dongtian leeng e!
The ceilings are really high. ... High is good, cool in summer. ... But cold in winter!
Scene 2 Howyuall bii jenq-yuanntz hair genq dah le; hair yeou leang-ke songshuh, nail hair yeou sheuduo jwutz, neyg chyrtz.lii meijoel hair keeyii yeang-yu ne.
The backyard is even larger than the main courtyard; there are two pine trees, lots of bamboo, and the pond there might even be used for raising fish.
Scene 2 Neybial hair yeou g tsayyuan ne ... keeyii jonq deal bairtsay, bortsay, luo.bo, shihorngshyh ...
There is even a vegetable garden there ... we could plant cabbages, spinach, radishes, tomatoes ...
Scene 3 Jeh farngtz de men, chuang, dihbaan, diingbaan shermde, dou hair buhuay, ar? ... Jeh farngtz yeou dianndeng tzyhlai-shoei meiyeou?
The doors, windows, floors, ceilings and so on are still not bad, aren’t they? ... Does this house have electric lights and tap water?
Scene 3 Diannshiann dou yeou, jiowsh mei jie-diann. Tzyhlai-shoei yee yeou. ... Beenlai dawsh yeou [shiitzao-farng], keesh tamen ban-tzoou de shyrhowl jiow bae tsyrtiee de tzaopern, choushoei-maatoong shermde dou geei chaile.shiahlai ban-tzoou le.
The wiring is there, only the electricity is not connected. There is also tap water. ... There used to be a bathroom, but when the previous tenants moved out they dismantled and removed the enameled tub, flush toilet, and such things.
Scene 3 Jyy yaw shoei-goantz hair yeou, woomen keeyii mae le tzay juang.chiilai. E - jeh farngtz tzu sherm jiah.chyan?
As long as the pipes are still there, we can buy the things and install them again. What is the rent for this house?
Scene 4 Tzu.chyan sh wuushyr'ell-kuay chyan ig yueh. ... Jell bush Lao Heryall ma? ... shyrjii-fen jong jiow yeou shyhchaang gen tzarhuoh-pull ...
The rent is fifty-two dollars a month. ... This is Old Riverside, isn’t it? ... in ten-some minutes there are markets and general stores ...
Scene 4 Tzarmen jiow idinq yaw ha. ... Fuh ellshyrwuu-kuay chyan ha ... woo geei Nin kai g shoutyaul ... shiahwuu woo jiow chiuh bawgaw farngdong chiuh geei Nin yuhbey jertz.
Then let us definitely take it. ... I will pay twenty-five dollars first ... I will write you a receipt ... this afternoon I shall report to the landlord and prepare the lease folder.
Scene 4 Lao Heryall shyrsan-haw ... jeh bush jeh dihfangl de Syh-dah Shiongjair lii de ig shiongjair ma? ... Yuanlai pyanyi jiow pyanyi tzay jeyg shanq!
No. 13 Old Riverside ... isn’t this one of the Four Great Haunted Houses here? ... So that is exactly where the cheapness lies!
Closing Nii yow jiann-goei le. ... Naj g nantzyy-hann, itourl tyichanq pohchwu mishinn, itourl hair pah goei ne ... Nii bu pah woo yee bu pah. ... Neme jeh farngtz hairsh yaw le? Yaw.
You are seeing ghosts again. ... With all the dignity of a he-man, promoting the abolition of superstition and still fearing ghosts ... If you are not afraid, I am not afraid either ... Then we still want the house? Yes.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Discovery begins with disbelief

jau-tzu
jiann-goei le
  • The rental sign is immediately framed as almost unbelievable in the current housing situation.
  • That makes the entire house visit feel like finding unexpected luck.

2) The house is described as a sequence of expanding spaces

yuanntz
jinn
shanqfarng
  • The lesson leads the visitors deeper and deeper into the residence.
  • This creates spatial suspense while teaching the vocabulary of traditional courtyard housing.

3) hair ... ne marks added discoveries

more to notice
inspection discourse
  • The notes explain that hair ... ne signals interest in new aspects still being discovered.
  • It is one of the lesson’s most important discourse patterns.

4) Good features are always paired with tradeoffs

high ceiling
summer cool
winter cold
  • The dialogue never presents a feature as purely good or bad.
  • This is excellent for teaching balanced evaluation in Mandarin.

5) Domestic imagination transforms empty space into home

goldfish pond
vegetable garden
cabbage
tomato
  • The wife immediately imagines fish and vegetables, turning the house from structure into lived space.
  • This helps students see how language reveals values and priorities.

6) Modern convenience matters

dianndeng
tzyhlai-shoei
shiitzao-farng
  • The house may be large and elegant, but questions about electricity, water, and bathrooms are decisive.
  • This makes the lesson feel modern and practical.

7) Renting has its own speech routines

dinq.chyan
shoutyaul
jertz
  • The lesson teaches deposit, receipt, landlord reporting, and the lease folder as procedural vocabulary.
  • It therefore goes beyond description into transaction language.

8) Cheapness becomes an explanation problem

yuanlai
pyanyi jiow pyanyi tzay...
shiongjair
  • The ending reinterprets the low rent: it is not merely lucky, but perhaps cheap for a reason.
  • This is the comic twist that gives the lesson its memorable ending.

High-value classroom move

Use a “Would you rent it?” debate

Split the class into two sides: one side argues for renting the house, the other argues against it.
  1. Students must use house features from the lesson as evidence.
  2. One team emphasizes size, garden, location, and cheap rent.
  3. The other emphasizes winter cold, missing bathroom fixtures, and haunted-house reputation.

This turns vocabulary into real decision-making and helps students remember the lesson’s flow.

Teaching payoff

This lesson models how Mandarin can move from observation to judgment to action.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Describe the house

Students describe the property room by room, courtyard by courtyard, as if leading a visitor through it.

Task B — What is still there?

Students sort features into still present, removed by former tenants, and imagined future uses.

Task C — Practical or emotional?

Students decide whether each concern belongs more to comfort, convenience, cost, or superstition.

Task D — Rewrite the ending

Students invent one more exchange after the ghost argument and decide whether the couple signs the contract.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to the notes, what does hair ... ne mainly express in this lesson?

Correct. The notes explicitly explain that hair ... ne signals that more is still being discovered about the thing under inspection.
Not quite. This is one of the lesson’s key discourse notes, and it is central to how the house inspection unfolds.

2) What is the best explanation of the husband’s line pyanyi jiow pyanyi tzay jeyg shanq at the end?

Correct. The phrase means that the explanation for the cheapness lies precisely in that frightening fact.
Not quite. The line is not just about price; it is about discovering the hidden explanation for the bargain.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Name two features of the house that make it attractive.
  2. Name one thing that the former tenants removed.
  3. Explain, in English, why the house seems so cheap.
  4. Give one example of how the wife imagines turning the house into a home.
  5. State the couple’s final decision at the end of the lesson.
