Mandarin Primer — Lesson 7

Mr. Can’t Stop Talking

Lesson 7 is an energetic character portrait and one of the earliest lessons that teaches Mandarin through personality, evaluation, and comparison. It presents a humorous man who talks constantly, and through that comic setup it introduces comparison structures, degrees of quality, habitual action, interruption, amount, frequency, and condition. The lesson is excellent for helping beginners see how Mandarin expresses more, less, most, as…as, not so…as, the more…the more, and other relational meanings.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so it stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow and discuss a humorous descriptive narrative about a person with a strong habit: always talking.
  • Use and understand comparison patterns such as A bii B hao, A yeou B nemm hao, and A mei.yeou B nemm hao.
  • Recognize superlative/intensive forms like tzuey and diing.
  • Understand no-matter / generality expressions such as sweibiann and bugoan.
  • Notice degree patterns like yuh duo yuh hao and yueh ... yueh ....
  • See how Mandarin handles qualities, evaluation, gossip, interruption, and conditional impossibility in natural speech.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 7 teaches relational thinking. Students compare people, speeds, quantities, qualities, and habits rather than just naming objects or telling one event.

That makes it ideal for high-school learners, because it connects language to a vivid social type: the person who keeps talking. The humor keeps interest high, while the grammar carries serious conceptual weight.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Character hook
Start by asking students whether they know someone who talks all the time. Build curiosity before grammar.
2
Dialogue as portrait
Read the lesson in chunks: nickname, constant talking, comparisons, opinions on people, eating habits, impossible solutions.
3
Comparison system
Pause and map superior, equal, and inferior degree clearly on the board.
4
Intensity and evaluation
Show how the lesson scales judgment: a little better, pretty bad, terribly bad, the fewer the better.
5
Habit and interruption
Focus on repeated behavior, not remembering to eat, and what happens when someone interrupts him.
6
Retell + quiz
Have students summarize the character and test two key relational patterns.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source still emphasizes whole utterances, echoing, repetition, and learning grammar through exercises. Lesson 7 works best when the grammar is constantly attached to the comic character.
  1. Teach every grammar point through Mr. Can’t Stop Talking, not through abstract sentences first.
  2. Keep a visible “comparison ladder” on the board: better, worse, as good as, not as good as, more and more.
  3. Use performance: let one student act as the talkative man while another tries to interrupt.
  4. Return often to the central joke so the lesson stays memorable.
  5. Have students classify statements as about speed, quality, amount, or habit.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Because the lesson is funny, students may miss how carefully it teaches comparison and degree. Slow down enough that “more/less/as…as” becomes visible.
  • Chinese adjectives are still verbs, so comparison structures do not work like English adjective phrases.
  • bii handles explicit comparison, while .i.deal can express an implicit “-er / more.”
  • tzuey and diing are superlative/intensive, but often function simply as strong intensifiers.
  • ren.jia means “people/others” here, not “a family/household.”
  • chwufei changes meaning depending on whether the following clause is negative or positive.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 7 as a character portrait

This lesson is easiest to teach as a series of observations about one comic person.

A Woo yeou g perng.yeou shinq Tarn, ming.tzyh jiaw Buhtyng.
I have a friend whose surname is Talking and whose personal name is Can’t Stop.
B .He.he, 'Tarn.bu-tyng'!
Ha ha, “Can’t Stop Talking”!
A Jey.wey shian.sheng tzuey ay shuo-huah …
This gentleman most loves talking …
A Suoo.yii ren.jia dou goan .ta jiaw huahshyatz … yee yeou shyr.howl goan .ta jiaw Goangboh Dianntair.
So people call him the Talking Machine … and sometimes even the Broadcasting Station.
A Ta woan.shanq shuey-jiaw .de .shyr.howl jiow shuo menqhuah. Tzao.chin i-shiing jiow chii-tourl gen tzyhjii shuo-huah.
At night when he sleeps he talks in his sleep. In the morning, as soon as he wakes up, he starts talking to himself.
A Sweibiann nii gen .ta shuo sherm shyh, bugoan nii wenn .ta sherm huah, ta tzoong yeou huah gen .nii shuo .de.
No matter what you talk to him about, no matter what you ask him, he always has something to say to you.
A Shuo-wan.le i-jiuh, yow.sh i-jiuh … lao nemm shuo.
He finishes one sentence and starts another … always talking like that.
A Yaw.sh neyg bii jeyg duo, jeyg jiow bii neyg shao.
If that is more than this, then this is less than that.
A Huooche bii luenchwan jiow kuay .de duo. Yow yeou feiji tzoou .de bii sherm dou kuay.
Trains are much faster than steamships. And there are airplanes, which go faster than everything else.
A Huay-ren jiow yuh shao yuh hao. Huay-ren buyaw tay duo; hao-ren .kee.sh yuh duo yuh hao.
As for bad people, the fewer the better. Bad people should not be too many; good people, however, the more the better.
B Nii jey-wey perng.yeou nandaw i-tian-daw-woan lao .tzay.nall shuo-huah .ma?
Do you mean to say that this friend of yours talks all the time from morning to night?
A Ta tsorng tzao.chin liow-dean jong chii-tourl, i-shuo jiow shuo .daw shaang.huo shyr'ell-dean …
He starts at six in the morning, and once he starts, he talks until noon …
A Ta chy dong.shi .de kuay-mann wanchyuan mei.yeou idinq .de.
There is absolutely nothing fixed about the speed with which he eats.
A Ta itourl shuoj itourl chy, shuo .de yueh kuay jiow chy .de yueh duo.
He talks and eats at the same time, and the faster he talks, the more he eats.
B .Ne.me mei fartz jiaw .ta shao shuo .deal .ma?
Then is there no way to make him talk less?
A Nii yueh daa .ta.de chah, ta jiow yueh shuo.bu-tyng — chwufei nii jen wuu.juh .ta.de tzoei.
The more you interrupt him, the less he can stop — unless you literally cover his mouth.
A Chwufei shi-tian chu.le tay.yang, yee buhuey yeou g shuo-wan.le .de ryhtz .de.
Unless the sun rises in the western sky, there will never be a day when he finishes talking.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) A person can be introduced through object-subject structure

yeou + object
object-subject
  • The notes explain that after yeou, an object can also serve as subject to the following predicate.
  • Woo yeou g perng.yeou shinq Tarn works like “I have a friend, and this friend has the surname Tarn.”

2) Superlative words often work as strong intensifiers

tzuey
diing
  • tzuey ay and diing shii.huan both mark a highest degree.
  • The notes explain that these are often used not only strictly as superlatives but also as strong intensifiers.

3) Comparison has a system, not just one word

bii
yeou
mei.yeou
iyanq
  • Superior: A bii B hao
  • Equal: A yeou B (nemm) hao or A gen B iyanq hao
  • Inferior: A mei.yeou B (nemm) hao

4) Implicit comparison can use .i.deal

better
worse
  • The notes explain that .i.deal or .deal can mean “a little more / -er.”
  • This is useful because English often forces a comparison marker where Chinese can be lighter.

5) No-matter expressions widen the statement

sweibiann
bugoan
  • These forms mean “as you please / no matter …”
  • They help the lesson describe Mr. Can’t Stop Talking as someone who always has something to say, regardless of topic or question.

6) The lesson teaches gradients of evaluation

budah hao
jeanjyr heen huay
huay-jyi.le
  • The character describes people with increasingly strong judgments.
  • This is perfect for showing that evaluation is not simply binary but scaled.

7) The more…the more can express moral preference too

yuh duo yuh hao
yuh shao yuh hao
  • The lesson applies degree not only to speed or quantity, but to good and bad people.
  • That gives students a wider sense of how productive the structure is.

8) Conditional impossibility closes the joke

chwufei
shi-tian chu.le tay.yang
  • The notes explain that chwufei plus a negative clause means “unless.”
  • The impossible image “the sun rises in the western sky” makes the closing line memorable and strong.

High-value classroom move

Turn the lesson into a mock character interview

One student plays Mr. Can’t Stop Talking. Another student tries to interview him while a third keeps interrupting.
  1. The “talking” student must keep answering everything.
  2. The class describes him with comparison and evaluation language.
  3. Another student tries to stop him, but the class adds yueh daa .ta.de chah, ta jiow yueh shuo.bu-tyng.

This turns the grammar into a performance and makes the comparison system much more memorable.

A key structural system

Explicit comparison in Lesson 7

Superior

A bii B hao

A is better than B.

Equal

A yeou B (nemm) hao
A gen B iyanq hao

A is as good as B.

Inferior

A mei.yeou B (nemm) hao

A is not as good as B.

Teaching point

This system is one of the most important grammatical takeaways in the lesson, and it should be revisited with many examples.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Character summary

Students describe Mr. Can’t Stop Talking in 3–4 short statements.

Task B — Comparison ladder

Students compare trains, steamships, cars, and airplanes using the lesson’s patterns.

Task C — Better or worse?

Students transform one comparison into equal and inferior forms.

Task D — Interrupt the talker

Students role-play interrupting and then describe what happens using yueh ... yueh ... and chwufei.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to the notes, which pattern expresses that A is not as good as B?

Correct. The notes explicitly give A mei.yeou B (nemm) hao as the inferior comparison pattern: A is not so good as B.
Not quite. Lesson 7 lays out a full comparison system, and the inferior pattern is A mei.yeou B (nemm) hao.

2) Why is the line about the sun rising in the western sky important?

Correct. The notes explain this as a common figure for an impossibility, which makes the comic ending stronger.
Not quite. In Lesson 7, this image is used as an impossibility expression to emphasize that he will never stop talking on his own.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Say who Mr. Can’t Stop Talking is and why people call him that.
  2. Use one superior comparison and one inferior comparison.
  3. Say one sentence with sweibiann or bugoan.
  4. Explain, in English, why interrupting him does not help.
  5. State the lesson’s comic ending in one sentence.
