Mandarin Primer — Lesson 4

Telephoning

Lesson 4 is a major step forward in real-life interaction. Students move into a telephone conversation full of listening problems, numbers, directions, polite address, dates and times, travel methods, requests for repetition, and negotiation about plans. This lesson is ideal for showing that beginner Mandarin is already a system for doing things, not just naming things.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system to stay aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Handle a basic phone exchange with greetings, identification, and number confirmation.
  • Recognize and use numbers, days of the week, and clock time in a conversational setting.
  • Ask about routes and motion: tsorng naal tzoou?, tzeem lai .de?
  • Distinguish different question types, including high-pitched .ma, low-pitched confirming .a, and disjunctive .hair.sh.
  • Use polite forms in phone talk, such as Nin, gueyshinq, shian.sheng, and tairfuu.
  • Request repetition and slower speech when comprehension breaks down.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 4 is the first high-pressure communication scene. The speaker cannot see the other person and must rely on sound, clarification, confirmation, and repair.

That makes this lesson especially powerful for teaching listening strategy. Students see that real conversation includes not understanding, asking again, hearing incorrectly, checking names and numbers, and negotiating plans.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Sound and situation
Begin with a ringing-phone scenario. Ask what students might need to say when they cannot see the speaker.
2
Chunk the conversation
Teach the dialogue in stages: ringing and locating, greeting and identification, date/time questions, travel details, comprehension repair, planning.
3
Number and time drill
Practice telephone numbers, dates, weekdays, and o’clock expressions separately before reinserting them into the conversation.
4
Question types in action
Show how different intonation and particles change the communicative force of the question.
5
Repair strategies
Focus on not understanding: asking for slower speech, asking again, checking meaning, and hearing key details.
6
Role play + quiz
End with paired telephone role plays and two understanding checks on core structures.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source pages emphasize utterances, echoing, repetition, and grammar learned through exercises. Lesson 4 works best when students experience it as a performance scene, not as a vocabulary list.
  1. Teach with one student facing away from the other, to simulate no visual cues.
  2. Separate the lesson into subskills: numbers, greetings, names, dates, travel, clarification.
  3. Use repeated mini-dialogues such as Sherm.me? Woo ting.bu-doong.
  4. Keep a visible timeline on the board for tzwol / jiel / miengl and weekdays.
  5. Have students compare what is hard in phone talk versus face-to-face talk.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

English-speaking beginners often think conversation is mainly about content words. Lesson 4 shows that conversation is also about control signals: checking, slowing down, repeating, and choosing between alternatives.
  • Uai is a telephone or hailing “Hello,” not a general greeting hello.
  • Nin naal? on the phone asks “what place are you?” rather than directly “who are you?”
  • Disjunctive .hair.sh is different from non-disjunctive .huoh.jee.
  • ting.bu-doong and related patterns use potential complements, not a separate word for “can’t.”
  • .de after time expressions often highlights a specific past point: “the plane that arrived at twelve o’clock.”
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 4 as a live communication sequence

This lesson is best understood as a sequence of communicative problems and repairs.

Scene 1 Ting! Nii ting! Sherm sheang? Sherm sheng.in sheang?
Listen! What is ringing? What sound is it?
Scene 1 Sh menliengl .hair.sh diannhuah .ia?
Is it the doorbell or the telephone?
Scene 1 Diannhuah tzay naal? Tsorng naal tzoou? Tzeem tzoou?
Where is the telephone? Which way do I go? How do I go?
Scene 2 .Uai, .uai, Nin naal? Nin sh neei-i-wey? Gueyshinq .a?
Hello? What place are you? Who are you, please? What is your honorable surname?
Scene 2 Shinq Wang .a? Wang .Shian.sheng Tairfuu .sh-?
Your surname is Wang? And your courtesy name / personal style, Mr. Wang, is…?
Scene 3 .Oh, nii jiow.sh Wang Ell! Woo mei ting.chu.lai jiow.sh nii.
Oh, it is just Wang Ell! I didn’t make out that it was you.
Scene 3 Nii tzeem tzemm.shie shyr.howl yee mei lai kann woo .ia?
Why haven’t you come to see me for such a long time?
Scene 4 Nii lai.guoh .ma? Nii sherm .shyr.howl lai .de? Neei-tian lai .de?
You did come? When did you come? What day did you come?
Scene 4 Jiel .hair.sh tzwol .a? Tzwol shanq.wuu .hair.sh shiah.wuu .a?
Today or yesterday? Yesterday morning or afternoon?
Scene 5 Nii tzeem lai .de? Sh tzoou-dawl lai .de, sh tzuoh-che lai .de, .hair.sh tzuoh-chwan lai .de?
How did you come? Did you walk, come by car, or come by boat?
Scene 5 .Oh, nii sh tzuoh feiji lai .de! Jii-dean jong .de feiji? Sherm .shyr.howl daw .de?
Oh, you came by plane! What time plane? When did it arrive?
Scene 6 Ar? Sherm.me? Nii shuo sherm.me? Woo ting.bu-doong.
Huh? What? What did you say? I can’t understand.
Scene 6 Chiing nii mann.i.deal shuo. Chiing nii shuo-mann.i.deal.
Please speak a little more slowly. Please say it more slowly.
Scene 7 Woo sheang woo jey-leang-tian mei konql … Nii.men leang-wey shanq woo.jell lai .ha, .hao .bu.hao?
I think I’ll be busy these next few days … You two gentlemen come to my place instead, all right?
Scene 7 Miengl jiann, .ah! .Tzay.jiann vx!
See you tomorrow! Goodbye!

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Phone talk begins with place and number

tzoongjyu
jii-haw
Nin naal
  • The lesson begins with a telephone number and exchange.
  • On the phone, the first question is often “what place are you?” rather than “who are you?”

2) Politeness has special forms

Nin
wey
gueyshinq
shian.sheng
  • Nin is a polite “you” in the Peiping area.
  • wey is the polite form used in asking identity.
  • gueyshinq asks one’s honorable surname.

3) Numbers matter in real time

1–100
AN after numerals
o’clock
  • The notes give a full number system from 1 to 100.
  • Lesson 4 makes numbers practical: phone numbers, dates, doors, and arrival times.

4) Days and time are part of planning logic

Liibay
tzwol
jiel
miengl
  • The lesson moves through yesterday, today, tomorrow, and days of the week.
  • Students learn how schedule questions unfold step by step.

5) Travel is expressed through manner of arrival

tzoou-dawl
tzuoh-che
tzuoh-chwan
tzuoh feiji
  • The lesson asks how someone came: walking, by car, by boat, by plane.
  • This is excellent for teaching verb-object travel expressions.

6) Potential complements express listening ability

ting.bu-doong
ting.bu-ching.chuu
kann.bu-jiann
  • The notes explain that -de- and -bu- inside verb-complement compounds express can / cannot.
  • This gives students a more structural way to say “I can’t understand.”

7) Question particles and intonation matter a lot

.ma
.a
low pitch
high pitch
  • High-pitched .ma leans toward agreement/disagreement.
  • Low-pitched .a can function as echo-confirmation: “Did you say…?”

8) Mandarin distinguishes two kinds of “or”

.hair.sh
.huoh.jee
  • .hair.sh is used in disjunctive choice questions: which one?
  • .huoh.jee is used in non-disjunctive or statement-like “or.”
  • This is a subtle but important conversational distinction.

High-value classroom move

Turn Lesson 4 into a telephone lab

Have students sit back-to-back or separated by a divider so they cannot see each other.
  1. One student reads a phone number. The other repeats and confirms it.
  2. The caller asks for identity politely.
  3. The receiver intentionally says one phrase unclearly.
  4. The caller must use repair language such as Woo ting.bu-doong or Chiing nii tzay shuo i.biann.
  5. End by arranging a visit for today or tomorrow.
Two “or”s, two different meanings

A key lesson 4 concept

Disjunctive “or”

Jiel .hair.sh tzwol .a?

This asks the listener to choose: today or yesterday?

Non-disjunctive “or”

Nii yaw chianbii .huoh.jee gangbii .bu.yaw?

This is more like “do you want a pencil or a pen, or not?” not a simple immediate forced choice in the same way.

Why this is important for beginners

English uses “or” for both of these situations, but the lesson explicitly teaches that Chinese separates them. That gives students a deeper sense that conversation meaning depends on the type of alternative being presented.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Number relay

Students take turns saying:

  • telephone numbers
  • dates
  • o’clock times

Task B — Weekday timeline

Students answer:

  • Tzwol Liibayliow, jiel Liibayjii .ne?
  • Miengl .ne?

Task C — Repair chain

One student mumbles a phrase. The other must respond with:

  • Sherm.me?
  • Woo ting.bu-doong.
  • Chiing nii shuo-mann.i.deal.
  • Chiing nii tzay shuo i.biann.

Task D — Arrange a meeting

Students negotiate whether to meet:

  • today or tomorrow
  • at my place or your place
  • right away or later
Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) In this lesson, why is Nin naal? especially important?

Correct. The notes explain that telephone subscribers are treated as places or organizations, so “what place are you?” is a natural opening question.
Not quite. In this lesson, Nin naal? is tied to telephone etiquette and identification of the calling place.

2) What is the key difference between .hair.sh and .huoh.jee in the notes?

Correct. The notes make this distinction explicit: they are two different kinds of “or,” not interchangeable forms.
Not quite. Lesson 4 explicitly teaches two different “or” logics, with .hair.sh for disjunctive choice questions and .huoh.jee for non-disjunctive uses.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Answer a phone opening politely.
  2. Confirm a number correctly.
  3. Ask when someone came and how they came.
  4. Use one repair phrase when you “mishear” something.
  5. Arrange whether to meet today or tomorrow and end the call politely.
