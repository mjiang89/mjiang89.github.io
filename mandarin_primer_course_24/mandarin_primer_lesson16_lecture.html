Mandarin Primer — Lesson 16

Chinese Geography

Lesson 16 continues the classroom-geography unit by moving from world geography to China. The teacher gives a regional overview of China, contrasting dense and sparse population zones, discussing the Northeast, Inner Mongolia, the Yellow River basin, the Yangtze basin, the far south, and the cultural and political importance of each region. The lesson also uses geography to teach identity, history, migration, and the limits of regional labels like “north” and “south.”

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so it stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow a lecture-style discussion of China’s major regions and population distribution.
  • Understand how Mandarin describes regional geography, provinces, river basins, cultural centers, and internal migration.
  • Recognize the contrast between the Yellow River basin as the cradle of ancient culture and the Yangtze basin as the center of modern China.
  • Learn region-related vocabulary such as dongnan-buh, liouyuh, day, dongbeei, and province names.
  • Understand the discussion of Manchus, Han people, assimilation, and why regional identity labels can be complicated.
  • Notice how the teacher manages disagreement and clarifies that both students can be partly right and partly wrong.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 16 teaches geography as cultural reasoning. Students are not only locating provinces. They are thinking about population, history, cultural centers, identity labels, and what makes a region “north” or “south.”

For high-school students, this lesson is especially rich because it turns a map into a conversation about who counts as northern or southern, what makes a region important, and how historical categories do not always match present-day usage.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
China overview hook
Start with the striking fact that China has the world’s largest population and almost one-fourth of the world’s people.
2
Population map
Contrast densely populated eastern/southern China with more sparsely populated Tibet, Qinghai, Xinjiang, and Inner Mongolia.
3
Northeast and identity
Teach the Northeast provinces, the term Manchuria, and the discussion of Manchus, Han, and assimilation.
4
Yellow River and ancient culture
Teach the northern provinces, the Yellow River basin, and its role in early Chinese culture and dynastic capitals.
5
Yangtze and the south
Teach the Yangtze basin, the richest provinces, the far south, and debates over what counts as “the South.”
6
Famous southerners + quiz
End with the discussion of Chairman Chiang, Shanghai dialect, Sun Yat-sen, and the principle that some figures represent all of China.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source’s “To the Student” pages emphasize utterances, repetition, echoing, and grammar-through-exercises. Lesson 16 becomes especially teachable when the map of China stays visible and each region is tied to one memorable idea.
  1. Use a color-coded map of China: Northeast, Yellow River basin, Yangtze basin, far south, and sparsely populated border regions.
  2. Give each region a “headline”: ancient culture, modern center, sparsely populated frontier, etc.
  3. Keep the identity discussion grounded: ethnicity, migration, and labels are discussed as classroom reasoning, not slogans.
  4. Use the page 5 notes to explain why the teacher’s language is formal and lecture-like.
  5. Turn the south/north debate into a controlled classroom discussion, since the lesson itself models respectful correction.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students may think the lesson is just a long list of provinces. It is not. Its real structure is contrast: dense versus sparse, ancient versus modern center, regional versus national identity.
  • been- is a formal “this,” as in beengwo “this country,” and differs in style from more ordinary forms.
  • tornghuah means assimilate, not merely identify.
  • sherm Chyiren Hannren functions like an indirect rhetorical question in the notes.
  • Charng Jiang is much more common than Yangtzyy Jiang for the Yangtze River.
  • “South” is relative: Jiangsu and Zhejiang can count as “south” relative to the northern provinces, yet a national leader is also beyond east-west-south-north labeling in the teacher’s logic.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 16 as a lecture on regions and identity

This lesson is easiest to teach as a sequence of regional contrasts and classroom clarifications.

Opening Hao, shianntzay kaishyy jeang beengwo dihlii. Jonggwo sh shyhjieh.shanq renkoou tzuey duo de gwojia, chahbuduo jann chyuan-shyhjieh renkoou de syh-fenn-jy-i.
Good, now I shall begin to lecture on the geography of this country. China has the largest population in the world, making up almost one-fourth of the world’s population.
Population map Dannsh Jonggwo de renmin, duoshuh juh tzay Jonggwo de dongnan-buh ... Shitzanq, Chinghae, Shinjiang Sheeng, gen Ney Mengguu ... renkoou biijeaude shishao ideal.
But the majority of the Chinese population lives in the southeastern part of China ... Tibet, Qinghai, Xinjiang, and Inner Mongolia are comparatively sparsely populated.
Northeast Tzuey beeibial de jii-sheeng jiowsh dongbeei jii-sheeng ... waygworen yeou shyrhowl goan ta jiaw Maan.jou.
The northernmost provinces are the provinces of the Northeast ... foreigners sometimes call them Manchuria.
Identity correction Sanbae-duo-nian chyan, Chyiren ruhle Guan yiihow ... yiijing gen Hannren tornghuah le ... faan.jenq doush Jonggworen jiowshle.
More than 300 years ago, when the Manchus entered the Pass, they dispersed into the interior and have gradually assimilated to the Han; anyway, they are all Chinese now.
Migration point Jyhyu shianntzay Dongbeei de jiumin a, chahbuduo chyuansh Hannren, tamen dahduoshuh doush tsorng Shan.dong banle chiuh de.
As for the present inhabitants of the Northeast, almost all are Han Chinese, and the great majority moved there from Shantung.
Inner Mongolia Rehher, Charhaeel, Sweiyeuan jey-san-sheeng her.chiilai jiaw Ney Mengguu.
Jehol, Chahar, and Suiyuan together are called Inner Mongolia.
Yellow River basin Hwangher liouyuh de beei jii-sheeng ... jiaw Beeibian, jehsh Jonggwo guu.shyr.howl wenhuah fadar tzuey tzao de dihfang.
The northern provinces of the Yellow River basin ... are called “the North”; this is where ancient Chinese culture developed earliest.
Dynastic capitals Shang.chaur a, Jou.chaur a, Chyn.chaur a, leang-Hann, Tarng, Sonq, Yuan, Ming, Ching ... de jingcherng chahbuduo doush tzay Hwangher liouyuh de.
The capitals of the great ancient dynasties were for the most part in the Yellow River basin.
Modern center Dannsh Charng Jiang liouyuh sh shiannday Jonggwo de jongshin ... ney-jii-sheeng yaw suannsh tzuey fuh de jii-sheeng le.
But the Yangtze basin is the center of modern China ... those provinces count as the richest provinces.
Far south Tzuey nanbial de jii-sheeng jiowsh Fwu.jiann, Tair.uan, Goang.dong, Goang.shi, Guey.jou, Yun.nan ... tzay jenqjyh.shanq, gwofarng.shanq, jingjih.shanq, wenhuah.shanq, yee doush jann feicharng jonqyaw de dihwey de.
The southernmost provinces are Fujian, Taiwan, Guangdong, Guangxi, Guizhou, and Yunnan, and they occupy very important political, strategic, economic, and cultural positions.
South debate Jiang.su Jeh.jiang gen Beeibian bii.chiilai sh tzay nanbial, suooyii dyichiueh sh yeou ren goan Jiang- Jeh- leang-sheeng jiaw 'Nanbian.'
Compared with the northern provinces, Jiangsu and Zhejiang are in the south, so indeed some people call those two provinces “the South.”
National identity point Inwey i-gwo jenqfuu de juushyi sh daybeau chyuan-gwo de, genbeen jiow wusuoowey dong shi nan beei ... Tan yahgel jiowsh Jonggworen sh bush'a? Duey le, jiow(sh) jeh huah lo.
Since the chairman of a country’s government represents the whole country, fundamentally there is no point in speaking of east, west, south, or north ... He is just Chinese, right? Yes, that is exactly the point.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) China is introduced through population scale

renkoou
syh-fenn-jy-i
  • The lesson begins with a large-scale demographic claim: China has the world’s largest population and nearly one-fourth of the world’s people.
  • This gives students a big-picture anchor before the regional details begin.

2) Regional geography is tied to density

dongnan-buh
shishao
  • The central contrast is between the populous east/south and the more sparsely populated southwest, west, northwest, and north border regions.
  • This is an easy map-based concept for beginners to visualize.

3) The Northeast becomes a lesson in migration and ethnicity

Maan.jou
Chyiren
Hannren
tornghuah
  • The teacher rejects a simplistic identification of Northeast people with “Manchus.”
  • Instead, the lesson explains assimilation and later Han migration from Shantung.

4) The Yellow River basin is framed as the cradle of ancient China

Hwangher liouyuh
guuday
wenhuah
  • The teacher explicitly links the North and the Yellow River basin to the earliest flourishing of Chinese culture.
  • Confucius and the dynastic capitals reinforce that claim.

5) The Yangtze basin is framed as the center of modern China

Charng Jiang
jongshin
tzuey fuh
  • The lesson contrasts ancient and modern centers rather than saying there is only one core region.
  • This is a powerful structural contrast for teaching.

6) The far south is politically and culturally important

jonqyaw de dihwey
jenqjyh
gwofarng
jingjih
  • The southernmost provinces are not treated as marginal; they are said to hold important political, strategic, economic, and cultural positions.
  • This broadens how students think about regional importance.

7) “South” is shown to be relative and contextual

Nanbian
Shanqhae-huah
  • The teacher shows that Jiangsu and Zhejiang can count as south relative to the North, and that Shanghai speakers use “southern dialect” from their own perspective.
  • This is a lesson in linguistic relativity inside geography.

8) National representatives exceed regional labels

daybeau chyuan-gwo
wusuoowey dong shi nan beei
  • The teacher argues that a national chairman represents the whole country, so east-west-south-north labels become beside the point.
  • This culminates the lesson’s tension between local and national identity.

High-value classroom move

Use a “headline map” activity

Ask students to assign one short headline to each major region in the lesson.
  1. Northeast = migration and Manchuria.
  2. Yellow River basin = earliest ancient culture.
  3. Yangtze basin = center of modern China.
  4. Far south = politically and culturally important.

This helps students remember the lesson as a structured argument, not as a list of provinces.

A key structural system

Ancient center versus modern center

Ancient center

Hwangher liouyuh

Earliest cultural development and many dynastic capitals.

Modern center

Charng Jiang liouyuh

Modern China’s center and many of the richest provinces.

Why it matters

The lesson teaches that cultural centrality can shift over time.

Teaching payoff

Students can compare how historical and present-day importance are not always located in the same place.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Region summary

Students summarize one region of China from the lesson in three short sentences: where it is, what it is known for, and why it matters.

Task B — Identity explanation

Students explain in English why the teacher says Northeast residents are not simply “the so-called Manchus.”

Task C — North/South debate

Students explain how Jiangsu and Zhejiang can be both “southern” and yet not the whole meaning of “south.”

Task D — Famous people map

Students place Confucius, Chairman Chiang, and Sun Yat-sen into the geography discussion and explain what each example is used to show.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to Lesson 16, which region is described as the center of modern China?

Correct. The lesson explicitly contrasts the Yellow River basin as the center of ancient China with the Yangtze basin as the center of modern China.
Not quite. One of the lesson’s clearest contrasts is that the Yangtze basin is presented as the center of modern China.

2) What is the best summary of the teacher’s final point about calling a national leader “southern” or “northern”?

Correct. The lesson’s closing reasoning is that a national representative stands for the whole country, so east-west-south-north labels are no longer the main issue.
Not quite. The teacher is not denying that regions exist; he is saying that, at the national level of representation, those labels become secondary.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. State one fact about China’s population distribution from the lesson.
  2. Name one province group associated with the Yellow River basin.
  3. Explain, in English, why the teacher says the Northeast is not simply “the so-called Manchus.”
  4. Describe the contrast between the Yellow River basin and the Yangtze basin.
  5. Give one example from the lesson showing that “south” is a relative term.
