Mandarin Primer — Lesson 24

An American Makes a Speech

Lesson 24 turns a delayed train arrival into a public speaking event. Two students rush into an assembly just as the national anthem begins, discover that an American exchange student is about to speak in Chinese, and then listen to a speech that mixes humility, humor, language learning, cultural desire, gratitude, and gender commentary. The lesson is excellent for teaching assembly language, speech framing, audience response, self-deprecating humor, and how public Chinese differs from casual conversation.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so the page stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow the lesson’s structure: arriving late, entering the hall quietly, hearing the anthem, listening to the president’s introduction, listening to the American student’s speech, and reacting afterward.
  • Understand vocabulary for meetings, auditoriums, dormitories, whispering, welcoming, exchange students, speeches, scholarships, rapid language courses, universities, faculty guidance, applause, and adjournment.
  • Recognize how the speaker uses humility formulas and jokes to make his Chinese speech more effective.
  • See how the lesson presents Chinese as difficult, desirable, culturally rich, and socially rewarding.
  • Notice the public style of formal thanks and respectful address in the speech.
  • Understand the role of the national anthem on page 5, including its literary diction and ceremonial effect.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 24 teaches Chinese in a public, performative setting. Students do not just order tea or argue with friends here. They enter an auditorium, hear formal introductions, interpret audience laughter, and analyze how a speaker wins a room.

For high-school students, this lesson is memorable because the American speaker is both admirable and funny. He uses a proverb about a foreigner speaking Chinese, describes being intimidated by the language, then earns applause by speaking gracefully anyway.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Late-arrival hook
Begin with the rushing dialogue: the meeting has already started, the bell is ringing, and they should have gone straight to the auditorium.
2
Ceremony frame
Teach the anthem, the formal hall setting, whispering students, and the president’s introduction.
3
The speech opening
Focus on the proverb joke and the speaker’s humble claim that he cannot really make a speech.
4
Motivation and study path
Teach childhood interest in China, books, restaurants, fear of difficulty, the Harvard rapid course, and the scholarship.
5
Audience laughter and social meaning
Teach the coeducation joke, applause, polished Mandarin, and peer evaluation.
6
After-meeting retell + quiz
Use the exercise dialogue to retell the meeting and practice reported speech.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The “To the Student” guidance still applies: work from clear understanding toward fluent repetition. Lesson 24 benefits especially from performance because much of its meaning comes from tone, applause, whispering, and laughter.
  1. Open class by staging the late-arrival conversation at the door, with one student hurrying another into the “auditorium.”
  2. Show the anthem visually as a ceremony object rather than treating it as just another text; page 5 includes the musical score, which makes the event feel formal and public.
  3. Read the American speaker’s lines aloud with deliberate pauses, false starts, and self-correction, so students hear how humility and humor work.
  4. Track audience responses on the board: whispering, laughter, great laughter, applause.
  5. Use the exercise retell on page 6–7 to shift students from direct speech to narrative retelling.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students may think the lesson is mainly about the foreign speaker’s Chinese ability, but it is equally about public ritual, audience interpretation, and formal gratitude language.
  • yeanshuo means “to make a speech,” and the notes distinguish it from jeangyean or yeanjeang “lecture.”
  • The proverb formula matters: page 5 notes explain that with substitutions it is a common native reaction when hearing a foreigner speak one’s language or dialect.
  • ... de huah in the apology formula means “if it is a matter of / if,” not simply a noun phrase ending.
  • Speaker A’s accent joke matters: page 6 notes that he is presumably from Szechwan and pronounces gwoyeu like gueeyuh.
  • The anthem’s wording is intentionally literary: page 5 notes say the national anthem text is in an archaic literary style, even more literary than current wenli.
Lesson walkthrough

Lesson 24 as arrival, ceremony, speech, and reaction

This lesson becomes much easier when treated as four connected scenes.

Scene 1 Huay le, huay le! Tzarmen daw-chyrle. Nii ting, yiijing daa-jong kai-huey le.
Oh no, we are late. Listen, they are already ringing the bell for the meeting.
Scene 1 Tzarmen huooche jihran wuhle dean, jiow inggai ijyr daw dah-liitarng ... nii iseel yaw shian daw suhsheh.
Since our train was late, we should have come straight to the auditorium ... you insisted on going to the dormitory first.
Scene 1 Tamen yiijing kaishyy chanq gwoge'l le! Tzarmen chingchienglde jinnchiuh.
They have already started singing the national anthem. Let us go in quietly.
Scene 2 Neusheng I: Jeangtair.shanq yeou g yangrel tzuoh de nall. ... Tzay nall, tzay shiawjaang weytz de yowbial.
Woman Student I: There is a foreigner sitting on the platform. ... There, to the right of the president’s seat.
Scene 2 Jintian woomen huan'yng i-wey shinjinn daw Jonggwo lai de Meeigwo torngshyue ... Ta sh ... Haafor Dahshyue gen been-shiaw de jiauhuann shyuesheng.
Today we welcome an American student who has recently arrived in China ... He is an exchange student from Harvard University to our university.
Scene 3 Swuyeu shuo de hao, “Tian bu pah, dih bu pah, jiow pah yanggoeitz shuo Jonggwo-huah.” ... Shuo de buhao de huah, chiing dahjia yuanlianq.
As the proverb says, “Heaven is not to be feared, Earth is not to be feared, only a foreign devil speaking Chinese is fearful.” ... If I speak badly, please forgive me.
Scene 3 Woo shiahle jyueshin, tzay Haafor Dahshyue sheuan le i-men Jonggwo Yeuyan Suhcherng Ke; yow derle i-bii jeangshyue-jin, shanq jell lai tzuoh jiauhuann-shyuesheng.
I made up my mind, chose a rapid Chinese language course at Harvard University, and received a scholarship to come here as an exchange student.
Scene 3 Shianntzay jiuran dar-dawle daw Jonggwo chyou-shyue de muhdih, woo jyuej jeanjyr kuay.hwo de shuo.bu-chulai le.
Now that I have really achieved my aim of coming to China to study, I simply cannot express how happy I am.
Scene 4 Haafor sh jyy shou nansheng d'e! ... kannjiann gehgehl dahshyue doush nan-neu torng-shiaw, jeyg shyy woo jyuej youchyi gaushinq!
Harvard admits only male students! ... seeing that every university here is coeducational makes me especially happy.
Scene 4 Cherng geh-wey shyjaang, geh-wey torngshyue, chuhchuh de bang-mang jyydean, bujydaw tzeemyanq gaanshieh tsair hao. Yiihow hair shiwanq dahjia shyrshyr jyydao!
Having received help and guidance everywhere from all the teachers and students, I do not know how best to thank you. I hope you will continue to guide me from time to time.
Closing Jeyg waygworen de Jonggwo-huah shuo de jen butsuoh ... ta jey-i-koou de piawlianq gwoyeu lian woo dou shuo.bu-guoh ta. ... Dai.hoel sann le huey tzarm chiuh jiann ta chiuh.
This foreigner’s Chinese is really not bad ... his polished Mandarin is so good that even I cannot surpass him. ... After the meeting we will go meet him.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Public events require public behavior

dah-liitarng
gwoge
chingchienglde
  • The opening teaches how to move into a formal event late: quietly, with awareness of ceremony.
  • This makes the lesson socially grounded before the speech even begins.

2) Whispering sets audience mood

eelyeu
jeangtair
yangrel
  • The women students’ whispers show excitement, curiosity, and the presence of a foreign guest as a spectacle.
  • That prepares students to think about audience response, not only speaker content.

3) Formal introduction creates legitimacy

huan'yng
jiauhuann shyuesheng
been-shiaw
  • The president’s introduction frames the American speaker as officially welcomed and academically serious.
  • This matters because the audience hears him first as an institutional guest, then as a person.

4) Self-deprecating humor lowers audience resistance

yanggoeitz shuo Jonggwo-huah
yuanlianq
  • The proverb joke lets the speaker acknowledge his foreignness and the risk of speaking Chinese.
  • This wins laughter before any audience judgment becomes harsh.

5) Motivation story creates sincerity

charngcharng mae shu-baw
Jonggwo fanngoal
shiah de tzoong bugaan shyh
  • The speaker explains his childhood desire to come to China and his fear of trying the language.
  • That makes his later achievement feel earned rather than decorative.

6) A scholarship turns desire into reality

Suhcherng Ke
jeangshyue-jin
chyou-shyue
  • The speech links resolve, formal study, and financial opportunity.
  • This gives students a clear cause-and-effect academic path.

7) Humor can shift to social critique

jyy shou nansheng
nan-neu torng-shiaw
hong-tarng dah-shiaw
  • The coeducation joke is funny, but it also reveals institutional difference between Harvard and Chinese universities in the lesson’s moment.
  • This is why the room erupts in big laughter.

8) Formal gratitude closes the speech

bang-mang jyydean
gaanshieh
jyydao
  • The ending models a respectful public-thank-you style.
  • The notes also distinguish jyydean for practical guidance from jyydao for more general guidance.

High-value classroom move

Stage the speech twice

Have one student give the speech flatly, then another give it with humor, pauses, and audience awareness.
  1. Compare how the proverb lands in the two versions.
  2. Notice how pauses around uh and self-correction create vulnerability.
  3. Discuss why the coeducation joke works better once the room already trusts the speaker.

This shows that public speaking is not only about words but about delivery and relationship with the audience.

A key structural system

Fear → effort → arrival → gratitude

Fear

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Retell the event

Using the page 6–7 exercise, students retell the whole meeting to a friend who missed it.

Task B — Why did the audience laugh?

Students identify each laugh point in the speech and explain what makes it funny or socially effective.

Task C — What makes the speech persuasive?

Students sort lines into humility, credibility, cultural interest, gratitude, and audience-pleasing humor.

Task D — Compare two kinds of Chinese

Students compare the informal student dialogue with the more formal public speech register.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) What is the best explanation of why the foreign student opens with the proverb about “a foreign devil speaking Chinese”?

Correct. The proverb lets him laugh at himself first, which lowers tension and makes the audience more receptive.
Not quite. The line works because it is humble and funny, not hostile.

2) Why does the room break into especially big laughter at the line about Harvard and coeducation?

Correct. The joke lands because it is both surprising and flattering to the audience’s own university environment.
Not quite. The humor comes from the contrast between institutions and the way the speaker frames it for the room.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Explain why the two students arrive late and what they do when they enter the hall.
  2. Name two reasons the American wanted to study China before he ever came.
  3. Describe, in English, how he finally got to China.
  4. Explain why the audience laughs more than once during the speech.
  5. Retell the ending of the speech in one or two sentences of formal gratitude.
