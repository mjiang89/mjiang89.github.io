Mandarin Primer — Lesson 11

Watching the Year Out

Lesson 11 is a rich seasonal and calendar lesson built around New Year’s Eve. Two speakers move from immediate physical experience — cold room, snow, stove, hat, coat — into dates, weekdays, years, holidays, the Republic calendar, the lunar and solar calendars, solstices, equinoxes, and finally the exact countdown to midnight. It is one of the best early lessons for turning language into a structured time map.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so it stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Talk about cold, warmth, snow, clothing color change, and immediate surroundings in a winter scene.
  • Use relative time expressions like tzwo.tian, chyan.tian, dah-chyan.tian, ming.tian, how.tian, and dah-how.tian.
  • Read and reason about dates, weekdays, months, and years, including Republic-year and Gregorian-year references.
  • Understand the difference between lunar and solar calendars as presented in the lesson.
  • Recognize key seasonal terms: winter solstice, summer solstice, vernal equinox, autumnal equinox, and the four seasons.
  • Follow the countdown to midnight and understand how time is expressed by minutes, quarter-hours, and seconds.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 11 teaches Mandarin through time systems. Students learn not just words for dates and weather, but how different time scales connect: today, this week, this month, this year, this era.

For high-school beginners, this lesson is especially valuable because the topic is concrete and abstract at once. They can feel the cold room and see the snow, but they also begin to map historical time, public holidays, calendars, and astronomical markers.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Winter scene hook
Begin with the room, the stove, the cold, the snow, and the coat turning white. Let students enter the scene first.
2
Timeline of near days
Map yesterday, day before yesterday, tomorrow, day after tomorrow, and the first few days of January.
3
Calendar reasoning
Work through weekdays, dates, next Saturday, last Wednesday, and the chronological table.
4
Big time systems
Teach Republic years, Gregorian years, and the contrast between lunar and solar calendars.
5
Seasonal astronomy
Explain solstices, equinoxes, longest and shortest day/night, and how they organize the four seasons.
6
Midnight countdown + quiz
Finish with the final minute, the count in seconds, and a class countdown to the new year.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source’s “To the Student” pages emphasize utterances, repetition, echoing, and grammar-through-exercises. Lesson 11 becomes especially teachable when students can see time laid out visually while hearing the dialogue aloud.
  1. Use a large classroom timeline for today, yesterday, tomorrow, next week, next month, and next year.
  2. Put an actual calendar on screen or board and let students calculate along with the dialogue.
  3. Separate “small time” and “big time”: first dates and weekdays, then years and calendars, then solstices and equinoxes.
  4. Keep the winter room imagery visible so the lesson does not become pure abstraction.
  5. Use the chronological table from the source as a teaching graphic and have students read across it aloud.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students often mix up the different time scales in this lesson. Help them distinguish what is about weather, what is about dates, what is about calendar systems, and what is about astronomy.
  • shiahg with months behaves differently because yueh is treated as a noun requiring an AN, unlike nian.
  • Days of the week can be treated as measure words or nouns, so forms like shiah Shingchi-liow and shiahg Shingchi-liow both occur.
  • Next Saturday in the lesson does not simply mean the very next Saturday chronologically; it often means the Saturday of next week.
  • Lunar vs. solar calendar is a conceptual distinction, not only a vocabulary distinction.
  • Numbers in Chinese group by four digits, not by three, which matters in the notes and chronological table.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 11 as a movement through time

This lesson works best when the dialogue is grouped into thematic scenes rather than read as one long block.

Scene 1 Utzlii jen leeng! Woo yueh tzuoh yueh leeng. Leeng de jen nanshow!
It is really cold in the room! The more I sit here, the colder I get. It is miserably cold.
Scene 1 Jeh lutz ideal rehchiell dou meiyeou me! Goanbao sh meiyeou mei le ha?
This stove has not a bit of warmth in it. I bet it is running out of coal.
Scene 2 Hei-mawtz biannle bair-mawtz, hei-dahchaang biannle bair-dahchaang le.
The black hat has turned into a white hat, and the black overcoat into a white overcoat.
Scene 2 Jin.nian hair mei shiah.guoh tzemm dah de sheue ne ha?
There has not yet been such a heavy snow this year, has there?
Scene 3 Jiel Shyr'ell.yueh sanshyri-haw. Tzwo.tian sanshyr, chyan.tian ellshyr-jeou, dah-chyan.tian ellshyr-ba.
Today is December 31st. Yesterday was the 30th, the day before yesterday the 29th, and the day before that the 28th.
Scene 3 Ming.tian sh shiahg yueh de i-haw ... how.tian sh Jeng.yueh chu'ell, dah-how.tian sh Jeng.yueh chu-san.
Tomorrow is the first day of the next month ... the day after tomorrow is January 2nd, and the day after that is January 3rd.
Scene 4 Mingwo sanshyr-chi nian ... jiowsh Gonglih ichian jeoubae syhshyr-ba nian.
This is the 37th year of the Republic ... which is the same as 1948 in the public/Gregorian calendar.
Scene 4 Chiuh.nian sh i-jeou-syh-chi, chyan.nian i-jeou-syh-liow ... jiee ming.tian chii-tourl jiowsh i-jeou-syh-jeou-nian le.
Last year was 1947, the year before last 1946 ... from tomorrow on it will be 1949.
Scene 5 Jintian sh Shingchi-wuu ... shiah Shingchi-liow sh ba-haw.
Today is Friday ... next week’s Saturday will be the 8th.
Scene 5 Jeyg Shingchi-san sh ellshyr-jeou, shanq Shingchi-san sh ellshyr'ell — gaan.chyng jiowsh Dongjyh ou!
This Wednesday was the 29th, last Wednesday was the 22nd — why, that was the winter solstice!
Scene 6 Dongjyh ... sh i-nian liitou bair.tian tzuey doan yeh.lii tzuey charng de ryhtz.
The winter solstice is the day in the year with the shortest day and the longest night.
Scene 6 Dongjyh de dueymiall sh Shiahjyh ... leang-jyh de dangjiall ne, hair yeou Chuenfen Chioufen.
The counterpart of winter solstice is summer solstice ... and between the two there are also the vernal and autumnal equinoxes.
Scene 7 Tzyhtsorng Shinhay Germinq yiihow, jiow bae inlih geei feychwu le, jiow na yanglih danqtzuoh gwolih yonq le.
Since the Revolution of 1911, the lunar calendar has been abolished and the solar calendar has been used as the national calendar.
Scene 8 Shianntzay yiijing sh shyri-dean san-keh le ... hair yeou i-keh jong jiowsh bannyeh shyr'ell-dean l'a.
It is already quarter to twelve ... another quarter hour and it will be midnight.
Scene 8 Wuushyr meau, wuushyri, wuushyr'ell ... wuushyrjeou — Shyr'ell-dean! Shianntzay sh i-jeou-syh-jeou l'a!
Fifty seconds, 51, 52 ... 59 — Twelve o’clock! Now it is 1949!

1) The lesson starts from bodily winter experience

noan.hwo
bair-mawtz

  • This is important because the lesson later becomes much more abstract.

2) Relative day expressions form a local timeline

tzwo.tian
chyan.tian
dah-chyan.tian
ming.tian
  • The lesson is excellent for teaching how one reference day anchors a whole set of nearby time words.
  • This becomes much clearer when laid out visually.

3) Days, dates, and weekdays are a reasoning system

Shingchi
liibay
ba-haw
  • The dialogue asks students to calculate across weekdays and dates, not just recite them.
  • The notes emphasize that “next Saturday” can mean the Saturday of next week, not just the next Saturday that arrives.

4) Two calendar systems are contrasted

yanglih

  • The lesson explains why old-style New Year falls later and why it feels like spring afterward.
  • Then it explains the post-1911 use of the solar calendar as the national calendar.

Gonglih

  • This lesson gives a direct bridge between political/historical year naming and Western chronology.
  • That makes it unusually rich for cultural context.

6) Solstices and equinoxes are taught as seasonal anchors

Dongjyh
Shiahjyh
Chuenfen
Chioufen
  • The lesson does not just name the four seasons. It explains how astronomical points structure them.
  • This makes the lesson conceptually deeper than a simple weather unit.

  • The notes explicitly explain that large numbers are grouped by four digits rather than three.

High-value classroom move

Use the source’s chronological table as a live map

The chronological table on the last page is one of the lesson’s best teaching tools.
  1. Have students read across days of the week and dates aloud.
  2. Then read down from current year to next year and year after next.
  3. Then use it to answer questions like “What day was last Wednesday?” and “What date is next Saturday?”

This turns a difficult calendar lesson into a visual reasoning task instead of pure memorization.

Teaching payoff

Lesson 11 is strong because it moves smoothly from one time scale to another without losing the conversational setting.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Build the day line

Students place cards for tzwo.tian, chyan.tian, ming.tian, and so on in the correct order.

Task B — Calendar detective

Students answer date/weekday questions using the lesson’s table and expressions.

Task C — Explain the year systems

Students explain in English how Republic years and Gregorian years are related in the lesson.

Task D — Solstice versus equinox

Students sort the four seasonal terms and say which ones involve longest/shortest day or equal day and night.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to the lesson and notes, why does the speaker say that after old-style New Year it is already spring?

Correct. The lesson explains that New Year in the old lunar calendar arrives noticeably later than in the solar calendar, so spring is near or already felt after those holidays.
Not quite. The key point is the time difference between lunar and solar New Year, not a simple seasonal mistake.

2) What is the best description of the winter solstice in Lesson 11?

Correct. The dialogue explicitly defines winter solstice as the day with the shortest day and longest night in the year.
Not quite. Lesson 11 gives a very specific astronomical definition of the winter solstice.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Say what the room feels like at the start of the lesson.
  2. Tell what date “today” is in the lesson.
  3. Give one sentence about tomorrow or the day after tomorrow.
  4. Explain, in English, the difference between the lunar and solar calendars as the lesson presents it.
  5. State what happens at midnight.
