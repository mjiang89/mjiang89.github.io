Mandarin Primer — Lesson 5

Up, Down, Left, Right, Front, Back, and Middle

Lesson 5 is the first major lesson on space. It teaches how Mandarin talks about where things are, what lies above or below, how writing appears on surfaces, how objects stand in front of or behind one another, how “inside” and “outside” work, and even how writing direction differs between Chinese and foreign scripts. This makes it one of the richest early lessons for building a mental map of Mandarin structure.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so it stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Use key localizers: shanq, dii.shiah, chyan.tou, how.tou, jongjiall, lii.tou, way.tou.
  • Understand the structure of location expressions like juol.shanq “on the desk” and utz.lii “in the room.”
  • Distinguish between yeou “there is” and sh “it is” in spatial descriptions.
  • Describe where things are relative to one another: desk, chair, wall, paper, floor, blackboard.
  • Talk about writing direction, inside vs. outside, and visible vs. invisible writing.
  • Notice how adjectives behave like verbs in Mandarin and how hei .de differs from a simple English adjective phrase.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 5 turns the classroom itself into the textbook. The desk, chair, wall, paper, blackboard, room, and people all become grammar in action.

For high-school beginners, this is excellent because spatial meaning is concrete and visible. At the same time, the lesson introduces important structural ideas: postpositions/localizers, the “there is A on B” pattern, result complements, adjective predicates, and the way context changes interpretation.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Room as language lab
Use the actual classroom: desk, chair, wall, door, floor, paper, board. Students should point before they explain.
2
Localizers first
Introduce space words visually: on, under, in front of, behind, inside, outside, between.
3
Dialogue in movement
Read the dialogue while moving objects or moving yourself: walk behind the chair, then in front of the desk.
4
Writing and surfaces
Use paper and board to show “there is writing on…” and the difference between black outside vs. white inside.
5
Grammar from contrasts
Highlight yeou vs. sh, adjective predicates, tzay usage, and serial verb phrases like “use right hand write.”
6
Knowledge graph + quiz
Finish with a structured review of spatial relations, visibility, and writing direction.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source’s teaching philosophy still applies here: whole utterances, repetition, echoing, and doing grammar through exercises. Lesson 5 becomes much stronger when students physically see the relations.
  1. Do not start with a definition list. Start with one object above another, then ask what students notice.
  2. Use the same few objects repeatedly so cognitive load stays low.
  3. Let students act out front/back/between with their own bodies.
  4. Write on actual paper and on a board so “there is writing on…” becomes visible.
  5. When confusion appears, translate the structure, not just the word. For example, explain “desk-top” rather than just “on.”
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

English often uses simple prepositions like “on,” “in,” and “under.” Lesson 5 shows that Mandarin often builds these relations through noun-plus-localizer structures.
  • Localizers are not just prepositions: juol.shanq is literally “desk-top.”
  • tzay can be translated or left untranslated depending on whether it is the main predicate or comes before another verb.
  • sh vs. yeou: one tells what something is; the other tells whether there is anything there.
  • Adjectives behave like verbs: Jey-been shu hei does not need a separate “is.”
  • hei .de shu is structurally more like “books that are black” than a simple English adjective slot.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 5 as a classroom world

The lesson works especially well when each line is tied to a visible action or spatial arrangement.

A Juol.shanq jiow.sh juotz .de shanq.tou. Yiitz.shanq jiow.sh yiitz .de shanq.tou.
“On the desk” means on the top of the desk. “On the chair” means on the top of the chair.
A Juol.shanq yeou shu … suoo.yii shu tzay juol.shanq.
There are books on the desk, therefore the books are on the desk.
A Chyang.shanq yeou men; suoo.yii men tzay chyang.shanq.
There is a door in/on the wall; therefore the door is in the wall.
B Men tzay chyang .de shanq.tou .ma?
Is the door above the wall?
A Bu.sh … 'men tzay chyang.shanq' jiow.sh 'tzay chyang.nall' .de .yih.sy.
No … “the door is on the wall” means it is where the wall is, not physically above it.
A Jeyg shu .de dii.shiah yeou jii-jang jyy, jyy dii.shiah sh juotz, juotz dii.shiah sh dih.
Under this book are papers; under the papers is the desk; under the desk is the floor.
A Baw.shanq yeou tzyh. Nii kann, baw.shanq yeou tzyh.
There is writing on the newspaper. Look, there is writing on the newspaper.
A Shianntzay woo tzay jyy.shanq shiee-tzyh … Shianntzay jyy.shanq yeou tzyh .le.
Now I write on the paper … now there is writing on the paper.
B Nii na yowshoou .hair.sh na tzuoo.shoou shiee-tzyh .a?
Do you write with your right hand or left hand?
A Woo sh yonq yow.shoou shiee-tzyh .de, woo buhuey yonq tzuoo.shoou shiee-tzyh.
I write with my right hand; I cannot write with my left hand.
A Way.gwo-tzyh sh tsorng shanq.tou tzuoo.bial wanq yow shiee .de.
Foreign writing is written from the upper left side toward the right.
A Juotz tzay woo.de chyan.tou … Yiitz tzay woo.de how.tou … Woo jiow tzay jey-leang-jiann dong.shi .de jongjiall.
The desk is in front of me … the chair is behind me … I am between these two things.
A Jey-been shu way.tou hei, kee.sh lii.tou bu hei.
This book is black outside, but not black inside.
B Tzar.men ren shianntzay tzay way.tou hair.sh tzay lii.tou .a?
Are we outside or inside now?
A Tzar.men tzay lii.tou .a.
We are inside.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Localizers are structural, not just vocabulary

juol.shanq
utz.lii
yiitz-how.tou
  • The notes call these localizers or postpositions.
  • They are built as noun-plus-localizer compounds like “desk-top” and “room-inside.”
  • This helps students see that Mandarin packages space through noun structure, not only separate prepositions.

2) There-is-A-on-B has a regular pattern

B.shanq yeou A
yeou
  • The notes explicitly state the general form: “There is A on/at/in B” → B.shanq yeou A.
  • This is a high-value pattern students can reuse across many concrete scenes.

3) tzay changes how the sentence translates

main predicate
before another verb
  • Shu tzay juol.shanq = The book is on the desk.
  • Ta tzay jyy.shanq shiee jiig tzyh = He writes a few words on the paper.
  • The difference matters for accurate beginner understanding.

4) sh and yeou do different jobs

identity
existence
  • Jyy dii.shiah sh juotz says what the thing under the paper is.
  • Jyy dii.shiah yeou juotz says that there is something there, namely a desk.

5) Writing can make an invisible relation visible

tzyh
paper
blackboard
  • The lesson uses writing on paper and on the board to show changing states.
  • This makes .le and visible-result meaning easy to demonstrate in class.

6) Mandarin can omit obvious possessives

right hand
left hand
  • The notes explain that English says “my right hand,” but Mandarin often does not need the possessive because it is obvious whose hand it is.
  • This is a good example of how English can be more explicit than Mandarin.

7) neng and huey are not identical

neng
huey
  • The lesson contrasts neng “able, can” with huey “know how to, can.”
  • This distinction is subtle but useful when students talk about what they are able to do physically versus what they know how to do.

8) Adjectives behave like verbs

hei
bai
hei .de
  • Chinese adjectives can be full predicates: Jey-been shu hei.
  • With .de, forms like hei .de shu are more like “books that are black.”
  • The notes explicitly say the rule is: either use both sh and .de, or omit both.

High-value classroom move

Teach the room by moving in it

This lesson becomes much clearer if the teacher physically changes position relative to a desk and chair.
  1. Stand behind the chair and say what is now in front of you.
  2. Walk to the front of the desk and ask what is between you and the chair.
  3. Place paper under a book and ask what is under what.
  4. Write on paper and ask students whether there is writing there now.

Students remember spatial grammar better when their own bodies are part of the scene.

A key structural contrast

Potential complement or descriptive predicate?

The notes make an important point: shiee.de-hao and shiee .de hao sound the same in actual speech, but they do not always mean the same thing.

Potential complement reading

Ta shiee.de-hao .ma?

Can he write well?

Descriptive predicate reading

Ta shiee .de hao .ma?

Does he write well?

This is a rich teaching moment: meaning sometimes depends not on sound alone, but on grammar and context.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Build the scene

Students place objects and describe them:

  • Juol.shanq yeou shu.
  • Shu tzay juol.shanq.

Task B — Move yourself

Students stand and change positions:

  • in front of desk
  • behind chair
  • between desk and chair

Task C — Write and reveal

Teacher writes on paper or board. Students answer:

  • Jyy.shanq yeou tzyh .le .ma?
  • Kann.jiann .le.

Task D — Black or white?

Students describe book outside vs. inside, and explain why they differ.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) What is the best explanation of juol.shanq in this lesson?

Correct. The notes explicitly explain these forms as localizer compounds such as “desk-top,” which English usually translates with a preposition.
Not quite. Lesson 5 treats juol.shanq as a localizer structure: noun plus localizer, not just a single unanalyzed word.

2) Why is the contrast between sh and yeou important in this lesson?

Correct. The notes explain that sh tells what the thing is, while yeou tells whether there is anything there.
Not quite. The lesson contrasts two different jobs: identification with sh and existence with yeou.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Describe where a book is relative to a desk and a chair.
  2. Say what is under something and what is in front of something.
  3. Use one sentence with yeou and one with sh.
  4. Explain whether we are inside or outside.
  5. Say, in English, why the outside and inside of the black book are not the same.
