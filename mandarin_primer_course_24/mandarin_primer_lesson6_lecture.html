Mandarin Primer — Lesson 6

A Smoke Ring

Lesson 6 is the first strongly narrative and imaginative lesson in the sequence. Instead of staying in the concrete classroom or a daily social scene, it moves into fatigue, rest, a cigarette, a smoke ring, a dream-like transformation, flying over the sea, falling lower and lower, mistaken perception, and waking up. This gives students early access to storytelling Mandarin: sequence, aspect, direction, result, surprise, and unreal-looking experience.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so it stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow and retell a longer narrative sequence with clear stages: finishing work, resting, seeing a smoke ring, entering a dream, flying, falling, and waking.
  • Understand directional complements like chii.lai, shiah.chiuh, daw ... chiuh, and how they shape movement meaning.
  • Recognize narrative aspect and completion patterns such as tzuoh-wan.le, chy.bu-wan, and past action with quantified objects.
  • Use comparison and degree structures like yueh ... yueh ....
  • Understand how Mandarin expresses seeming, mistaken perception, and surprise with forms like hao.shianq, yiiwei, jyuej, and sheir jy.daw.
  • See how a story can teach grammar through vivid movement and consequence.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 6 teaches Mandarin as narrative motion. The learner is no longer just identifying things or describing locations, but tracking events unfolding over time.

For high-school students, this lesson is especially engaging because it has fantasy, risk, surprise, and a twist ending. It also helps them understand that grammar is not separate from story: completion, direction, result, and mistaken interpretation are exactly what make the story work.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Story hook
Start with the image of a smoke ring and ask what strange things might happen in a dream.
2
Narrative map
Break the lesson into scenes: fatigue → rest → smoke ring → dream entry → flight → fall → tree → waking.
3
Movement grammar
Teach the directional complements with arrows and motion diagrams before returning to the story.
4
Perception and mistake
Focus on seeming, feeling, thinking wrongly, and discovering the truth.
5
Result and consequence
Highlight completion, inability, falling, crashing, and opening the eyes again.
6
Retell + quiz
End with students retelling the story in stages and checking two key grammar concepts.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source pages still emphasize whole utterances, echoing, repetition, and learning grammar through exercises. Lesson 6 benefits from an especially strong storyboarding approach.
  1. Teach the lesson as a sequence of pictures or frames, not as one long paragraph.
  2. Use arrows, rising/falling lines, and scene cards to track direction and aspect.
  3. Keep students anchored in English meaning before asking them to repeat Mandarin chunks.
  4. Pause at every “change of reality” moment: smoke ring, entering, flying, falling, waking.
  5. Have students separate “what seemed true” from “what was actually true” in the story.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Because the story is imaginative, students may overlook the fact that it is also packed with precise grammar patterns. Slow down enough that the story remains enjoyable but the structure stays visible.
  • Directional complements are not optional decoration. They are the engine of the story’s motion.
  • hao.shianq and .shyh.de signal seeming, not certainty.
  • yiiwei means taking something to be true, often wrongly.
  • yueh ... yueh ... is a pattern, not just two repeated words.
  • Pretransitives like bae break up object-plus-complement meanings in a way that differs from English.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 6 as a dream narrative

This lesson is easiest to teach when broken into scenes with clear turning points.

Scene 1 Tzwo.tian woo tzuoh-wan.le i-jiann yawjiin .de shyh.chyng, kee.sh tzuoh .de heen ley, tzuoh .de ley-jyi.le.
Yesterday I finished an important matter, but I was very tired from working, extremely tired.
Scene 1 Hwei.lai .le lian woanfann yee bu sheang chy, i-woan fann dou chy.bu-wan.
After coming back I did not even want supper, and could not even finish one bowl of rice.
Scene 1 Woo jiow tzuoh .tzay ig dahyiitz .shanq shie.x, chou.x ian .sherm.de.
So I sat on a big sofa to rest a bit and smoke a little and so on.
Scene 2 Gang chou-wan.le i-gel shiang'ian, kann.jiann ig ianchiual.lii hao.shianq yeou g shanshoei-huall .shyh.de.
Just after finishing a cigarette, I saw that inside a smoke ring there seemed to be something like a landscape painting.
Scene 2 Jeyg ianchiual tzoong bu sann.kai. Woo chuei.x .ta, yee lao chuei.bu-sann.
The smoke ring simply would not dissolve. I blew at it, but still could not blow it apart.
Scene 3 Hao.shianq woo tzyhjii ren yee tzoou .daw ianchiual.lii .chiuh .le.
It seemed that I myself had also walked into the smoke ring.
Scene 3 Syhmiann i-kann, yee kann.bu-jiann woo u.lii .de dong.shi .le.
Looking all around, I could no longer see the things in my room.
Scene 4 Jiow jyuej woo tzay ig dah-hae .shanq.tou lao .tzay.nall fei .shyh.de.
I simply felt as if I were over a great sea, always flying there.
Scene 4 Kann.x dii.shiah, hao.shianq yeou i-tzuoh .v.x .de heen haokann .de haedao.
Looking below, it seemed there were one beautiful island after another.
Scene 5 Woo lao sheang fei, lao fei.bu-chiilai, jey.hwei kee jen fei.chii.lai .le.
I had always wanted to fly and could never fly up; this time I really flew up.
Scene 5 Fei .de yow gau yow kuay, fei .de jen haowal! Jen yeou yih.sy!
I flew both high and fast; flying was really fun and really interesting.
Scene 6 Kee.sh bye diaw.le.shiah.chiuh … bu.sh diaw .tzay haedao.shanq shuai.syy, jiow.sh huey diaw .de hae-shoei .lii ian.syy .ne.
But don’t fall down … either I would fall on an island and smash to death, or I would fall into the sea and drown.
Scene 6 Deeng .woo fei-shiah.chiuh .i.deal kann.x .ba.
Let me fly down a little and take a look.
Scene 7 Woo i wann-shiah fei, jiow ijyr lao wann-shiah fei, tzay sheang fei.chii.lai yow fei.bu-chiilai .le.
Once I started flying downward, I just kept going downward, and when I wanted to fly up again I could not fly up anymore.
Scene 7 Woo yueh diaw yueh di … ijyr di .daw kann.de-jiann dii.shiah hao.shianq yeou sheuduo ren .tzay.nall tzoou-dawl.
I fell lower and lower … so low that I could see what seemed like many people walking below.
Scene 8 Woo .tsair kann.chu.lai gangtsair woo yiiwei sh ren .de, bush jen.de ren, sh sheuduo dah-shuh.
Only then did I see that what I had taken for people were not real people, but many big trees.
Scene 8 Hualhalha! i-sheng, woo.de shentz diaw .de i-ke dah-shuh .de dieengl.shanq.
Crash! I fell onto the top of a big tree.
Scene 9 Jeng.kai.le yean.jing i-kann, … Sheir j(yd)aw gangtsair sh shuey-jaur.le, tz-h-uoh.le ig menq!
I opened my eyes and looked … who would have known, I had fallen asleep and had had a dream!

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Narrative completion is built into the verb

tzuoh-wan.le
finish doing
  • The notes explain tzuoh-wan as “do to a finish.”
  • Lesson 6 begins with completed work and inability to finish food, immediately setting up contrast between finishing and not finishing.

2) Past action with quantified objects follows a pattern

verb + .le
one bowl
two things
  • The notes state that when a past action verb has an object containing a quantity word, the verb takes .le.
  • This is a subtle but important pattern for storytelling.

3) Seeming can be marked several ways

hao.shianq
.shyh.de
jyuej
  • The lesson uses both hao.shianq and .shyh.de for “it seems as if.”
  • jyuej adds “I felt that.”
  • This lets students distinguish visible fact from dreamlike impression.

4) Directional complements are the engine of the story

chii.lai
shiah.chiuh
chiuh
daw
  • The notes explain that motion verbs like lai and chiuh can attach after another verb to show direction.
  • In this story, flying up, flying down, walking into, and falling onto all depend on these forms.

5) “The more … the more …” is a real pattern

yueh ... yueh ...
  • Woo yueh diaw yueh di = the more I fell, the lower I got.
  • This is a memorable pattern because the story gives it a vivid physical meaning.

6) Mistaken perception is explicit

yiiwei
kann.chu.lai
  • yiiwei means taking something to be the case, often wrongly.
  • The story later corrects the mistake with kann.chu.lai: what seemed like people are actually trees.

7) Alternatives and consequence are tightly linked

buran
bu.sh A jiow.sh B
  • The story imagines two possible bad outcomes: fall on an island and die, or fall into the sea and drown.
  • This gives beginners an early example of alternative consequence logic.

8) Pretransitives break up object-plus-result meaning

bae
object + complement
  • The notes explain that instead of tightly keeping object and complement together, Chinese often uses bae plus the object before the result phrase.
  • This is an important bridge toward later Mandarin structures.

High-value classroom move

Storyboard the dream

This lesson works especially well as an eight-panel storyboard.
  1. Panel 1: finished work, very tired, can’t finish supper.
  2. Panel 2: smoke ring with a tiny landscape inside.
  3. Panel 3: enters the smoke ring; room disappears.
  4. Panel 4: flying over sea and islands.
  5. Panel 5: joy of flying high and fast.
  6. Panel 6: fear of falling; tries to fly down a little.
  7. Panel 7: falls lower and lower, mistakes trees for people.
  8. Panel 8: crash, opens eyes, realizes it was a dream.

Students can retell one panel each, which keeps the lesson rich but manageable.

A key structural contrast

Seeming, feeling, and wrongly thinking

It seems as if …

hao.shianq ... .shyh.de

This marks appearance or seeming, not firm fact.

I felt that …

jyuej ...

This adds personal inner perception.

I took it to be …

yiiwei ...

This often introduces a wrong interpretation.

Then I finally saw …

kann.chu.lai

This gives the correction or discovery.

These are ideal for teaching students how a story can move from uncertainty to realization.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) Why are directional complements so important in Lesson 6?

Correct. Lesson 6 depends on movement, and the directional complements are what make that movement precise and vivid.
Not quite. In this lesson the directional complements are central because they drive the story’s motion and change of scene.

2) What is the best explanation of yiiwei in this lesson?

Correct. The notes gloss yiiwei as taking something to be true, and the story uses it for mistaken perception.
Not quite. In Lesson 6, yiiwei is about taking something to be true, especially when that interpretation later turns out to be wrong.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Say what happened first, second, and last in the story.
  2. Use one sentence with hao.shianq and one with yiiwei.
  3. Use one upward and one downward directional complement correctly.
  4. Explain, in English, what the narrator mistook for people.
  5. State the twist ending in one short sentence.
