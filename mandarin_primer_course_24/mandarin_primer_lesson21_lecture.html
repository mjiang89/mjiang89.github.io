Mandarin Primer — Lesson 21

Listening and Listening In

Lesson 21 is a major metalinguistic lesson. Instead of focusing on one real-life setting like renting a house or visiting a factory, it turns language learning itself into the topic. The lesson moves through dictation, classroom observation, praise for conversational fluency, discussion of dialects, and then a comic scene in Shanghai where people mock foreigners in front of foreigners who actually understand them. It is an ideal lesson for teaching listening, accent awareness, sociolinguistic humility, and the difference between speaking a language and assuming others cannot.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so the page stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Understand Lesson 21’s three-part structure: dictation, classroom visit, and the Shanghai street scene.
  • See why listening is treated as a separate skill from speaking and reading.
  • Learn vocabulary for dictation, listening ability, correction, conversation practice, pronunciation, fluency, topic, dialect, accent, and embarrassment.
  • Recognize how accent, dialect, and register shape what people understand and how they are judged.
  • Understand the comic irony of assuming foreigners are linguistically ignorant.
  • Reflect on how language learning includes humility, noticing, and sociolinguistic awareness, not just grammar and vocabulary.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 21 teaches students to listen to listening itself. It asks not only “Can you say this?” but “Can you catch it at speed? Can you follow accent differences? Can you tell when someone actually understands you?”

For high-school students, this lesson is especially valuable because it captures a real danger in language learning: feeling confident when speech is slow and familiar, then falling apart when speech speeds up or accents shift. It also teaches respect. The Shanghai scene shows why mocking people in front of them can backfire badly.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Listening hook
Begin with the teacher’s diagnosis: students are doing fairly well, but their ability to listen to ordinary speech is still insufficient.
2
Dictation as skill
Teach why the sentences are given at ordinary speed and why one-by-one writing matters.
3
Classroom visit
Teach praise, metalinguistic evaluation, and the idea that students can now converse freely on many topics.
4
Dialect awareness
Focus on Cantonese, Shanghai dialect, Standard Mandarin, and what counts as “foreign accent.”
5
Shanghai irony scene
Work through the mocking conversation, the Shanghai accent, and the punch line that the foreigners understand enough to answer back.
6
Reflection + quiz
End with the embarrassment scene and a discussion of why linguistic assumptions are dangerous.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The “To the Student” pages emphasize utterances, repetition, echoing, and fluency. Lesson 21 adds something crucial: speed and unpredictability. Students need to hear what happens when Chinese is spoken as Chinese speakers ordinarily speak it.
  1. Actually do a mini-dictation in class at natural speed so students feel the pressure of listening in real time.
  2. Put the three acts on the board as a progression: skill test → public praise → social trap.
  3. Use one color for Standard Mandarin and another for Shanghai-accent lines so students can hear code and accent shifts.
  4. Ask students after each scene: what is the real lesson here about communication?
  5. Have students role-play the final street scene twice: once with ignorance, once with awareness of being overheard.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students may think the lesson is only about dictation, but it is really about listening across speed, register, dialect, and social assumptions.
  • ting shuo-huah means “listen to speaking,” while ting-huah can also mean “obey instructions.” The notes draw this contrast explicitly.
  • guay in “you talk too guay” reflects Student A’s confusion between aspirated and unaspirated sounds; the joke depends on a pronunciation error.
  • jinnlai with full tone on lai means “recently,” not the more ordinary directional “come in.”
  • Shanghai-accent English and Shanghai-accent Mandarin are both being stylized in the scene; students should hear this as performance as well as content.
  • bu da.yinq can mean “take offense at,” not just “do not answer.”
Lesson walkthrough

Lesson 21 as skill, praise, and social irony

This lesson becomes easiest to teach when its three acts are treated as one connected argument about listening.

Act I Jintian woo geei niimen ig shin de liannshyi tzuoh. Niimen shu dou hair niann de butsuoh, keesh niimen ting shuo-huah de beenshyh dou hair bushyng.
Today I am going to give you a new exercise. Your studies are not bad, but your ability to listen to speech is still not good enough.
Act I Woo shianntzay jaw Jonggworen pyngcharng shuo-huah de kuay-mann shuo jiig jiuhtz. Niimen tingle geei woo i-jiuh i-jiuh de dou shiee.shiahlai.
I am now going to say several sentences at the speed at which Chinese people ordinarily speak. After listening, write them down one by one.
Act I Lau Nin jiah, jiee jell shanq huooche-jann sh tzeem tzoou d'a? ... Woo bae jeyg shoou-tyibau ge de jell ay-shyh bu ay-shyh? ...
Excuse me, sir, how do I get from here to the railway station? ... Will this suitcase be in the way if I put it here? ...
Act I A: Shiansheng, nii shuo de tay guay. ... Bush bush, woo sh yaw shuo nii shuo de tay kuay. ... Woo shuo de tay mann!
A: Professor, you speak too “queer.” ... No, no, I mean too fast. ... I speak too slowly!
Act II Woo jintian lai tsanguan niimen shanq Jongwen-keh, jyuej feicharng de yeou chiuh.wey. Woo kann niimen ... dwu-in dwu de nemm joen, shuo-huah shuo de nemm liou.lih ...
I came today to visit your Chinese class and found it extremely interesting. I heard you pronounce so accurately and speak so fluently ...
Act II Tamen shianntzay sherm tyi.muh dou neng gen nii tarn ... kai-wanshiaw a, shenn.jyh.yu taoluenn shyueshuh a ...
They can now talk with you on any topic ... joke with you, or even discuss learned subjects ...
Act II A: Shiansheng, gangtsair nii gen Wu Shg. shuo de sh naal de huah? Sh Goangdong-huah hairsh Shanqhae-huah?
A: Professor, what dialect were you speaking with Mr. Wu just now? Cantonese or the Shanghai dialect?
Act III Jinnlai jeh dihfangl tzeem yueh biann yueh reh.naw le ... jie.shanq tingjiann de budann jinqsh shuo wayluh-koouin de ...
Recently this place seems to have grown more and more lively ... on the streets one hears not only accents from other provinces ...
Act III D: (Yonq Shanqhae-koouin) Tehbyesh woo jey-tzoong — Shanqhae sheng'in de Gwoyeu — waygworen tingjiann le idinq genq.jia moh-ming-chyi-miaw de.
D: Especially this kind of mine — National Language with a Shanghai accent — when foreigners hear it they will be even more at a loss.
Act III A: Nong weyte kaang Zanqhe-hhewoh va? (Nii huey shuo Shanghae-huah ba?) B: Hng! Dih Shyrliow Keh!
A: You can speak the Shanghai dialect, can’t you? B: Hng! Lesson Sixteen!
Act III C: Aiiau! Jeyg ren jende huey shuo Shanqhae huah! ... C: Nah tzarmen gangtsair yihluenn le tamen nemm banntian de huah, dou geei tamen ting le chiuh le!
C: Goodness, this man really can speak Shanghai! ... Then everything we were discussing about them for all that time was heard by them!

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Listening is not the same as studying

niann-shu
ting shuo-huah
  • The teacher says the students are studying fairly well, but listening to speech remains weak.
  • This separates academic preparation from real-time comprehension.

2) Dictation is framed as hearing whole utterances

ordinary speed
sentence-by-sentence
  • The dictation sentences are practical utterances, not abstract word lists.
  • This directly matches the book’s general philosophy of learning utterances in situations.

3) Mishearing can come from pronunciation weakness

guay / kuay
aspiration contrast
  • Student A says the teacher speaks too “queer,” then repairs it to “too fast.”
  • The notes explain this as a problem with aspirated versus unaspirated initials.

4) Fluency invites social mobility

kai-wanshiaw
taoluenn shyueshuh
  • The visiting guest is told the students can chat freely, joke, and even discuss learned subjects.
  • This lesson connects language competence to social and professional usefulness.

5) Dialect knowledge complicates identity

Goangdong-huah
Shanqhae-huah
Gwoyeu
  • The lesson treats dialect choice as noticeable and meaningful.
  • Students learn that “Chinese” is not one single undifferentiated speech variety.

6) Accent is socially judged

wayluh-koouin
piaw.lianq
moh-ming-chyi-miaw
  • Characters evaluate foreign accents, Shanghai-accented Mandarin, and polished speech.
  • This makes accent part of social meaning, not just sound.

7) The Shanghai scene is a lesson in overhearing

shiaw.huah
bu da.yinq
  • One speaker warns that foreigners might understand and take offense.
  • The warning turns out to be correct, making listening a matter of ethics as well as skill.

8) Embarrassment is the moral ending

buhaoyihsy
nanweichyng
  • The final emotion is not anger but embarrassment.
  • That softens the ending while preserving the lesson’s social sting.

High-value classroom move

Do a live listening experiment

Read a few short sentences once at careful speed and once at natural speed, then ask students what changed in their comprehension.
  1. Use practical sentences like the dictation lines in the lesson.
  2. Then add a playful accent shift or slight pronunciation twist.
  3. Afterward, connect the experience directly to Act III.

This lets students feel the lesson’s central message instead of only reading about it.

A key structural system

Skill → assumption → consequence

Skill

ting shuo-huah, dwu-in, liou.lih

Can you hear and process real speech?

Assumption

waygworen tingjiann le ...

What do you assume others can or cannot understand?

Consequence

buhaoyihsy, nanweichyng

Embarrassment follows bad assumptions.

Teaching payoff

The lesson shows that language ability is cognitive, social, and ethical all at once.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Dictation reflection

Students write down what made one dictation sentence hard: speed, sound confusion, unfamiliar words, or memory load.

Task B — Praise and doubt

Students identify which parts of Act II praise the learners and which parts still reveal anxiety about comprehension.

Task C — Accent and attitude

Students explain how the Shanghai scene links accent judgment to social judgment.

Task D — Rewrite the ending

Students create one more exchange after the embarrassed realization and decide whether the speakers apologize or pretend nothing happened.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to the notes, why is ting shuo-huah used in Act I instead of only ting-huah?

Correct. The notes explicitly make this distinction and it is central to the lesson’s focus on real listening.
Not quite. The lesson carefully distinguishes ordinary listening to speech from the broader expression that can also mean obeying orders.

2) What is the main irony of the Shanghai scene in Act III?

Correct. The humor and embarrassment come from the collapse of a bad assumption about who can understand what.
Not quite. The point of the scene is exactly that assumptions about others’ language ability can be very wrong.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Explain why the teacher gives a dictation exercise in Act I.
  2. Name one sentence type from the dictation that sounds like real everyday speech.
  3. Describe, in English, why the classroom visitor is impressed in Act II.
  4. Explain what mistake the speakers in Shanghai make about the foreigners.
  5. Name the final emotion of the ending and explain why it fits.
