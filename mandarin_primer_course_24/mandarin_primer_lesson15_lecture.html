Mandarin Primer — Lesson 15

World Geography

Lesson 15 shifts from the hospital storyline to the classroom. The setting is a geography review before a lecture on China. The lesson moves through continents, hemispheres, canals, oceans, countries, capitals, false starts, self-correction, and classroom humor. It is especially strong for teaching structured knowledge talk, recall under pressure, and how Mandarin handles correction and clarification in real time.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so it stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow a classroom discussion about world geography and basic political geography.
  • Understand vocabulary for continents, hemispheres, oceans, canals, countries, capitals, and political systems.
  • Recognize how speakers hesitate, self-correct, challenge errors, and recover from confusion.
  • Notice location expressions such as tzay ... de nanbial, yii-nan, dong bannchyou, and shi bannchyou.
  • Learn how the teacher’s more formal classroom register differs from ordinary conversation.
  • Use the lesson’s patterns to explain where continents, oceans, countries, and capitals are located.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 15 teaches knowledge as dialogue. Students are not just naming places. They are recalling, correcting, questioning, and refining what counts as a good answer.

For high-school students, this is powerful because it feels like an actual classroom: someone answers partly right, someone mixes up Austria and Australia, someone jokes about Moxico, and the class responds. It is also an excellent bridge from everyday talk to academic talk.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Classroom hook
Start with the teacher’s plan: before talking about China, let us review world geography first.
2
Continents and hemispheres
Teach Eastern vs Western Hemisphere, continent names, and basic relative location.
3
Oceans and canals
Map the Suez Canal, Mediterranean, Red Sea, Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Arctic, and Antarctic.
4
Classroom error correction
Highlight how the lesson handles confusion about Antarctica, Switzerland/Sweden, and Austria/Australia.
5
Countries and capitals
Work through large countries, then capitals, with deliberate attention to self-correction and humorous mistakes.
6
Map + quiz
Finish with a knowledge graph, oral map activity, and two comprehension checks.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source’s “To the Student” pages emphasize utterances, repetition, echoing, and grammar-through-exercises. Lesson 15 becomes especially effective when students can see the map while hearing the classroom exchange.
  1. Project or draw a world map and label it as the class speaks.
  2. Separate the lesson into three knowledge zones: continents, oceans, and countries/capitals.
  3. Keep the humorous mistakes in the lesson because they make memory stronger.
  4. Teach location phrases as reusable frames, not isolated words.
  5. Use role-play: teacher, Chyan, Lii, Wang, and whole class.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students may think this is just a vocabulary lesson. In fact, it is also a lesson in classroom discourse, formal register, and spatial relations.
  • me, ne, and a can all mark pauses, but the notes distinguish their discourse functions.
  • tzay Yahjou de nanbial versus Yahjou yii-nan are not exactly the same in meaning.
  • bey in this lesson can mean “recite by heart,” not only “be by” or a passive marker.
  • Similar-sounding country names create the humor: Switzerland/Sweden, Austria/Australia, Moscow/Moxico, New York/Washington.
  • The teacher’s register is a bit more formal, using forms like woomen in the sense of tzarmen and fewer -l forms.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 15 as a classroom review

This lesson works best when grouped as geography recall, correction, and recitation.

Opening Jintian jeang Jonggwo dihlii. Dannsh tzay meiyeou jeang jintian de gongkeh yiichyan ne, woomen inggai shian baa shanq-tsyh suoo jeang de shyhjieh dihlii uenlii i-biann.
Today we will talk about the geography of China. But before that, we ought first to review the world geography from last time.
Continents Dong Bannchyou tzuey dah de dahluh dangran jiowsh Yahjou le. Gen Yahjou Shi-buh lhianj de jiowsh Oujou. Tzay Oujou de Nanbial ... jiowsh Feijou.
The largest continent in the Eastern Hemisphere is Asia. On the west of Asia is Europe. South of Europe is Africa.
Canal logic Beenlai Ou-, Yah-, Fei- san-jou doush lhianj de. Howlai kaile i-tyau Suyishyh Yunnher, bae Horng Hae gen Dihjong Hae daa-tong le, suooyii Feijou gen Yahjou jiow fen.kai le.
Originally Europe, Asia, and Africa were connected. Later the Suez Canal was opened and the Red Sea connected to the Mediterranean, so Africa and Asia are now separated.
Hemispheres Dong Bannchyou me hair yeou Awjou, Shi Bannchyou yeou Beei Meeijou gen Nan Meeijou leang-piann dahluh, dangjong yeou i-tyau Banamaa Yunnher.
There is also Australia in the Eastern Hemisphere, and North and South America in the Western Hemisphere, with the Panama Canal between them.
Antarctica question Nanjyi Jou sh tzay Dong Bannchyou hairsh tzay Shi Bannchyou a?    Jihran jiaw Nanjyi Jou, nah jiow wusuoowey dong-shi lo.
Is Antarctica in the Eastern or Western Hemisphere? Since it is the South Pole continent, there is no point in talking about east or west.
Oceans Tzuey dah de me — dangran jiowsh Taypyng Yang lo ... chyitsyh sh Dahshi Yang ... chyitsyh sh Yinn.duh Yang ... Hair yeou Beei Bingyang gen Nan Bingyang.
The largest ocean is the Pacific, then the Atlantic, then the Indian, and then the Arctic and Antarctic oceans.
Country recall Jonggwo tzay Yahshihyah lo. Hair yeou Yinn.duh, gen Eh.gwo ... Oujou chwule Eh.gwo jy way, chyiyu de gwojia doush tiing sheau d'e!
China is in Asia. There is also India and Russia ... outside Russia, the other countries in Europe are rather small.
Teacher correction Rueyshyh sh Yihdahlih beeibial de neyg gonqhergwo ... nii shin.Iii sheangj de neyg sh Rueydean, sh g jiunjuu lihshiann de gwojia.
Switzerland is the republic north of Italy ... the one you had in mind was Sweden, a constitutional monarchy.
Mixed-up example Aw.gwo sh Oujou de gwojia e ... Awjou sh Inggwo de lianbang jy i, yeou g dwulih de jenqfuu, yeesh g minjuu-gwojia.
Austria is a European country ... Australia is one of the British dominions, with an independent government and also a democratic country.
Capital humor Su Eh Lianbang de shooudu tzay Mohshige — ... woo sh yaw shuo Mohsyke ... Meeigwo Neouiue — Oh, bush vx, Meeilihjian de shooudu sh Hwashenqduenn.
The capital of the U.S.S.R. is at “Moxico” — ... I mean Moscow ... America at New York — Oh no, the capital of the United States is Washington.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Geography is taught as recitation plus correction

teacher asks
student recalls
teacher corrects
  • The class is not a lecture only. It is an interactive review in which students supply facts and the teacher refines them.
  • That makes the lesson excellent for spoken classroom practice.

2) The lesson builds a map through relations

east/west
north/south
between
connected/separated
  • Students are not just asked to name places. They must relate them spatially.
  • This is a strong lesson for learning how Mandarin describes where things are relative to one another.

3) Canals change geography in the story

Suez Canal
Panama Canal
  • The Suez Canal is used to explain the separation of Africa and Asia.
  • The Panama Canal links the discussion of North and South America.

4) Pause particles have different discourse jobs

me
ne
a / ia
  • The notes explain that me buys the speaker time to think, while ne or a can give the listener time to process.
  • This is important because the lesson is full of hesitant recall.

5) Formal classroom style differs from ordinary conversation

woomen
baa
fewer -l forms
  • The notes explicitly point out that the teacher speaks in a somewhat more formal style.
  • This helps students hear register as part of meaning.

6) Similar names create memory anchors

Rueyshyh / Rueydean
Aw.gwo / Awjou
Mohshige / Mohsyke
  • The lesson deliberately becomes memorable through near-miss answers and corrections.
  • These mistakes are pedagogically useful, not just comic.

7) Capitals are recited as linked world knowledge

Nanjing
Moscow
Berlin
Paris
Washington
  • The capital list turns geography into a chain of spoken associations.
  • It is a good place to practice rhythm and recall.

8) Political vocabulary enters naturally

gonqhergwo
jiunjuu lihshiann
lianbang
minjuu-gwojia
  • The lesson introduces terms for republic, constitutional monarchy, dominion, independent government, and democracy.
  • That makes it richer than a simple map lesson.

High-value classroom move

Run it as a live geography review

Assign students the roles of teacher, Chyan, Lii, Wang, and class, then let them perform the lesson with a map behind them.
  1. The “teacher” points to places and asks questions.
  2. The student speakers answer, hesitate, and self-correct.
  3. The rest of the class reacts at the comic mistakes just like in the text.

This makes the lesson memorable and highlights its authentic classroom rhythm.

A key structural system

Location language in Lesson 15

In the south of

tzay Yahjou de nanbuh

Inside a region, specifically in its southern part.

To the south of

Yahjou yii-nan

Outside the region, south of it.

Between

tzay Yahjou Meeijou de dangjong

Used for oceans or canals positioned between larger regions.

Teaching payoff

This lesson is a natural place to train students to think spatially instead of translating word by word.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Map the continents

Students label all the continents and say whether each belongs mainly to the Eastern or Western Hemisphere as framed in the lesson.

Task B — Ocean recitation

Students recite the names and positions of the five oceans in order, using the lesson’s phrasing.

Task C — Correction pairs

Students explain the difference between Switzerland and Sweden, Austria and Australia, Moscow and “Moxico,” New York and Washington.

Task D — Capital chain

Students take turns giving one country and the next student gives its capital, continuing the chain around the room.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to the notes, what is the main discourse difference between a pause with me and a pause with ne or a?

Correct. The notes explicitly distinguish these pause particles by whose processing time they support.
Not quite. Lesson 15 specifically explains that me buys time for the speaker, while ne and a help the listener follow.

2) Which pair best represents the lesson’s pattern of classroom correction?

Correct. The lesson explicitly corrects Switzerland and Sweden, and uses several other similar-sounding corrections too.
Not quite. One of the most memorable features of Lesson 15 is the correction of near-miss answers like Switzerland versus Sweden.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Name the continents discussed in the lesson.
  2. Say where the Pacific Ocean is located relative to Asia and America.
  3. Give one example of a country/capital pair from the lesson.
  4. Explain, in English, one correction the teacher makes and why it matters.
  5. Use one location frame from the lesson correctly in a new sentence.
