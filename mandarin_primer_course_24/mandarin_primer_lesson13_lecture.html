Mandarin Primer — Lesson 13

Inquiring After a Sick Man

Lesson 13 moves the crash story into a hospital setting. The focus is now on fever, temperature, mental clarity, injuries, treatment, and bedside etiquette. It is a strong lesson for teaching how Mandarin handles condition updates, reported speech, diagnosis, and care.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so it stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow a hospital conversation about the condition of an injured patient.
  • Understand how Mandarin asks about improvement, fever, temperature, consciousness, pain, injuries, treatment, and hospital procedure.
  • Recognize temperature language in both Centigrade and Fahrenheit, including the conversion example in the notes.
  • Notice subject-predicate relations such as ta sh 38.5°.
  • Learn medical and hospital vocabulary such as kanhuh, binq-ren, jie-guu, kai-dau, way-shang, ney-shang, maafei-jen, and jiowshang-che.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 13 teaches explanation under care. The speakers report what nurses and doctors said, interpret symptoms, and try to understand risk.

For high-school students, this lesson is valuable because it connects language to a real human situation: worry, relief, careful questioning, and professional medical judgment. It also introduces scientific and numerical expressions in a meaningful context.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Hospital hook
Begin with quiet voices, patients sleeping, and asking whether someone is better today.
2
Condition update
Teach fever, temperature change, and relative improvement.
3
Temperature reasoning
Use the source’s conversion example and explain Centigrade vs Fahrenheit.
4
Mental clarity
Focus on muddled mind, delirious speech, and not knowing where one is.
5
Diagnosis and treatment
Teach external/internal injuries, broken bones, muscles, anesthetic, iodine, first aid, ambulance.
6
Doctor + quiz
Finish with the famous surgeon, polite waiting, and a final check for understanding.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source emphasizes utterances, echoing, repetition, and grammar-through-exercises. Lesson 13 becomes especially teachable when the medical information is organized visually rather than presented as one long explanation.
  1. Use a patient-status board with four columns: fever, mind, injuries, treatment.
  2. Keep the hospital social setting visible: visitors, nurse, doctor, patient, room, ward.
  3. Teach the temperature part slowly with a number line and conversion steps.
  4. Differentiate clearly between external injuries, internal injuries, broken bones, and muscle injury.
  5. Turn the conversation into a sequence of “questions families ask at hospitals.”
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students can lose the thread if the teacher does not keep the overall condition of the patient visible.
  • Centigrade vs Fahrenheit may feel mathematically dense.
  • rehduh vs uenduh are not the same.
  • Subject-predicate relations such as ta sh 38.5° do not map directly onto ordinary English subject structure.
  • ren in ta ren hair chingchuu ha? refers to the state of mind.
  • Medical verbs need contextual explanation, not dictionary-style memorization.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 13 as a medical update conversation

This lesson is easiest to teach as a series of hospital questions and answers.

Scene 1Kanhuh: Uai, dueybujuh, chiing niimen sheng'in sheau ideal!
Nurse: Excuse me, please keep your voices a little lower.
Scene 1Jell tzarm buneng tzemm dahsheng shuo-huah, hweitour bae binq-ren geei naw-shiing le.
We cannot talk so loudly here, otherwise we might wake the patient.
Scene 2Ta jintian haodeal le ma? Hair fa-shau bu fa le? Hair yeou shau meiyou?
Is he better today? Is he still running a fever? Does he still have a fever?
Scene 2Jintian bii tzwotian hao de duo le ... keesh jintian tsorng tzaochin chii rehduh jiow jiannjialde di.shiah.lai le.
He is much better today than yesterday ... and since this morning his temperature has gradually come down.
Scene 2Tzwotian tzuey gau gau daw syhshyri-duh-san ne, jintian shanqwuu ba-shyr sanshyr-fen de shyrhowl jianq daw sanshyr-ba-duh-bann le.
Yesterday the highest temperature was 41.3°C, but today at 8:30 a.m. it had dropped to 38.5°C.
Scene 3Woo tsornglai yee yonq.bu.guann Shehshyh de rehduhbeau de.
I can never get used to the Centigrade thermometer.
Scene 3Shehshyh de sanshyr-chi-duh sh deengyu Hwashyh de jeoushyr-ba-duh-liow ... shianntzay ta sh sanshyr-ba-duh-wuu ... sh Hwashyh ibae-ling-i-duh yow shyr-fenn jy san.
37°C equals 98.6°F ... now he is 38.5°C ... that is 101.3°F.
Scene 4Ta ren hair chingchuu ha?
Is his mind still clear?
Scene 4Tzwotian fa-shau fa de lihhay de shyrhowl ren jiow yeoudeal hwu.twu, jinq shuo hwu-huah, lian tzyhgeel ren tzay naal yee bujydaw, ren yee budah rennde.
Yesterday, when the fever was severe, his mind was confused and he talked deliriously, not knowing where he was or recognizing people well.
Scene 5Yong buyong jie-guu huohjee kai-dau shermde?
Is it necessary to set the bone or perform an operation or something?
Scene 5Keeyii buyong jie-guu, yee buyong yong sherm bye-joong de shooushuh.
There is no need to set any bones, and there is no need for another operation.
Scene 5Yeou sh yeou jii-chuh shau-shangle de dihfangl, gen haojii-chuh way-shang.
He does have several burned places and quite a few external injuries.
Scene 5Ta chii-tourl pah dahtoei de gwutou sher le, howlai char.chulai binq mei sher. Jianbaangl nail dahgay yee jyy yeou jinrow showle deal shang.
At first they feared the thigh bone had broken, but later found it had not; the shoulder probably had only muscle injury.
Scene 6Jiow guangsh way-shang yee idinq terng de yawminq lo.
Even the external injuries alone must hurt terribly.
Scene 6Woomen geei ta nonq.shanq chwan lai de shyrhowl, ta hair tzaynall shyy-jinn fuhj shoei.
When we got him up into the boat, he was still swimming hard.
Scene 6Woomen geeile ta deal borlandih he ... shanqle dah-chwan yiihow, ta jiow iun.guohchiuh le.
We gave him some brandy ... after getting on the big ship, he fainted.
Scene 7Jyy hao shian geei ta daa deal jyy-tonq de maafei-jen a, shanq deal shiau-dwu de deanjeou a ... jiow-jyi de bannfaa.
The only thing to do was to give some morphine by injection, apply iodine, and carry out first-aid measures.
Scene 7Gaan chwan i kawle ann jiow daa-diannhuah jiawle i-lianq jiowshang-che bae ta sonq.jinn jeyg iyuann li lai le.
As soon as the ship docked, they telephoned for an ambulance and sent him into this hospital.
Scene 8Neyg jiowsh Niou Dayfu; ta sh jell tzuey chuming de wayke-i.sheng le.
That is Dr. New; he is the most famous surgeon around here.
Scene 8Ta budann beenshyh hao, shyuewenn hao, ren yee feicharng her.chih, ideal jiahtz yee meiyeou.
He is not only skilled and learned, but also charming and without pretension.
Scene 8Chiing niimen ell-wey tzay deeng.deengl, ah! Deeng woo bae binqshyh shian shyr.dou-vx-hao! Ihoel jiow der!
Please wait another moment, gentlemen. Let me tidy up the room first. It will be ready in a minute.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Hospital etiquette shapes the conversation

quiet voices
waiting
asking permission
  • The lesson begins with volume control and ends with waiting politely before entering.
  • This shows that language learning includes social setting, not just information.

2) Improvement is described relative to yesterday

bii tzwotian hao de duo
jiannjialde di.shiah.lai
  • The patient is better than yesterday and the temperature has gradually come down.
  • This makes the lesson excellent for teaching change over time.

3) Temperature is both medical and grammatical

rehduh
uenduh
Shehshyh
Hwashyh
  • The notes distinguish high temperature words from general temperature words.
  • The conversation also makes students reason numerically.

4) Subject-predicate relations matter

ta sh 38.5°
  • The notes explain that the Chinese subject is the topic under discussion.
  • This is an important conceptual step for learners expecting English-style clause structure.

5) The lesson distinguishes kinds of injury

shau-shangle
way-shang
ney-shang
show-shang
  • The doctor’s report is careful: burns, external injuries, possible bone damage, muscle injury, and no internal injuries.
  • This shows how diagnostic language narrows possibilities.

6) Reported speech drives the medical narrative

woo ting neyg kanhuh shuo
ta shuo
  • The visitors rely on what the nurse and doctor said.
  • The lesson therefore gives repeated practice in reporting professional information indirectly.

7) Treatment is described step by step

daa-jen
maafei
shiau-dwu
deanjeou
jiow-jyi
  • The lesson explains what could be done on the ship before the patient reached the hospital.
  • This gives the conversation a strong procedural flow.

8) Professional admiration is part of the scene

chuming
wayke-i.sheng
her.chih
jiahtz
  • The description of Dr. New combines skill, learning, charm, and humility.
  • This creates a different kind of evaluation from earlier lessons.

High-value classroom move

Use a bedside briefing simulation

Put students into roles: family visitor, nurse, doctor, and note-taker.
  1. The visitors ask about fever, pain, and mind clarity.
  2. The nurse reports what she observed.
  3. The doctor gives a careful diagnosis and treatment summary.
  4. The note-taker fills in the patient-status chart in English or simple Mandarin.
Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to the notes, what is the best description of uenduh compared with rehduh?

Correct. The notes distinguish uenduh as temperature in general from rehduh as a word associated with high temperature.
Not quite. Lesson 13 uses this distinction to help describe fever and ordinary body temperature more precisely.

2) Why is the example ta sh 38.5° important in the notes?

Correct. The notes use this example to explain that the Chinese subject is the subject matter under discussion, not necessarily the same kind of grammatical subject English requires.
Not quite. This note is one of the lesson’s key grammar insights.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Say whether the patient is better today and give one piece of evidence.
  2. State one temperature from the lesson and what it means.
  3. Explain one injury the doctor ruled out and one injury that remained possible or real.
  4. Describe one emergency treatment step taken before arrival at the hospital.
  5. Explain, in English, why the visitors cannot enter the room immediately at the end.
