Mandarin Primer — Lesson 20

The Walrus and the Carpenter

Lesson 20 is a major shift in genre. Instead of everyday conversation or civic discussion, students encounter a literary adaptation: Yuen Ren Chao’s spoken-Chinese rendering of Lewis Carroll’s “The Walrus and the Carpenter.” This lesson is unusually rich because it teaches imagery, rhythm, sound play, absurdity, literary narration, and how spoken Mandarin can carry a poem while still sounding vivid and teachable.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so the page stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow the poem as a narrated story rather than as a list of isolated vocabulary words.
  • Understand how the lesson builds an absurd world: sun at midnight, dry sand, no clouds, no birds, talking sea creatures, and philosophical conversation before betrayal.
  • Learn literary and narrative vocabulary such as haeshianq, muh.jianq, guei.jyh, jiue-tzoei, sann-buh, duenbuh, hwangyou, keelian, and chy-leaule.
  • Notice how the translation uses deliberate oddness and spoken rhythm to mirror Carroll’s nonsense style.
  • Recognize how the poem moves structurally from strange setting → invitation → procession → discussion → meal → betrayal.
  • Use the poem to practice storytelling, imagery, and tone rather than only transactional speech.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 20 teaches that spoken Chinese can also be literary, playful, and surreal. It stretches students beyond ordinary realism and shows how rhythm, imagery, and narrative voice can become language-learning tools.

For high-school students, this lesson is memorable because it is funny, eerie, and slightly cruel. The oysters dress up even though they have no feet. The walrus sounds sympathetic while selecting the largest oysters to eat. This makes the lesson excellent for deep reading and discussion.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Absurd-world hook
Begin with the impossible setting: the sun shines on the sea in the middle of the night.
2
World-building images
Teach sun, moon, sea, sand, clouds, birds, and why the scene feels wrong in a deliberate way.
3
The invitation
Introduce the walrus, the carpenter, the oysters, the beach walk, and the persuasion strategy.
4
The procession and the talk
Teach the comic march, the row of oysters, and the famous “talk of many things.”
5
The meal and the trick
Focus on bread, vinegar, pepper, sympathy language, and the gap between words and actions.
6
Ending + quiz
Finish with the revelation that the oysters have all been eaten and discuss why the poem is funny and unsettling at the same time.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source’s “To the Student” pages still apply here, but Lesson 20 especially benefits from performance. Students should hear it as a narrated poem with voice, gesture, and changing mood.
  1. Read the poem aloud in sections with strong voice changes for narrator, walrus, carpenter, and oysters.
  2. Keep a “strange world” list on the board: sun at midnight, dry sand, no clouds, no birds, shoe-tying oysters with no feet.
  3. Teach the story arc visually as a plot line rather than as disconnected stanzas.
  4. Pause after each stanza and ask: is this funny, sad, suspicious, or absurd?
  5. Use simple drawings or icons for each stage: moon, beach, oysters, bread, butter, tears, empty shore.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students may assume poems are too advanced for beginners, but the lesson becomes manageable once they see that it is still built from clear narrative scenes and repeated patterns.
  • Some lines are deliberately “un-Chinese” because the translation preserves Carroll’s oddness; the notes explicitly mention this for forms like gan d' shianq gan.
  • Absurdity is intentional: many odd lines should not be “fixed” into realism.
  • tzay can be a shortened progressive marker in current prose, as the notes explain.
  • The walrus’s sympathy is not trustworthy; students may miss the irony if they hear only the words and not the action.
  • Literary narration and spoken rhythm coexist; the lesson is neither pure poetry in a formal written style nor ordinary conversation.
Dialogue-poem walkthrough

Lesson 20 as a staged literary story

This lesson is easiest to teach as a story told in six movements.

Movement 1 Tayyang jaw tzay dah-hae shanq ... keesh jeh heen guay, i'wey nah jenq sh tzay bannyeh san-geng-bann.
The sun was shining on the sea ... but that was very strange, because it was the small hours of the night.
Movement 1 Yueh.lianq kannle jiueje tzoei ... "Ta jeanjyr mei guei.jeu ... tzemm pao.lai chai wood' tair."
The moon pouted ... “He simply has no manners ... coming to spoil my fun like this.”
Movement 2 Nii kannb'jiann tianshanq i-piann yun, l'wey binq mei yun tzay tian ... yee meiyeou neaul tzay chuan-kong guoh.
You could not see a cloud in the sky, because there was no cloud there ... and there were no birds flying overhead.
Movement 3 Haeshianq gen ig Muh.jianq ... "Yawsh jeh dou sao-ching le ... chiibush feicharng hao?"
The Walrus and a Carpenter ... “If all this were cleared away, wouldn’t that be excellent?”
Movement 3 "Eh, Lihhwangmen ... lai gen woom sannsann-buh ... Lai shuoshuo-huah, lai daadaa-chah ..."
“O Oysters ... come and take a walk with us ... come and chat a little ...”
Movement 4 Yeou syhg sheau lihhwangl heen sheang lai ... tam shuale ishang shiile lean, bae shyedall yee jih-hao ... keesh jeh heen guay ... tam yahgel jiow mei jeau.
Four little oysters were eager to come ... they brushed their coats, washed their faces, and tied up their shoestrings ... but that was very strange, because they did not have any feet.
Movement 4 Nah Haeshianq shuo, "Lai tarn-huah ba ... shuo shye, shuo chwan, hair shuo huoochi ... shuo bairtsay, gen gwowang ..."
The Walrus said, “Let us talk ... of shoes, of ships, and sealing-wax, of cabbages, and kings ...”
Movement 5 "Tzarm tzuey yawjiin d' sh lai g dah-miannbau ... hao-tzwo.liaw sh suan-tsuh gen hwujiau ... hao, tzarm jiow donq-shoou tiau."
“The most important thing is a big loaf of bread ... vinegar and pepper are good seasonings ... all right, let us begin to select them.”
Movement 5 Nah Haeshianq shuo, "Woo wey niim ku a ... Ai! niim jen keelian!" Ta yeanley-uang'uangld' tzaynall tiau, bae dah de dou jao-chyuan ...
The Walrus said, “I cry for you ... Oh, you are truly pitiable!” Tearfully he sorted out all the large ones ...
Movement 6 "Hwei-jia le, hei!" nah Muh.jianq shuo ... keesh jeh nan guay, i'wey tam ge'l-lea bae gehgehl dou chy-leau.
“Time to go home!” said the Carpenter ... but that was no wonder, because the two chums had eaten every one of them.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) The poem opens by breaking reality immediately

sun at night
moon complains
  • The opening creates a world that is wrong on purpose.
  • This teaches students to expect literary logic rather than realistic logic.

2) Absurdity is built through careful detail

no clouds
no birds
wet sea / dry sand
  • The poem does not just say “something strange happened.” It keeps adding precise details that make the world feel impossible.
  • This is a great lesson in descriptive accumulation.

3) The walrus and carpenter are not equal in tone

weeps
requests
comments on butter
  • The walrus sounds emotional and persuasive; the carpenter often sounds flatter and more practical.
  • This contrast is useful for teaching characterization through speech style.

4) The oysters are innocent, not foolish in a simple way

eager
out of breath
fat
  • The oysters are social, polite, and trusting.
  • That makes the ending feel more ironic and slightly cruel.

5) “Talking of many things” is a delay tactic

shoes
ships
cabbages
kings
  • The famous list feels playful, but it also distracts from the danger.
  • This is a good place to teach how playful speech can hide intention.

6) Food language shifts the poem toward danger

bread
vinegar
pepper
pick out
  • Once seasonings appear, students can sense that the oysters are being reclassified as food.
  • This is the turning point of the poem.

7) Sympathy becomes ironic performance

weep for you
largest size
handkerchief
  • The walrus’s tears do not cancel his violence.
  • That tension is central to the poem’s moral discomfort.

8) The final silence is the punch line

no answer
all eaten
  • The last joke lands because the poem calmly explains why there is no response.
  • This flat closing tone intensifies the absurd humor.

High-value classroom move

Use a staged poem reading

Divide the class into narrator, walrus, carpenter, and oysters.
  1. The narrator reads the descriptive lines.
  2. The walrus reads persuasive and emotional lines.
  3. The carpenter reads shorter, drier comments.
  4. The oysters speak together as a chorus.

This helps students hear voice, irony, and plot rather than treating the poem like a flat text.

A key structural system

Invitation → trust → consumption

Invitation

lai gen woom sannsann-buh

Come take a walk with us.

Trust

lai shuoshuo-huah, niim jen gaushinq

Pleasant talk and apparent friendliness.

Consumption

donq-shoou tiau, chy-leaule

Start selecting them; they were all eaten.

Teaching payoff

Students can see how language of friendliness can hide a very different intention.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Strange world list

Students list every impossible or absurd feature in the opening stanzas and explain why it matters.

Task B — Character voices

Students compare how the walrus speaks versus how the carpenter speaks and what that reveals.

Task C — When do you realize the oysters are in danger?

Students identify the exact line or stanza where the poem shifts from playful to threatening.

Task D — Retell in prose

Following the exercise on page 8, students rewrite the story in simple prose and prepare to tell it aloud.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to the notes, what is special about the expression gan d' shianq gan?

Correct. The notes explicitly point out that this line is intentionally strange in Chinese, just as the English original is strange.
Not quite. The lesson intentionally preserves oddness here rather than smoothing it into ordinary prose.

2) What is the clearest reason the final line is both funny and unsettling?

Correct. The humor comes from the flat, almost casual explanation of something grim, which is exactly what makes the ending memorable.
Not quite. The poem’s last joke depends on the quiet tone of the revelation that there are no oysters left to answer.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Name two impossible things in the poem’s opening world.
  2. Explain why the oysters’ preparation scene is funny.
  3. Say, in English, when the poem begins to feel dangerous.
  4. Describe one line that makes the walrus seem sympathetic and one action that proves he is not.
  5. Retell the final twist in one or two clear sentences.
