Mandarin Primer — Lesson 1

You, I, and He — “Four Men”

This page turns Lesson 1 into a rich lecture for English-speaking beginners. It follows the book’s own teaching philosophy: build from sound and repetition, teach whole utterances instead of isolated word lists, and help students understand how meaning, grammar, and conversation fit together.

Note: the lesson is presented in the book’s original romanization (for example nii, woo, ta, sh), not modern pinyin.

What students should learn

Learning goals

  • Recognize and use the basic pronouns you, I, he/she/it: nii, woo, ta.
  • Ask and answer identity questions like Nii sh sheir? and Ta sh sheir .a?
  • Understand how the lesson builds a small conversation out of people, counting, and group membership.
  • See that Mandarin often teaches meaning through patterns and utterances, not one-word-for-one-word translation.
  • Notice the difference between inclusive tzar.men (“we, including you”) and exclusive woo.men (“we, not including you”).
  • Practice beginner-friendly question forms, particles, and counting logic.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

This is not “just pronouns.” Lesson 1 quietly teaches students how Mandarin organizes social reference, how questions are built, how counting works with a noun like ren “person,” and how conversation can move by checking, correcting, and including or excluding speakers.

For beginners, the lesson is especially useful because the vocabulary is small, but the conceptual payoff is large: students learn how to track who is being talked about and who belongs in a group. That is real conversational thinking.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Warm-up and ears first
Review that Mandarin depends on sound distinctions. Have students echo a few lesson words and notice that pronunciation matters from the very start.
2
Story setup
Introduce the four named people: Ding I, Wang Ell, Jang San, Lii Syh. Put them on the board as a mini cast.
3
Dialogue in chunks
Read the conversation in short stretches. After each chunk, pause for English explanation and quick choral repetition.
4
Grammar from use
Pull out patterns only after students have heard them: pronouns, sh, question words, .ne, yee.sh, number phrases.
5
Knowledge graph review
Show how every form connects: people → pronouns → groupings → counting → “we” forms → yes/no checking.
6
Practice and quiz
Students answer, correct, and justify. End with two quick multiple-choice checks and a short spoken exit task.
Big idea from the book

How to teach this lesson well

The book’s message is clear: students should learn utterances in situations, not just memorize loose vocabulary.
  1. Start in English so beginners feel secure, then bring Mandarin back in every few minutes.
  2. Use echoing: teacher says a phrase, students look away, repeat it, then check it.
  3. Teach from whole lines such as Nii sh sheir? and Woo sh Wang Ell., not from random word lists.
  4. Keep the same names and groupings visible so students can follow the counting logic.
  5. Ask students to physically point to pictures or classmates when using nii, woo, ta, tzar.men, woo.men.
Common beginner confusions

What English-speaking students may struggle with

Beginners often expect Mandarin to map neatly onto English. This lesson shows that it often does not.
  • No required English-style “it” subject: Sh woo. means “It is I / It’s me,” but Mandarin does not need a dummy subject.
  • The same pronoun form covers multiple English forms: woo can mean “I” or “me.”
  • Question words stay in place: Nii sh sheir? is literally “You are who?”
  • Chinese nouns do not mark plural the same way English does: ren can mean person, people, man, woman, human being depending on context.
  • “We” has two meanings in this lesson: one includes the listener, one does not.
Lesson text as conversation

Dialogue walkthrough

Selected lines are grouped to make the teaching flow visible. Keep returning to “Who is being counted? Who is included?”

Ding 1 Sheir .a?
Who is it?
Wang 2 Woo, sh woo.
Me — it is I.
Ding 1 Nii sh sheir?
Who are you?
Wang 2 Woo sh Wang Ell. Nii .ne?
I am Wang Ell. And you?
Ding 1 Woo .ia, woo sh Ding I. Ta sh sheir .a?
Me? I am Ding I. Who is he?
Wang 2 Ta sh Jang San.
He is Jang San.
Ding 1 Jang San sh sherm.me?
What is Jang San?
Wang 2 Jang San sh ren.
Jang San is a person / man.
Ding 1 Nii gen woo, tzar.men sh jiig ren?
You and I — how many people are we?
Wang 2 Woo gen nii, tzar.men yee.sh leangg ren.
You and I — we are also two people.
Ding 1 Ne.me leangg ren gen leangg ren sh jiig ren .ne? Sh san'g ren .bu.sh?
Then how many people are two people and two people? Are they three people?
Wang 2 Bu.sh, leangg leangg sh syhg.
No, two and two are four.
Ding 1 Woomen bu.sh syhg ren; woo.men sh san'g ren. Lian nii tzar.men tsair sh syhg ren .ne.
We are not four people; we are three people. Only including you do we become four people.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Pronouns are the skeleton of the dialogue

nii
woo
ta
  • nii = you
  • woo = I / me
  • ta = he / him / she / her / it
  • The lesson keeps reusing them so students learn reference through repetition.

2) Question words stay where the answer goes

sheir
sherm.me
  • Nii sh sheir? literally “You are who?”
  • Jang San sh sherm.me? literally “Jang San is what?”
  • This is powerful for beginners because the question mirrors the answer pattern.

3) sh means “be,” but not always like English

sh
nominal predicate
  • Woo sh Wang Ell. = I am Wang Ell.
  • Ta sh Jang San. = He is Jang San.
  • Later in the lesson, students also see that sh can sometimes be omitted in number statements.

4) ren is a broad human noun

ren
person
people
  • The book glosses it broadly: man, woman, person, human being, people.
  • That means students should not reduce it too quickly to only one English word.
  • This supports the book’s wider point: meaning lives in context.

5) Counting needs a classifier-like unit

ig ren
leangg ren
syhg ren
  • Chinese numerals usually combine with an auxiliary noun before a regular noun.
  • So “four men/people” is not just “four + person,” but a structured phrase.
  • This is one of the earliest signals that Mandarin packages quantity differently from English.

6) There are two “we” forms in the lesson

tzar.men
woo.men
  • tzar.men includes the listener.
  • woo.men excludes the listener.
  • This is the conceptual heart of the “four men” joke at the end of the lesson.

7) Small particles organize conversation

.a
.ia
.ne
  • .a can soften a question or mark a new question.
  • .ne often means “and…?” or “how about…?”
  • These tiny forms help the dialogue move naturally.

8) Logic and correction are part of conversation

Bu!
Bu.sh!
Duey .le
  • The lesson teaches agreement, disagreement, and correction as real speech acts.
  • Students are not just naming people; they are checking whether a statement is true.
  • This makes the lesson interactive and easy to dramatize in class.

Pronunciation attention

High-value sound targets

The book’s exercises highlight specific difficult syllables. For beginners, keep the goal narrow: accurate noticing first, fluent repetition second.

  • Initials: ren, sheir, Jang, sherm, tzar.men, jiig, tsair
  • Finals: keh, woo, syh, sh(yh), ell, lian
  • Have students echo short chunks before full sentences.
  • When students hesitate, let them point to people and count physically. Meaning support helps pronunciation stick.
Inclusive vs. exclusive “we”

Visualizing the trickiest idea in the lesson

This lesson’s most interesting concept is not the number four — it is perspective. When Ding speaks to Wang, sometimes “we” means Ding + Wang, and sometimes it means Ding + other people, but not Wang. That is why the same scene can produce both three and four.

Inclusive “we”

tzar.men = the speaker plus the listener

Example idea: “you and I” or “you and the rest of us.”

Exclusive “we”

woo.men = the speaker’s group, but not the listener

Example idea: “he and I” / “they and I” / “our group, not you.”

Suggested classroom move

Place four volunteers at the front. Let one student be Wang, one be Ding, and two be Jang San and Lii Syh. Have Ding point to himself and Wang and say tzar.men. Then have Ding point to himself plus Jang San and Lii Syh, while looking at Wang, and say woo.men. The idea becomes visible immediately.

Practice

Guided practice tasks

Task A — Build the line

Give students word cards and ask them to assemble:

  • Woo sh Wang Ell.
  • Ta sh Jang San.
  • Nii sh sheir?

Task B — Count the people

Show small stick-figure groups and ask:

  • Jiig ren?
  • Sh san'g ren .bu.sh?

Task C — Correct the statement

Teacher says a false sentence. Students answer with:

  • Bu!
  • Bu.sh!
  • then the corrected line

Task D — “We” sorting

Students sort picture cards into:

  • tzar.men group
  • woo.men group
Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) In this lesson, what does tzar.men mean most specifically?

Correct. In the lesson, tzar.men is the inclusive “we,” meaning the speaker includes the person being spoken to.
Not quite. The key distinction in the lesson is that tzar.men includes the listener, while woo.men does not.

2) Why is Nii sh sheir? important for beginners?

Correct. The lesson explicitly supports the idea that the question word occupies the same slot as the answer: Woo sh Wang Ell. ↔ Nii sh sheir?
Not quite. This line matters because it helps beginners see that Mandarin question words often remain in place, instead of moving to the front like English “who.”
Exit prompt

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

Without looking at the text, students should be able to do this short sequence:

  1. Ask one identity question.
  2. Answer with a name.
  3. Point to two people and say how many people there are.
  4. Use either tzar.men or woo.men correctly in a sentence.
