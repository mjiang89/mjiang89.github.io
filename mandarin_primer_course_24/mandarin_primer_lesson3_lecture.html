Mandarin Primer — Lesson 3

Speaking Chinese

Lesson 3 shifts from naming people and objects to talking about language, nationality, origin, ability, sameness, and difference. It is the first lesson that feels clearly abstract: students are not only identifying things in the room, but also discussing who speaks what language, where people come from, and how two labels can overlap or differ.

The page keeps the book’s original romanization system so it stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Say that someone is Chinese, American, English, or foreign: Jong.gworen, Meei.gworen, Ing.gworen, way.gworen.
  • Talk about language ability with huey “can, know how to.”
  • Use origin expressions like tsorng Meei.gwo lai .de and understand the idea “one who comes from …”
  • Understand that English labels like “English” and “American” do not map neatly one-to-one onto the Chinese discussion of language names.
  • Ask about sameness and difference with iyanq, butorng, and wanchyuan.
  • Notice how the lesson contrasts spoken language Jong.gwo-huah with Jongwen.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 3 is conceptually bigger than it first appears. It teaches language as an idea: nationality, language name, origin, skill, similarity, difference, and naming.

For English-speaking beginners, this lesson is especially valuable because it breaks the habit of assuming every English label has a neat Mandarin equivalent. The dialogue itself explores that mismatch and turns it into a learning opportunity.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Review and bridge
Briefly review Lesson 1 people and Lesson 2 objects, then announce the new leap: now we talk about languages and where people come from.
2
Cast and categories
Introduce country, person, and language labels together: China → Chinese person → Chinese speech.
3
Dialogue in chunks
Read the text in meaningful stretches: who speaks what, who comes from where, what “English” means, what “Jongwen” means.
4
Grammar from use
Pull out huey, dou, A-not-A questions, .ma, jiaw, and .de constructions.

Help students think through why “Englishmen speak English, Americans also speak English” matters in the dialogue.
6
Knowledge graph + quiz
Review the whole system and check whether students can connect person, country, language, and ability.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source pages repeatedly emphasize learning through whole utterances, echoing, repetition, and exercise-based fluency. That matters even more in Lesson 3 because the content is less concrete than earlier lessons.
  1. Use a visual three-column board: country, person, language.
  2. Keep meaning visible with arrows: Jong.gwo → Jong.gworen → Jong.gwo-huah.
  3. Pause often to ask: “Is this a country, a person, or a language?”
  4. When the lesson discusses “English” and “American,” slow down and explain that the point is semantic mismatch, not trivia.
  5. Use repeated frames, not single-word memorization: Ta tsorng Ing.gwo lai, suoo.yii ta sh Ing.gworen.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students may assume this lesson is just about vocabulary. In reality, it is about how Chinese organizes categories and relations.
  • huey means “can” in the sense of knowing how / having the skill, not general permission.
  • A-not-A questions require answers that choose A or not-A, not simple agreement words like “right” or “wrong.”
  • dou is not a simple noun-like “all”; it is an adverb that belongs with the following predicate.
  • .de constructions can mean “the person who comes from America” or “one who comes from America,” depending on context.
  • Jongwen vs. Jong.gwo-huah is presented as nearly the same in this lesson, with a later inference that one is more tied to writing and the other to speech.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 3 as a conceptual conversation

This lesson is best taught as a sequence of thought moves, not as a raw block of text.

A Woo sh Jong.gworen, woo sh ig Jong.gworen.
I am Chinese, I am a Chinese person.
A Woo shuo Jong.gwo-huah, nii shuo Jong.gwo-huah, ta yee shuo Jong.gwo-huah — woo.men gehgehl dou shuo Jong.gwo-huah.
I speak Chinese, you speak Chinese, he also speaks Chinese — every one of us speaks Chinese.
A Nii.men huey shuo Jong.gwo-huah .bu.huey .a?
Can you people speak Chinese?
B Huey, woo.men huey.
Yes, we can.
A Nii sh Jong.gworen .bu.sh?
Are you Chinese?
B Bu.sh, woo sh way.gworen.
No, I am a foreigner.
A Nii sh neei-gwo .de ren .ne? Nii sh tsorng naal lai .de ren .ne?
What country are you from? Where do you come from?
B Woo sh tsorng Meei.gwo lai .de, suoo.yii woo sh Meei.gworen. Ta tsorng Ing.gwo lai, suoo.yii ta sh Ing.gworen.
I come from America, so I am American. He comes from England, so he is English.
A Ing.gworen shuo Ing.gwo-huah, Meei.gworen shuo Meei.gwo-huah, sh .bu.sh?
Englishmen speak English-language and Americans speak American-language, right?
B Bu.sh tzemm shuo … Ing.gworen shuo Ing'wen, Meei.gworen yee shuo Ing'wen.
That’s not how one says it … English people speak English, Americans also speak English.
A Nii.men leang-gwo ren shuo-huah, sh wanchyuan iyanq .de .ma?
Do the people of your two countries talk exactly alike?
B Bu, bu wanchyuan iyanq … jyy yeou ideal butorng .jiow.sh.le.
No, not completely the same … there is only a little difference, that’s all.
B Sherm jiaw 'Jongwen' .a? Jongwen gen Jong.gwo-huah yeou sherm butorng .a?
What is called “Chinese wen”? What difference is there between “Jongwen” and “Chinese speech”?
A Mei sherm butorng … Jongwen jiow.sh Jong.gwo-huah.
There is no real difference … Jongwen is simply Chinese language.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Country, person, and language form a family of meanings

Jong.gwo
Jong.gworen
Jong.gwo-huah
  • The lesson builds a chain: country → person from that country → language associated with that country.
  • This gives students a structured way to expand meaning rather than memorize separate words.

2) huey means “know how to”

ability
skill
  • The notes explain that huey means “can” in the sense of having the skill.
  • That makes it ideal for language ability: huey shuo Jong.gwo-huah.

3) dou belongs with the following predicate

dou
all
  • The notes warn that dou is not really a noun-like “all.”
  • It is an adverb meaning something like “in all cases / uniformly,” and it belongs with the verb that follows.

4) A-not-A questions are choice questions

A-not-A
huey / bu.huey
  • Nii.men huey shuo Jong.gwo-huah .bu.huey .a?
  • The answer should choose the predicate: Huey or bu huey, not simply “right” or “wrong.”

5) tsorng … lai .de creates origin descriptions

from … come
.de
  • The lesson uses tsorng Meei.gwo lai .de = “one who comes from America.”
  • This is an early example of a longer modifier packaged with .de.

6) jiaw links naming and meaning

call
is called
what is meant by
  • Sherm jiaw Ing'wen .a? asks what “English” means or what one calls “English.”
  • This makes the lesson more metalinguistic than Lessons 1 and 2.

7) Same and different are scaled, not binary

iyanq
butorng
wanchyuan
  • The lesson does not simply say “same” or “different.”
  • It says “not completely the same” and “only a little different.”
  • This is a subtle and realistic conversational move.

8) Jongwen and Jong.gwo-huah are treated as near-overlapping labels

spoken
written
label nuance
  • The dialogue first says there is no significant difference.
  • Then one speaker speculates that Jong.gwo-huah is what one speaks and Jongwen is what one writes.
  • This makes a perfect class discussion point: labels can overlap but still feel different in use.

High-value contrast

Why the “English / American” exchange matters

A says: Englishmen speak “English-language,” Americans speak “American-language,” right?

B rejects that framing and says both English people and Americans speak English. This is not just cultural information. It teaches students that language names and nationality labels do not always pattern symmetrically.

That is exactly the kind of misconception beginners often have when they try to build Mandarin by direct analogy from English.

Question system focus

Three different question types in this lesson

A-not-A question

Nii.men huey shuo Jong.gwo-huah .bu.huey .a?

Best answer chooses one side: Huey or bu huey.

.ma question

Nii.men leang-gwo ren shuo-huah, sh wanchyuan iyanq .de .ma?

Calls for agreement or disagreement and tends to lean slightly toward a dissenting response.

Echo / confirmation with .a

The exercises highlight the difference between high-pitched .ma questions and low-pitched repeating/confirming .a questions.

Teaching note

This is an excellent lesson for showing students that “question” is not one single grammatical thing. Mandarin has multiple ways to ask.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Build the chain

Students complete sets like:

  • Jong.gwo → Jong.gworen → Jong.gwo-huah
  • Meei.gwo → Meei.gworen → ?

Task B — Can / can’t

Students ask and answer:

  • Nii huey shuo Jong.gwo-huah .bu.huey?
  • Huey / Bu huey

Task C — Origin descriptions

Turn short facts into fuller descriptions:

  • Ta tsorng Ing.gwo lai.
  • Ta sh tsorng Ing.gwo lai .de.

Task D — Same or different?

Students respond with degrees:

  • iyanq
  • bu wanchyuan iyanq
  • yeou ideal butorng
Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) In this lesson, what is the best meaning of huey in Nii.men huey shuo Jong.gwo-huah .bu.huey??

Correct. The notes explain that huey here means “can” in the sense of knowing how or having the skill to do something.
Not quite. In this lesson, huey is about ability or skill, especially language ability, not permission or obligation.

2) Why is the exchange about Englishmen and Americans important?

Correct. The lesson uses that exchange to show that labels do not always line up symmetrically. That is an important beginner insight about meaning.
Not quite. The key point is not that labels are random, but that nationality and language names do not always map one-to-one.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Say what country you come from in the lesson’s style.
  2. Say whether you can speak Chinese.
  3. Name one country, one people-label, and one language-label.
  4. Use either iyanq or butorng in a sentence.
  5. Explain, in English, the difference between Jongwen and Jong.gwo-huah as the lesson presents it.
