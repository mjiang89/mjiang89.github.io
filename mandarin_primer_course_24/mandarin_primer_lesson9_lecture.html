Mandarin Primer — Lesson 9

A Good Man

Lesson 9 returns to a character-based discussion, but this time the topic is moral judgment. The speakers try to decide whether a man is truly good. The lesson becomes a rich exploration of what counts as goodness: words, actions, intention, ability, helpfulness, courage, disposition, and heart. For beginners, it is especially powerful because it mixes humor with serious reasoning.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so it stays aligned with the scanned lesson. The lesson note on page 3 also signals a formatting shift: from Lesson 9 onward, dots before neutral-tone syllables are mostly omitted in the source text except in special cases.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow a longer evaluative conversation about whether someone should be called a good person.
  • Understand how Mandarin talks about moral traits, helpfulness, disposition, and action.
  • Recognize discourse patterns for correction, hesitation, self-repair, and “what I mean is…”
  • Use structures for concession and contrast such as sweiran ... keesh ..., budann ... erlchiee ..., and jyy yawsh ....
  • Notice grammar points such as V-sh-V concessive forms, emphatic suoo, interrogative-plus-dou, and subject-predicate predicates like ta shin hao.
  • Think critically about the difference between saying good things, doing good deeds, and having a good heart.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 9 teaches evaluation through conversation. Instead of memorizing moral vocabulary in isolation, students watch two speakers build and test a case for why someone is good.

This is excellent for high-school learners because it combines personality, humor, dialect description, and ethical reasoning. It also gives students a natural way to discuss whether intentions, actions, and inner character are the same thing or not.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1

3

How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source’s “To the Student” pages stress utterances, repetition, echoing, and grammar-through-exercises. For Lesson 9, that means students should hear the reasoning as connected speech, not just memorize isolated moral terms.
  1. Use a visible two-column chart: reasons he seems good and reasons that raise doubt.
  2. Teach the joke about names and misnaming first, because it keeps students engaged.
  3. Then slow down when the lesson becomes abstract: “good words,” “good deeds,” “good heart.”
  4. Have students separate external actions from internal character.
  5. Keep the dialect-description section visual by mapping regions or labeling speech as mixed, unclear, southern-toned, northern-accented, etc.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students may think the lesson is simply about “good” vocabulary. In fact, it is also about discourse: correcting oneself, arguing, conceding, qualifying, and building a case.
  • V-sh-V does not mean repetition for emphasis alone; it carries concessive force like “it is good, to be sure, but…”
  • suoo is an emphatic adverb, not just a relative pronoun replacement, even though it often translates like “that which.”
  • ta shin hao is a subject-predicate predicate, not simply “his heart” with an omitted possessive.
  • jyy yawsh means “if only / so long as,” not the same as jyy yeou “only if.”
  • Interrogative-plus-dou like sherm dou bush means “it is nothing in particular,” not a literal question.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 9 as an argument about goodness

This lesson works best when broken into social and logical stages.

Opening joke Woo geei nii jeang ig ren.
I will tell you about a man.
Misfire Sh bush neyg ... Shuo Buwan Shg.?
Is it that Mr. Can’t Finish Speaking?
Correction Woode yihsy sh shuo ... jiaw Tarn Butyng Shg., sh bush'a?
What I mean is … he’s called Mr. Can’t Stop Talking, right?
New topic Jeyg ren a, ta tzoong duey ren shuo, "Farm.sh shyhjieh.shanq de ren a, dou inggai dwu hao-shu, shuo hao-huah, tzuoh hao-ren, shyng hao-shyh."
This man always says: Every person in the world ought to read good books, speak good words, be a good person, and do good deeds.
Problem Ta sweiran sheang tzemm tzuoh, keesh ta mei been.shyh bae yanqyanql dou tzuoh-dawle.
Although he wants to do these things, he does not have the ability to carry all of them out.
Example 1 Ta lian ig dah.tzyh dou bu renn.de, hair tyi sherm dwu hao-shu dwu huay-shu ne?
He cannot even recognize a character like “big,” so what is the point of talking about reading good books or bad books?
Example 2 Ta suoo shuo de huah, ren.jia charngchang ting.bu-dah-doong.
The words he says are often not very understandable to people.
Escalation Budann sh shengren ting.bu-doong ta de huah e, lian ... ta tay.tay a, ta tzyhjii jia.lii de sheauharl a, yonq.ren a, torngshyhmen a ... tamen yee yeou ting.bu-doong ta de shyrhowl.
Not only strangers, but even his wife, children, servants, and colleagues often cannot understand him.
Comic nickname O-h-oh, bush Tarn Butyng Shg., sh Shuo Buching Shg., ar?
Oh, so not Mr. Can’t Stop Talking, but Mr. No Clear Speaking?
Dialect sketch Ta shuo de yee bujydaw sh Beeipyng-huah, yee bujydaw sh Nan.jing-huah ... iball shianq Jonggwo-huah, yow iball shianq waygwo-huah shyhde.
His speech is neither clearly Pekingese nor Nankingese … half like Chinese, half like a foreign language.
Challenge Ta shuo de huah jihran nemm nan doong, neme tzeem jydaw ta sh g hao-ren ne?
If what he says is so hard to understand, how do you know that he is a good man?
Reason 1 Ta jeyg ren a, ta tzuey keen bang ren de mang. Jyy yawsh duey bye.ren yeou hao.chuh de shyh.chyng, ta meiyeou bukeen gann de.
He is most willing to help people. So long as something benefits others, there is nothing he is unwilling to do.
Reason 1, stronger Ta budann neng tzuoh renjia yiiwei nan tzuoh de shyh, binqchiee gaan tzuoh renjia suoo bugaan tzuoh de shyh.
He not only does things others think are hard to do, but also dares to do things others do not dare to do.
Reason 2 Jeyg ren budann ibeytz tzuoh hao-shyh, erlchiee ta shin hao.
This man not only does good deeds all his life, but also has a good heart.
Disposition evidence Bye.ren gaushinq ta yee gaushinq; bye.ren bukuay.hwo ta yee bukuay.hwo ... ren.jia nanshow de shyrhowl ta jiow tzoong chiuh an.wey ren.jia.
When others are happy, he is happy; when they are unhappy, he is unhappy … when they feel bad, he goes to comfort them.
Conclusion Woo shiangshinn jeh ren idinq shin hao. Suooyii shuo ta sh hao-ren a, nahsh jyueduey meiyeou wenntyi de.
I believe this person certainly has a good heart. So to say he is a good man is absolutely no problem.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) “Goodness” is tested, not assumed

good words
good deeds
good heart
  • The lesson begins with a slogan but then asks whether the man can actually do what he says.
  • That makes the lesson more analytic than purely descriptive.

2) Correction and hesitation are part of real conversation

woode yihsy sh shuo
neyg
  • The notes say woode yihsy sh shuo is a regular expression for “I mean” when correcting oneself.
  • neyg is also noted as a common hesitation filler.

3) V-sh-V forms can be concessive

hao sh hao
good, to be sure
  • The notes explain that when a verb or adjective is repeated with sh in the middle, it has concessive force.
  • This is a key pattern for the lesson’s “It is good, all right, but …” reasoning.

4) “What’s the point in…” has its own rhetorical pattern

verb + sherm + object
  • The notes explain that verb + sherm + object can be a rhetorical question meaning “What is the use / point of …?”
  • This is exactly how the text dismisses talk of reading good books when the man cannot recognize even a basic character.

5) suoo gives emphasis inside de-constructions

suoo
the thing that...
  • The notes call suoo an emphatic adverb, though it often translates in relative-clause-like ways.
  • This helps students see that translation and grammar are not always the same thing.

6) The lesson builds a two-reason argument

dih'i-tserng
dih'ell-tserng
  • First reason: he helps people and dares to do difficult things for their benefit.
  • Second reason: he has a good heart and a good disposition.

7) Subject-predicate predicates matter

ta shin hao
  • The notes explicitly explain that ta shin hao is not just an omitted possessive form.
  • It is structured more like “as for him, the heart is good.”

8) Moral inference comes from behavior

gaushinq
bukuay.hwo
an.wey
  • The speaker cannot literally see the man’s heart, so he infers goodness from shared feeling and comforting behavior.
  • This makes the lesson an exercise in reasoning from evidence.

High-value classroom move

Run a “case for goodness” debate

Split the class into two sides: one side argues that the man is clearly good; the other side argues that he only sounds good.
  1. Use the lesson’s own evidence only.
  2. Require at least one Mandarin phrase from the lesson in each claim.
  3. End by asking whether intentions, actions, and inner character should count equally.

This turns the lesson into a structured reasoning activity instead of a passive reading exercise.

A key structural contrast

Good actions vs. good heart

Outer action

tzuoh hao-shyh

Doing helpful things that benefit others.

Inner character

ta shin hao

Having a good heart or kindly disposition.

Why this matters

The lesson insists that doing good deeds and having a good heart are related but not identical.

Teaching payoff

This distinction gives students a natural context for talking about evidence, motive, and moral judgment.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Repair the sentence

Students practice hesitant and corrected speech using: woode yihsy sh shuo ...

Task B — Evaluate the man

Students list evidence for and against calling him a good person.

Task C — Contrast builder

Students turn simple statements into: sweiran ... keesh ... and budann ... erlchiee ... forms.

Task D — Read the heart?

Students explain how the speaker concludes ta shin hao without literally seeing the heart.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to the notes, what does the V-sh-V construction like hao sh hao do?

Correct. The notes explain that V-sh-V can be translated as “to be sure,” “all right,” or a concessive intonation pattern before a contrast.
Not quite. In Lesson 9, V-sh-V is important because it carries concessive force rather than just repetition.

2) What is the best reason the speaker finally gives for believing the man is good-hearted?

Correct. The speaker admits he cannot literally see the man’s heart, but infers goodness from how he rejoices with happy people, feels with unhappy people, and comforts others.
Not quite. The lesson’s final evidence for inner goodness comes from observed disposition and behavior, not from literacy or speech clarity.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Give one reason why the man seems hard to take seriously at first.
  2. Give the speaker’s first reason for calling him a good man.
  3. Give the second reason.
  4. Use one concessive form and one “not only … but also” form from the lesson.
  5. Explain, in English, whether you agree that he is a good person.
