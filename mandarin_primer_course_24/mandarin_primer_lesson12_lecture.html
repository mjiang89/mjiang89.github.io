Mandarin Primer — Lesson 12

A Rescue at Sea

Lesson 12 is one of the most vivid action scenes so far. It begins with sudden danger, moves through rapid visual description, confusion about direction and distance, recognition of a parachute, moral urgency about rescue, and then a tense small-boat operation full of commands, engine trouble, rowing, throwing rescue equipment, balance, pulling, and relief. It is an excellent lesson for showing students that beginner Mandarin can already handle crisis, motion, and teamwork.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so it stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow and retell a fast-moving rescue narrative from airplane crash to successful pickup.
  • Understand the distinction between definite and indefinite reference in patterns like i-jiah feiji versus feiji diaw.shiah.lai le.
  • Recognize directional and location language in a maritime setting: east/south/west/north, port side, right side, above, below, near, far.
  • Use verbs and result forms for seeing, finding, floating, burning, sinking, escaping, throwing, pulling, rowing, and rescuing.
  • Notice command forms, urgency language, afterthought clauses, and practical safety talk.
  • Learn how shape, color, size, and distance are described under uncertain visual conditions.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 12 teaches perception under pressure. Students do not simply describe what they know; they describe what they think they see, lose sight of, recover, and finally act on.

For high-school students, this is especially engaging because it feels cinematic. At the same time, it introduces important beginner grammar: definite vs. indefinite reference, impersonal event expressions, result complements, distance expressions, and command-rich spoken interaction.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Emergency hook
Start with the shout: a plane is falling. Ask students what information people need first in an emergency scene.
2
Visual tracking
Teach the first half as a seeing problem: smoke, fire, sinking, a shape in the sky, losing it, finding it again.
3
Space and direction
Map east/south/west/north, right/left, above/below, near/far, and “the side where the sun is.”
4
Recognition and inference
Show how speakers move from uncertainty to hypothesis: maybe a parachute, maybe the pilot, maybe only one person.
5
Rescue commands
Teach the second half as a command chain: launch, steer carefully, avoid collision, engine trouble, row, throw, pull, rebalance.
6
Retell + quiz
End with students narrating the rescue in stages and checking two key concepts.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source still emphasizes utterances, echoing, repetition, and grammar-through-exercises. Lesson 12 becomes especially effective when students can see the scene with their bodies and hands: point, steer, row, throw, and pull.
  1. Use a board sketch of sea, ship, cloud, parachute, and pilot while reading the dialogue.
  2. Let students physically point to “above,” “right side,” “port side,” and “the side where the sun is.”
  3. Separate the lesson into two modes: seeing and acting.
  4. Keep uncertainty language visible: “I can’t see it,” “I can’t distinguish,” “maybe,” “I guess,” “I wonder.”
  5. Turn the rescue half into a group role-play so commands become memorable spoken chunks.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

This lesson moves quickly. Students may lose the grammar because the story is exciting. Slow down at the points where visual uncertainty and action instructions change form.
  • Definite vs. indefinite reference matters in how nouns are introduced and then tracked.
  • kann vs. kann.jiann and jao vs. jao.jaur are crucial: looking is not the same as actually seeing or finding.
  • Chinese color words do not always line up neatly with English; the notes specifically discuss hwang and ching.
  • Afterthought clauses come after the main clause in forms like chwufei ... only when added later.
  • Command chains can sound dense; students benefit from physically acting them out.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 12 as a crisis-and-rescue sequence

This lesson works best when divided into phases: accident, searching, recognition, rescue approach, engine trouble, manual rescue.

Phase 1 Niimen kuaydeal lai kann, yeou i-jiah feiji diaw.shiah.lai le! Feiji chu-shyh le!
Come quickly and look, an airplane is falling down! There is an airplane accident!
Phase 1 Fadonqji jaur-huoo le ... ta jinq maw-ial ... feiji de shentz yee jaur-huoo le!
The motor has caught fire ... it is sending out smoke ... the body of the plane has caught fire too!
Phase 1 Diaw de hae.liitou le, ideal ideal de chern.shiah.chiuh le.
It has fallen into the sea and is sinking little by little.
Phase 2 Neybial yeou g sherm dongshi tzay bannkong jong piauj ne?
What is that thing floating in the air over there?
Phase 2 Woo yee fen.bu.chu dong nan shi beei lai le ... tzay nanbial, tzay tay.yang ney.bial.
I cannot distinguish east, south, west, and north anymore ... it is on the south side, on the side where the sun is.
Phase 2 Gangx tzay ney-kuay yun.tsae de shanqtou yowbial.
Just above and to the right of that patch of cloud.
Phase 3 Sh tzeemyanql de ig dongshi? Sh fang de sh yuan de? Yeou dwo dah? Sh sherm yan.seh de?
What sort of thing is it? Square or round? How big? What color?
Phase 3 I'wey nah dongshi li jell tay yeuan le, suooyii woo yee fen.bu.chu sh horng liuh hwang ching Ian tzyy hairsh sherm yan.seh lai le.
Because it is too far from here, I cannot distinguish whether it is red, green, yellow, green-blue, blue, purple, or what color.
Phase 3 Gaan.chyng sh geei ney-kuay hei-yun geei daang.juh le. Shianntzay dawle ney-kuay yun.tsae de tzuoobial le.
So it was covered by that black cloud. Now it has reached the left side of the cloud.
Phase 4 Nah bush g jianqluoh-saan ma? Saan diishiah guahj de bush g ren ma?
Isn’t that a parachute? And isn’t there a person hanging under it?
Phase 4 Goan.bao sh de ba? — chwufei feiji.lii beenlai bujyy ig ren.
I bet it is — unless there was more than one person in the plane.
Phase 4 Shinq.kuei ta chenntzao taurle.chulai, yawburan koongpah bush shau.syy yee deei ian.syy le.
Fortunately he escaped in good time; otherwise he would probably either burn to death or drown.
Phase 5 Tzarmen deei maashanq jiow chiuh jiow ta tsair shyng ne!
We must go rescue him at once, or else it will not do.
Phase 5 Daa jeybial tzoou — Sheaushin! Mannjdeal! Kawj dah-chwan de tzuoobial tzoou! Bye penq de duoh.shanq chiuh!
Go this way — look out! Slower! Go by the port side of the big ship! Don’t bump into the rudder!
Phase 6 Dao-mei, jeh huenn.janq de ji.chih, pianpial tiau tzemm g shyrhowl lai gen nii dao-luann.
What bad luck — this stupid engine has to pick exactly this time to make trouble.
Phase 6 Na jeang chulai la! Kuaydeal hwa, hwa kuaydeal! Shyy-jinn, shyy-jinn hwa!
Get the oars out! Hurry up and row, row faster! Use your strength, row hard!
Phase 6 Bae jiowsheng-chiuan rheng geei ta! ... Bushyng, nii rheng de li ta tay yeuan le.
Throw him the life-belt! ... That won’t do, you threw it too far from him.
Phase 7 Lha.juh! Bye sa-shoou! Deeng woom bae nii lha-shanq chwan.lai.
Hold on! Don’t let go! Let us pull you up into the boat.
Phase 7 Jeh sheauchwal tay ching-hai ... nii na shentz yah neybial ideal. Bye donq! Ranq woo jiee jeybial bae ta lha.shang.lai.
The little boat is too light ... put your weight over on that side. Don’t move! Let me pull him up from this side.
Phase 7 Kuaydeal hwa-hwei dah-chwan chiuh jiow hao le. (Du! Du!)
Hurry up and row back to the big ship and everything will be fine. (Whistle.)

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Definite and indefinite reference shape the sentence

i-jiah feiji
feiji
  • The notes explain a strong tendency: indefinite nouns often appear as objects, while definite nouns tend to appear as subjects or after pretransitives.
  • This helps explain why the lesson introduces “an airplane” and then immediately tracks “the airplane.”

2) The lesson distinguishes looking, seeing, searching, and finding

kann
kann.jiann
jao
jao.jaur
  • The notes explicitly compare kann with kann.jiann and jao with jao.jaur.
  • This is essential in a lesson where perception changes every few lines.

3) Distance changes what can be described

li jell tay yeuan
jinn.deal
  • The speakers cannot identify color or shape clearly because the object is too far away.
  • Later, when it comes nearer, their description becomes more certain.

4) Direction words become practical, not just lexical

dong nan shi beei
tayyang ney.bial
yowbial
tzuoobial
  • The lesson shows direction under stress: the speakers must orient themselves quickly at sea.
  • The notes also explain stress-based meaning differences in expressions like tayyang ney.bial.

5) Color terms do not map one-to-one to English

hwang
ching
  • The notes explain that hwang may cover what English calls brown in some contexts, and that ching spans a wide range of greens and blues.
  • This is a strong reminder not to translate too mechanically.

6) Rescue talk is command-heavy and embodied

sheaushin
mannjdeal
kuaydeal hwa
lha.juh
  • The second half of the lesson is an excellent command lesson.
  • Because the commands are meaningful and urgent, they are easier for students to remember.

7) Passive meaning and result meaning matter in rescue scenes

geei daang.juh
lha.shang.lai
rheng dawle
  • The notes emphasize passive meaning and result realization.
  • In this lesson, what matters is not just trying to rescue, but actually reaching, throwing, holding, and pulling successfully.

8) The lesson is organized by uncertainty becoming action

bujydaw
tsair shyng
yeou dawlii
  • First the speakers do not know what they are seeing.
  • Then they infer that it is a parachute and a man.
  • Then they decide that only immediate rescue will do.

High-value classroom move

Turn the rescue into a live simulation

Put one student at the board as the spotter, one as the pilot in the water, and several as the rescue crew.
  1. The spotter narrates what is seen and where it is.
  2. The crew must respond with correct commands.
  3. One student “throws” rope or life-belt, another “pulls,” and another rebalances the boat.

This lets the lesson’s command chains and result language become memorable spoken actions.

A key structural system

Seeing versus actually seeing

Looking

kann

Direct attention visually, but not necessarily with success.

Actually seeing

kann.jiann

The thing has come into view or has been perceived successfully.

Looking for

jao

Search action without guaranteed success.

Actually finding

jao.jaur

Search reaches a result.

Lesson 12 is one of the clearest places in the early chapters to teach how Chinese marks the difference between activity and attained result.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Track the object

Students narrate what happens to the plane, smoke, cloud-covered object, parachute, and pilot in order.

Task B — Direction lab

Students point and say: east, south, west, north, right side, left side, above, below, near, far.

Task C — Rescue commands

Students practice a command chain: sheaushin → mannjdeal → kuaydeal hwa → rheng geei ta → lha.juh.

Task D — Definite or indefinite?

Students compare sentences introducing a new object with sentences tracking an already identified one.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to the notes, what is one major reason the lesson says the speaker cannot identify the object’s color clearly at first?

Correct. The dialogue explicitly says the object is too far from here, so the speaker cannot distinguish its color clearly.
Not quite. The lesson emphasizes distance as the reason for uncertain color identification.

2) What is the best explanation of the difference between kann and kann.jiann in the notes?

Correct. The notes explicitly contrast the plain action verb with the result form that means the object has actually come into view.
Not quite. Lesson 12 is a very clear example of action versus attained-result meaning.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Say what first happens to the airplane.
  2. Explain how the speakers finally identify the object in the sky.
  3. Give two commands from the rescue scene.
  4. Explain, in English, why the rescue becomes harder halfway through.
  5. State what happens at the end of the rescue.
