Mandarin Primer — Lesson 2

Things

Lesson 2 expands beginner Mandarin from talking about people to talking about objects, location, possession, quantity, and classification. The lesson looks simple on the surface — “What is this?” “What is that?” — but underneath it teaches how Mandarin organizes reference, classifiers, “there is / there are,” location expressions, and noun modification.

The page keeps the book’s original romanization system rather than converting it to pinyin, so students can stay aligned with the source text.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Ask and answer object questions such as Jeh.sh sherm.me? and Nah.sh sherm .ne?
  • Use demonstratives and determinative forms: jeh / jeyg, nah / neyg, jey.shie, ney.shie.
  • Recognize that Mandarin uses different auxiliary nouns / classifier-like forms for different kinds of objects, such as jang, jiann, jy, shann, baa, shuang.
  • Understand basic location and existence patterns with tzay, jell, nall, naal, and yeou.
  • Differentiate possession and existence: “have” versus “there is / there are.”
  • Notice how Mandarin builds meaning through phrases such as bau dong.shi .de jyy (“paper for wrapping things”).
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 2 is the first object world. Students move from identifying people in Lesson 1 to identifying things, locating them, counting them, owning them, and reclassifying them when initial guesses are wrong.

That means this lesson is excellent for teaching beginners how Mandarin turns perception into language: see something → point to it → identify it → count it → locate it → describe what kind it is.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Warm-up with real objects
Place a table, chair, pencil, paper, and lamp images on screen or board. Start with pointing, not grammar terminology.
2
Dialogue in visible space
Read the opening lines while pointing: this / that / these / those. Let students echo short chunks.
3
Classifier noticing
Group objects by shape and type: tables, doors, stick-like things, sheets, pairs. Explain that Mandarin packages nouns differently from English.
4
Location and possession
Introduce tzay, jell, nall, naal, and yeou through classroom space.

Show how the lesson becomes a mini mystery: pencils, paper, lamps, newspaper, wrapping paper, chopsticks.
6
Knowledge graph + quiz
End with connected review and beginner checks on demonstratives, classifiers, and location structure.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source text repeatedly argues that students learn best from whole utterances, repetition, echoing, and exercise-based fluency, not from memorizing isolated word lists.
  1. Use real or pictured objects wherever possible. This lesson is highly visual.
  2. Teach question-answer pairs together: Jeh.sh sherm.me? ↔ Jeh.sh i-jang juotz.
  3. Let students point before they speak. Physical anchoring reduces beginner overload.
  4. Keep English support strong, but return often to repeated Mandarin sentence frames.
  5. Use correction as part of the meaning game: “newspaper?” “No — wrapping paper.”
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

English often lets students say “this,” “that,” “here,” “there,” and plural nouns very freely. Lesson 2 shows that Mandarin organizes these ideas more structurally.
  • Demonstratives often want an auxiliary noun / classifier-like form: jeyg, neyg.
  • “These” is not simply a plural of “this”: jey.shie is an indefinite quantity form, not just a plural marker.
  • tzay does not equal English “is”: in Maubii tzay jell, the structure is more like “The brushes are at this place.”
  • yeou does two jobs: “have” and impersonal “there is / there are.”
  • Object types matter: the lesson uses different auxiliary nouns depending on shape and semantic class.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 2 as a learning story

The lesson begins with simple identification, then expands into counting, possession, location, paper, lamps, and a final surprise about chopsticks.

A Jeh.sh sherm.me? Jeyg sh sherm.me?
What is this? What is this one?
B Jeyg .a?
This one, you mean?
A .Eng, jeyg.
Uh-huh, this one.
B Jeh.sh i-jang juotz. Juotz sh i-jiann dong.shi.
This is a table. A table is a thing.
A Nah.sh sherm .ne? Neyg sh sherm.me?
And what is that? What is that one?
B Nah.sh i-shann men. Men yee.sh i-jiann dong.shi.
That is a door. The door is also a thing.
A Jey.shieg sh sherm.me?
What are these?
B Jeh.sh bii. Jeh.sh jii-jy bii.
These are writing instruments. These are a few writing instruments.
A Woo sheang sh chianbii .ba?
I think they are pencils, aren’t they?
B Bush .ba? Woo sheang sh maubii. Ney.shieg sh chianbii.
Probably not. I think they are writing brushes. Those are pencils.
A Chianbii tzay nii.nall .bu .tzay?
Are the pencils there with you?
Wang Tzay.jell, tzay woo.jell. Woo jyy yeou liow-jy.
Yes, they are here with me. I only have six.
A Woo sheang sh bawjyy .ba?
I think it is newspaper, right?
B Jeh bu.sh bawjyy; jeh.sh bau dong.shi .de jyy!
This is not newspaper; this is wrapping paper!
B Nah.sh jii-shuang kuaytz!
Those are a few pairs of chopsticks!

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Objects enter the conversation through pointing

jeh
nah
jeyg
neyg
  • The lesson starts from pointing rather than abstract vocabulary.
  • jeh and nah can sometimes go directly with nouns or verbs, but forms like jeyg and neyg package “this one / that one.”
  • This is a good place to teach perception-based language.

2) Determinatives are a major organizing idea

demonstrative
interrogative
numeral
  • The notes group demonstratives, interrogatives like “which,” and numerals together as determinatives.
  • These often combine with an auxiliary noun / classifier-like form before a noun is added.
  • This helps students see Mandarin noun phrases as structured packages.

3) Object type matters for counting

jang
jiann
jy
shann
baa
shuang
  • jang for tables and sheets, shann for doors, jy for stick-like things, baa for handled things, shuang for pairs.
  • This is one of the earliest lessons in how Mandarin classifies the physical world.

4) shie means an indefinite quantity

jey.shie
ney.shie
  • The notes explain that shie is a measure word for “some / a number of / an amount of.”
  • So “these” is not just English-style plural marking.
  • That is a crucial beginner insight.

5) tzay expresses location, not simple English “is”

tzay
jell
nall
naal
  • Maubii tzay jell. = The brushes are here, but structurally more like “The brushes are at this place.”
  • The lesson warns students not to produce false sentences such as treating jell as if it were a plain adjective complement.
  • This is a great moment for careful beginner contrast with English.

6) yeou means both possession and existence

have
there is
mei.yeou
  • Nii yeou jii-jy chianbii? = How many pencils do you have?
  • Jell yeou deng .mei.yeou? = Is there a lamp here?
  • When no subject is attached, the meaning becomes “there is / there are.”

7) The negative of “have” is special

mei.yeou
mei
  • The lesson explicitly notes that the negative of yeou is mei.yeou, not a simple bu pattern.
  • This is a high-value pattern for beginner fluency.

8) .de builds modification before the noun

woo.de
bau dong.shi .de jyy
baw.shanq .de shinwen
  • The modifier comes before the noun in Chinese.
  • This allows long noun phrases like “paper for wrapping things.”
  • Lesson 2 quietly introduces students to a central structural habit of Mandarin.

Classroom demonstration

Use space to teach structure

  1. Place one object near you and one farther away.
  2. Ask students: Jeh.sh sherm.me? then Nah.sh sherm .ne?
  3. Move pencils to a student and ask: Chianbii tzay nii.nall .bu .tzay?
  4. Ask for quantity: Nii yeou jii-jy chianbii?
  5. Then shift to place without owner: Jell yeou deng .mei.yeou?

This turns grammar into a visible event, which is especially effective for high-school beginners.

A key structural contrast

“Here” is not just an English-style slot

The notes explicitly warn learners not to create false sentences like treating jell as if it were simply “here” after “be.”

In English, students may think: The brushes are here.
so Mandarin must be: *Maubii sh jell

But the lesson says the real structure is: Maubii tzay jell. That means the idea is location: “The brushes are at this place.”

Why this matters

This is a classic beginner moment. If students learn too quickly that jell = here, they will misbuild the rest of the sentence. So this lesson is excellent for teaching that translation shortcuts are useful only after the structure is understood.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Point and name

Students point to objects and answer:

  • Jeh.sh sherm.me?
  • Nah.sh sherm.me?

Task B — Sort by classifier

Give objects or pictures and ask students to group them under:

  • jang
  • jy
  • shuang
  • baa

Task C — Have vs. there is

Convert one idea two ways:

  • Nii yeou jii-jy chianbii?
  • Jell yeou jii-jy chianbii?

Task D — Mystery correction

Students guess an object, then revise it:

  • newspaper → wrapping paper
  • pencils → brushes
  • brushes/pencils/pens/chalks → chopsticks

Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Point to one near object and one far object.
  2. Ask what each one is.
  3. State where one object is.
  4. Ask how many pencils someone has.
  5. Produce one noun phrase with .de, such as “wrapping paper.”

A successful beginner should be able to move through object identification, quantity, and location in one short mini-dialogue.
