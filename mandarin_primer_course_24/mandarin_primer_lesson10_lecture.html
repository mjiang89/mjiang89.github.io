Mandarin Primer — Lesson 10

The Tailless Rat

Lesson 10 is a lively fable about indecision. A rat cannot make up his mind about anything, keeps talking himself out of every decision, and only becomes decisive after disaster literally cuts off his tail. The lesson is ideal for teaching narrative sequencing, weather expressions, hesitation, consequence, passives, and the many uses of .le in story progression.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so it stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow and retell a longer fable with repeated indecision, a storm, collapse, injury, and transformation.
  • Recognize patterns for hesitation, uncertainty, and failure to decide, especially daa.bu-dinq jwu.yih.
  • Understand impersonal weather expressions such as shiah-yeu le and how they differ from English subject-based weather sentences.
  • Use and understand narrative sequencing with gang, jiow, huran, tsorng tsyy yiihow, and repeated .le.
  • Notice result and consequence patterns such as yah-jaur, duann le, and transformation with biann-cherngle.
  • See how one story can teach grammar of weather, commands, passives, complements, and new situations all at once.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 10 teaches consequence through narrative. The rat’s indecision is funny at first, then dangerous, and finally transformative.

This makes the lesson highly teachable for high-school students: it has clear scenes, repeated comic structure, strong visual imagery, and a memorable moral shift. It also gives a natural context for teaching one of the book’s most important grammar topics: the many functions of .le.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Hook: indecision
Ask students whether they know someone who can never decide anything. Connect that personality trait to the rat before reading.
2
Storyboard the fable
Break the lesson into scenes: everyday indecision, weather example, stroll example, children example, midnight storm, refusal to leave, collapse, tailless transformation.
3
Weather and impersonal forms
Teach shiah-yeu, gua-feng, tian lianq as weather-event expressions rather than English-style subject clauses.
4
Narrative .le
Use the notes’ summary of .le to show new situation, completed action, command in response to a new situation, and consequential shift.
5
Result and passive meaning
Highlight how the tail is left inside, crushed, cut off, and how the rat becomes changed.
6
Retell + quiz
End with students retelling the rat’s indecision cycle and testing two key grammar/story concepts.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source pages still emphasize utterances, echoing, repetition, and grammar-through-exercises. Lesson 10 becomes especially effective when students hear the same indecisive pattern repeated across several situations before the storm scene changes the stakes.
  1. Teach the rat first as a personality type: always uncertain, always revising, always delaying.
  2. Use a repeated sentence frame board for “maybe yes / maybe no / let’s wait / why hurry?”
  3. Then switch to a high-energy storm scene so students feel the shift from comedy to danger.
  4. Use arrows and icons to track body vs. tail when the house collapses.
  5. Make the ending explicit: loss leads to change, and the tail becomes the story’s moral turning point.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students may focus only on the funny story and miss the amount of grammar packed into it. The storm scene especially needs slow, visible unpacking.
  • Impersonal weather expressions like shiah-yeu le do not need an English-style dummy subject.
  • derle ba is idiomatic and means something like “let’s call it a day / forget it / I’m going to change my mind.”
  • .le is not just one meaning; the notes summarize several distinct functions in this lesson.
  • Passive meaning can appear with no overt marker, though bey and geei can make it more explicit.
  • Result complements like yah-jaur express actually reaching the result, not merely trying.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 10 as a fable in scenes

This lesson is best taught as repeated indecision followed by one irreversible consequence.

Scene 1 Tsorngchyan yeou g hawtz, ta sherm shyhchyng dou daa.bu-dinq jwu.yih.
Once upon a time there was a rat who could never make up his mind about anything.
Scene 1 Sweibiann nii wenn ta sherm huah, ta tsornglai meiyeou idinq de huah hweidar nii de.
No matter what you asked him, he never gave a definite answer.
Scene 2 Jintian tian.chih butsuoh, keesh yee sheu butzeem hao ... woo yee bujydaw jey-tzoong tial dawdii suann hao suann huay le.
The weather is not bad, but perhaps not very good either ... I really do not know whether this kind of day counts as good or bad after all.
Scene 3 Woo shiahwuu mei gong.ful, shanqwuu dawsh yeou konq — eh bush vx, woo sh yaw shuo shanq.bann.tian mang, shiah.bann.tian mei shell ...
I have no time in the afternoon, though I do in the morning — no, no, what I mean is I’m busy in the morning and free in the afternoon ...
Scene 3 Tzarmen diing hao hairsh deeng chy-guohle wuufann tzay daa-dinq jwu.yih ba ... herbih lihkeh jiow jyuedinq ne? Gannma tzemm jau-jyi a?
We had better wait until after lunch before deciding ... why decide immediately? Why such a hurry?
Scene 4 Woo sheang woo yeou chig erltz bag neu.erl ... meijoel sh bag erltz chig neu.erl ba? ... woo tsai woo jyhshao yeou ellshyr.laig sheauharl ne.
I think I have seven sons and eight daughters ... or maybe eight sons and seven daughters? ... anyway I suppose I have at least twenty-odd children.
Scene 5 Yeou i-tian bannyeh, dah-feng dah-yeu; bae jey-wey Lao Shuu Shg. juh de ney-suool poh-farngtz dou kuay chuei-ta le.
One midnight there was great wind and great rain; the dilapidated house Mr. Rat lived in was on the point of being blown down.
Scene 5 Kuay.deal pao ba, bye shuey de nall l'a! Shiing le, hei, chiilai l'a!
Hurry up and run! Don’t lie there sleeping! Wake up, hey, get up!
Scene 6 Jey.hoel tian hair mei lianq, gannma tzemm tzao jiow chiilai a? ... butsuoh, dyichiueh sh shiah-yeu ne.
It is not even light yet; why get up so early? ... yes, it really is raining.
Scene 6 Jeh farngtz hair haoliaulde me! Ta binq mei dao a! Woo tzayjell daile tzemm jeou, jeh farngtz tsornglai yee mei dao.guoh ia.
This house is perfectly fine! It has not collapsed. I have been here so long and it has never collapsed before.
Scene 7 Feng yueh gua yueh lih.hay, yeu yueh shiah yueh dah.
The wind blew harder and harder, and the rain fell heavier and heavier.
Scene 7 Farngtz buhuey dao ... keesh ta sheangle sheang yow shuo, "Aiia, jeh farngtz yau de tzemm lih.hay, meijoel jen huey dao ha? Derle ba, woo yee tzoou le."
The house would not collapse ... but after thinking again he said, “Oh dear, this house is shaking so badly, maybe it really will collapse. Forget it, I’m going too.”
Scene 8 Gang tzoou-chule menkooul, ta yow shuo, "Tzemm dah de yeu! Hairsh bye tzoou derle."
Just as he walked out the doorway, he said again, “Such heavy rain! I’d better not go after all.”
Scene 8 Huah hair mei shuo-wan, hurande konglonglong! i-sheng, i-dah-suoo farngtz jeenggehlde ta le.
Before he finished speaking, suddenly — crash! — the whole big house collapsed.
Scene 9 Ta gang i tzoou-chule dahmen, nah farngtz jiow ta.shiahlai le. Shinq.kuei ta shentz yiijinq dawle waytou, suooyii dawdii mei yah-jaur.
He had just gone out the front door when the house collapsed. Luckily his body had already reached outside, so he was not crushed after all.
Scene 9 Keesh ta jann de menkooul de shyrhowl bae i-tyau yii.ba geei lah de utz liitou le ... suooyii bey i-gen dao.shiahlai de dah-juhtz geei tzar-duann le.
But while standing in the doorway he had left his tail inside the room, so it was cut off by a pillar that had fallen down.
Scene 10 Tsorng tsyy yiihow, ta jiow biann-cherngle i-jy wuwoei-shuu le ... bu tzay shianq yiichyan nemm mei jwu.yih le.
From that time on he became a tailless rat and was no longer as indecisive as before.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Indecision is built as a repeated discourse pattern

daa.bu-dinq jwu.yih
sweibiann
meiyeou idinq
  • The rat does not merely hesitate once. The entire lesson is built on repeated wavering.
  • This repetition is what makes the story funny before it becomes dangerous.

2) Weather can be expressed impersonally

shiah-yeu
gua-feng
tian lianq
  • The notes explain that Chinese weather remarks may have no subject at all.
  • Forms like shiah-yeu le and gua-feng le are verb-object expressions meaning “it is raining” and “the wind is blowing.”

3) “Had better” is not a literal modal translation

diing hao
diing hao hairsh
  • The notes say the usual spoken expression for “had better” is diing hao or diing hao hairsh.
  • This is a useful translation warning for English-speaking learners.

4) Result complements matter

yah-jaur
shuey-jaur
  • The notes explain -jaur as indicating that the action actually reaches the result.
  • This matters in the contrast between almost being crushed and not actually being crushed.

5) Passive meaning can be explicit or implicit

bey
geei
  • The tail-cutting part of the story is a great place to teach passive interpretation.
  • The notes explain that passive meaning may appear without a marker, but bey and geei can make the agent more explicit.

6) The story is also a lesson on .le

new situation
completed action
command
  • The notes give a full summary of the different uses of .le in the lesson.
  • That makes Lesson 10 one of the best early places to teach .le systematically.

7) One event can create a lasting new identity

biann-cherngle
wuwoei-shuu
  • The rat does not simply lose a tail. He becomes a new kind of rat: a tailless rat.
  • The story uses physical transformation to mark character change as well.

8) Consequence reshapes character

bu tzay
yiichyan
  • The ending is explicitly moral: after losing the tail, he is no longer as indecisive as before.
  • This gives the fable a clear teaching payoff for discussion.

High-value classroom move

Use a cause-and-effect storyboard

Put the rat’s decisions on one side and the consequences on the other.
  1. He delays.
  2. Friends warn him.
  3. He doubts the danger.
  4. He changes his mind again.
  5. The house collapses.
  6. The body escapes but the tail does not.
  7. He changes as a person afterward.

This helps students see the lesson as a logic chain, not just a funny episode.

A key structural focus

The many uses of .le in Lesson 10

The notes on the last page summarize several distinct uses of .le, and this lesson is an ideal place to teach them together.

New situation

Tzau le, shiah-yeu le!

A new situation is noticed: now it is raining.

Command in response

Shiing le, hei!

The new situation prompts an urgent command.

Completed action in narration

Tamen dou shiah-shiing le.

This moves the plot forward.

Transformation / resulting state

Ta biann-cherngle i-jy wuwoei-shuu le.

The completed change also creates a new state.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Indecision chain

Students list three moments where the rat changes his mind again.

Task B — Weather retell

Students describe the storm using dah-feng dah-yeu, shiah-yeu, and tian lianq.

Task C — What did .le do here?

Students label whether a sentence’s .le signals new situation, completed action, command-response, or transformed state.

Task D — Moral retelling

Students explain in English or simple Mandarin how losing the tail changed the rat’s character.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to the notes, what is special about expressions like shiah-yeu le in this lesson?

Correct. The notes explicitly explain that Chinese weather expressions like shiah-yeu le can be verb-object sentences with no subject at all.
Not quite. Lesson 10 specifically highlights impersonal V-O constructions for weather and similar phenomena.

2) What is the best explanation of why the rat survives but loses his tail?

Correct. The story is very precise: his body reaches outside, so he is not crushed, but his tail remains inside and gets cut off by the fallen pillar.
Not quite. The lesson’s ending depends on the split between body outside and tail inside at the instant the house collapses.
