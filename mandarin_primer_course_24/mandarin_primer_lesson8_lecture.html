Mandarin Primer — Lesson 8

Antonyms

Lesson 8 is the first openly conceptual lesson in the book. Instead of telling a story about a single event or person, it develops an idea: the world contains pairs. Some pairs are true opposites, some are softer “not” forms, and some are not opposites at all but correlatives or counterparts. This lesson is ideal for high-school students because it trains both language and reasoning at the same time.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so it stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Understand the difference between antonyms and correlatives / counterparts.
  • Recognize several ways Mandarin expresses an opposite: true opposite words, “not X,” and relational pairings.
  • Use and understand key conceptual vocabulary such as jenq, faan, dueymiall, shiangduey, and shiangfaan.
  • See that some English opposites do not map neatly onto Chinese in the same way, especially with words like “good,” “easy,” “hard,” “interesting,” and “understand.”
  • Notice how modality and permission are built into examples like kee.yii, bukee.yii, busheu, keen, and gaan.
  • Connect the lesson’s philosophical framing to practical beginner language use.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 8 teaches category thinking. Students are no longer only learning how to say things; they are learning how Mandarin organizes concepts into pairs and relations.

For English-speaking beginners, this is powerful because it prevents oversimplified one-word translation habits. The lesson repeatedly shows that “opposite” is not always one fixed type of relation.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Concept hook
Start with pairs students already know: hot/cold, buy/sell, here/there, you/I. Then ask whether all pairs are the same kind of opposite.
2
Text as argument
Teach the lesson as a discussion or mini-philosophical essay, not as a story. The logic matters.
3
Three pairing types
Separate true antonyms, not-X forms, and correlatives with clear examples.
4
Grammar from meaning
Focus on object-subject constructions, necessity with deei, willingness keen, daring gaan, and sentence-final .me.
5
Correlative network
Use a graph to connect man/woman, father/mother, son/daughter, here/there, I/you, speak/listen, write/read.
6
Reasoning + quiz
Have students classify pairs and explain why they belong in one category or another.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source pages still emphasize utterances, echoing, repetition, and learning grammar through exercises. For Lesson 8, that means students should repeatedly say and sort complete paired expressions, not just memorize isolated vocabulary.
  1. Use a three-column board: true opposite, not-X form, correlative / counterpart.
  2. Ask students to justify why a pair belongs in one column and not another.
  3. Keep the lesson grounded in familiar examples from earlier lessons: come/go, sleep/wake, here/there, you/I, speak/listen.
  4. Pause whenever the lesson moves from philosophy to practical speech, especially in the smoking-permission and willingness/daring examples.
  5. Use quick classification games to prevent the lesson from becoming too abstract.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students may think every opposite is simply one positive word and one different negative word. Lesson 8 shows that Chinese often builds these relations in different ways.
  • bu can be enough: sometimes the opposite is just “not X,” not a different lexical item.
  • Correlatives are not true opposites: father/mother or here/there are paired, but not opposites in the same way as true/false.
  • hao and nan can combine with following verbs in ways English handles differently.
  • kee.yii, busheu, keen, and gaan carry subtle meanings of permission, prohibition, willingness, and daring.
  • Sentence-final .me is not the same as ordinary question .ma; it carries impatience or “Can’t you see?” force.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 8 as a conceptual discussion

Teach this lesson as a sequence of claims, examples, and refinements.

Claim Tianshiah .de shyh.chyng, tian-dii.shiah .de dong.shi, mei.yeou i-yanql mei.yeou jenq-faan leang-miall .de.
Of the affairs and things in the world, there is not one that does not have two sides, a right and a reverse.
Example Lai .de faanmiall sh chiuh, mae .de faanmiall sh may, jen .de faanmiall sh jea, hao .de faanmiall sh huay.
The opposite of come is go, of buy is sell, of true is false, of good is bad.
Example Bu shin jiow.sh jiow, bu charng jiow.sh doan, bu leeng jiow jiaw reh, bu yinq jiow jiaw roan.
Not new is old, not long is short, not cold is hot, not hard is soft.
Explanation Yaw.sh i-jiann shyh.chyng bu hao tzuoh, jiow shuo jeh shyh.chyng heen nan … yaw.sh hao tzuoh .de .ne, jiow shuo jeh shyh.chyng heen rong.yih.
If a thing is not easy to do, we say it is difficult; if it is easy to do, we say it is easy.
Shift Yeou shyr.howl shuo faanmiall .de shyh, bu idinq deei yonq faanmiall .de tzyh, jia ig 'bu'-tzell jiow shyng .le.
Sometimes to speak of the opposite, you do not necessarily need a separate opposite word; just adding “not” is enough.
Example Shii.huan .de faanmiall sh henn; shuo-ching.deal jiow shuo bu shii.huan.
The opposite of like is hate, but more lightly one can just say “don’t like.”
Example Ting.de-chu yih.sy .lai jiaw ting.de-doong, ting.bu-chu yih.sy .lai jiaw ting.bu-doong.
To hear and get the meaning is to understand; not to hear the meaning is not to understand.
Application Yaw.sh woo sheu .nii chou-ian, woo jiow shuo 'Jell kee.yii chou-ian'; yaw.sh woo bu ranq .nii chou .ne, woo jiow shuo 'Bukee.yii chou-ian!'
If I allow you to smoke, I say “You may smoke here”; if not, I say “You may not smoke here!”
Application Nii keen geei .woo tzuoh jey-jiann shyh .bu.keen? … Nii gaan shanq nall chiuh .bu.gaan?
Are you willing to do this for me? … Do you dare go there or not?
Refinement Hair yeou haoshie leang-fangmiann .de shyh.chyng, kann.chii.lai hao.shianq sh jenq-faan leang-miall, chyishyr buguoh sh shiangduey .de, binq bu.sh shiangfaan .de.
There are many two-sided things which seem like opposites, but are really correlatives or counterparts, not true opposites.
Examples Nan.ren .de dueymiall sh neu.ren … jell .de dueymiall sh nail, nii .de dueymiall sh woo.
The counterpart of man is woman … the counterpart of here is there, and of you is I.
Closure Yaw.sh nii shianntzay Jongwen yee buhuey shuo, yee buhuey ting, yee buhuey shiee, yee buhuey kann, nah jiow.sh nii.de Jongwen hair mei shyue.de-huey.
If you still cannot speak, hear, write, or read Chinese, then you have not yet learned your Chinese.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Not all pairs are the same kind of pair

jenq
faan
dueymiall
  • The lesson begins by claiming the world has two sides, but then refines what that means.
  • Some pairs are true opposites; others are better understood as counterparts or correlatives.

2) Some opposites are lexical opposites

lai/chiuh
mae/may
jen/jea
hao/huay
  • These are strong, clear lexical pairs.
  • They are useful for teaching what a prototypical antonym looks like in the lesson.

3) Some “opposites” are really built with bu

bu shii.huan
buhao
ting.bu-doong
  • The lesson explicitly says a separate opposite word is not always needed.
  • This is a very important beginner insight: Mandarin may soften the contrast into “not X.”

4) Some words have paired meanings with verbs

haochy
nanchy
haokann
nanting
  • The notes explain that hao and nan commonly combine with following verbs.
  • These combinations can mean either “good / bad in quality” or “easy / difficult to do,” depending on the word and context.

5) Permission, prohibition, willingness, and daring are paired too

kee.yii
bukee.yii
keen
gaan
  • The smoking example turns abstract opposites into practical interaction.
  • Later examples with keen and gaan show how willingness and daring can be inferred from what someone says.

6) Correlatives are relational, not antonymic

man/woman
father/mother
you/I
here/there
  • The lesson warns that some pairs only look like opposites.
  • They are really counterparts defined by relation, not by true contradiction.

7) Object-subject structure appears again

mei.yeou i-yanql mei.yeou ...
  • The notes say mei.yeou i-yanql mei.yeou ... is an object-subject construction.
  • This shows how even a philosophical claim in the lesson is built with earlier grammar patterns.

8) The ending turns the philosophy back on the learner

speak/listen/write/read
learned/not yet learned
  • The lesson ends by pairing the four language skills and saying that true mastery means all four can be done well.
  • This is a powerful pedagogical close because it connects the concept lesson back to the student’s own learning.

High-value classroom move

Run a pair-sorting challenge

Give students cards with pairs from Lessons 1–8 and ask them to sort them into categories.
  1. True opposite
  2. “Not-X” contrast
  3. Correlative/counterpart

This activity directly matches the lesson’s logic and turns abstract conceptual distinctions into an active reasoning task.

A key structural distinction

Antonyms vs. correlatives

Antonyms

jen / jea, hao / huay, lai / chiuh

These are treated as genuine opposite meanings.

Correlatives / counterparts

nan.ren / neu.ren, fuh.chin / muu.chin, nii / woo, jell / nail

These are paired by relation, not by contradiction.

Teaching point

This distinction helps students avoid flattening every pair into the same kind of semantic relation.

Why it matters

The lesson is quietly teaching students how to think about meaning, not only how to say vocabulary items.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Sort the pairs

Students classify cards into antonyms, not-X forms, or correlatives.

Task B — Build softer contrasts

Students rewrite harsh opposites into softer forms, like henn → bu shii.huan.

Task C — Permission and refusal

Role-play asking permission and refusing it: Jell kee.yii .bu.kee.yii ... ?

Task D — Willing or afraid?

Students answer questions with keen/bukeen and gaan/bugaan, then explain why.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to Lesson 8, which of the following is the best example of a correlative rather than a true antonym?

Correct. Lesson 8 specifically treats pairs like nii / woo and jell / nail as counterparts or correlatives, not strict antonyms.
Not quite. The lesson distinguishes genuine opposites from paired counterparts; nii / woo is presented as a correlative pair.

2) What is one major point the lesson makes about opposites in actual language use?

Correct. The lesson explicitly says that sometimes, instead of using an opposite word, it is enough to add the word bu.
Not quite. A major teaching point in Lesson 8 is that some contrasts are built simply with bu, not necessarily with a different lexical opposite.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Give one true antonym pair from the lesson.
  2. Give one pair where Mandarin can use bu instead of a separate opposite.
  3. Give one correlative pair and explain, in English, why it is not a strict antonym.
  4. Use either keen or gaan in a short sentence.
  5. Explain the lesson’s final message about learning Chinese.
