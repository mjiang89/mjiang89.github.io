Mandarin Primer — Lesson 23

The Vernacular Literature Movement

Lesson 23 begins with a messy and very human train-car interruption: hot tea spills, a child cries, passengers complain, and a waiter apologizes. From that ordinary disturbance, the lesson opens into a deep argument about books, schooling, classical prose, novels, literary reform, and the case for writing in the vernacular. It is one of the richest lessons in the book for showing how literary history can emerge naturally out of conversation.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so the page stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow the lesson’s large-scale structure: train accident, return to conversation, childhood reading preferences, college-level change, and debate over the Vernacular Literature Movement.
  • Understand the contrast between wenyan and bairhuah, and why that distinction matters culturally and educationally.
  • Learn vocabulary for novels, classics, prohibiting books, public reading, elementary education, the masses, abstruse theories, official documents, advertisements, newspapers, correspondence, pretending, being fooled, and killing time.
  • Recognize how the speaker’s love of fiction becomes part of a broader defense of vernacular writing.
  • See how the lesson stages a real debate: one side emphasizing accessibility and living language, the other emphasizing precision and established written norms.
  • Notice that the lesson ends not with agreement only, but with teasing, irony, and the feeling that serious debate can also be playful.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 23 teaches that literature is also a social struggle over who gets to read, write, and be included. The move from classical writing to vernacular writing is presented not as an abstract slogan, but as something connected to novels, schools, mass education, and ordinary communication.

For high-school students, this lesson is especially effective because it begins with immediate drama and then becomes a debate about why certain kinds of writing feel alive, accessible, or restrictive. It is a perfect bridge between language learning and intellectual history.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Train-car hook
Begin with the scalding accident: crying child, wet clothes, apologies, anger, and social repair.
2
Return to reading history
Resume the interrupted story of what the speaker liked and did not like to read when young.
3
Forbidden novels
Teach “idle books,” hidden novels, and the thrill of reading what teachers forbade.
4
College and reversal
Show how university study turns novels from forbidden pleasures into legitimate course material.
5
Debate over vernacular literature
Teach the two sides of the argument: accessibility and mass education versus precision and prestige of literary language.
6
Playful ending + quiz
End with the reveal that one side was partly pretending, then shift into midnight-snack comedy.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The “To the Student” pages stress utterances, repetition, and grammar-through-exercises. Lesson 23 works especially well when students can feel the shift from concrete scene to intellectual argument rather than treating the debate as disconnected from the opening.
  1. Act out the train-spill scene first, with strong emotion and quick turn-taking.
  2. Then put a big board arrow showing the lesson’s shift from everyday disruption to literary argument.
  3. Use a side-by-side chart for wenyan and bairhuah: who can read it, where it appears, what it is good for, what its limits are.
  4. Put the titles of the novels on the board as cultural objects, not just vocabulary items.
  5. Treat the debate as live and unresolved; do not flatten it into “one side is simply correct.”
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students may assume this is only a literature-history lesson, but it is also about educational authority, taste, public language, and who gets access to reading.
  • bairhuah is not just “casual speech.” The notes say it is vernacular literature, especially the currently used style of writing that still includes many new terms and literary expressions.
  • koouyeu is the literary label for colloquial language in the debate section.
  • shyanshu means “idle books,” an old dismissive term for novels, not literally books with nothing inside.
  • The central-dialect features matter, such as final particle sha, naalii sheaude, and torng for gen.
  • The ending is playful but serious: pretending to argue the opposite side is framed as train-time chat, but the actual arguments still matter.
Lesson walkthrough

Lesson 23 as interruption, reading history, and literary debate

This lesson becomes easiest to teach when broken into five connected scenes.

Scene 1 E, e, e, nii yeanjing kann de naal chiuh le? ... Nii saa de woo i-shen de! ... Nii bae hairtz tanq de, shoou dou tanq de tong-horng de!
Hey, where are your eyes? Look what you spilled all over me! ... You have scalded the child and made the hands all red!
Scene 1 Dueybujuh, chiing Nin yuan.lianq! ... Naalii sheaude huooche huran de i-tyng sha? Woo ig jann.bu-woen ...
I am terribly sorry, please forgive me! ... How could I have known the train would stop so suddenly? I lost my footing ...
Scene 1 Hao, kann jey-wey shiansheng de mianntz, jey-hwei raule nii!
All right, for this gentleman’s sake, I will spare you this time.
Scene 2 Woo sheau.shyrhowl budah shiihuan niann jenq.jing shu. Chwule Menq Tzyy, Tzuoojuann, gen Lii Bor de shy jy way, chyiyude jing-shu woo i-deal yee butzay.hu niann.
When I was young I did not much like reading serious books. Apart from Mencius, Tso’s Chronicles, and Li Po’s poems, I did not care for the other classics at all.
Scene 2 Woo youchyi ay kann sheaushuol, shiansheng goan ta jiaw shyanshu, busheu woomen kann, char.jaur le hair deei air-mah.
I especially loved reading novels, which our teacher called “idle books,” forbidding us to read them, and if we were found out we would be scolded.
Scene 2 Woo yeou shyrhowl toutoulde bae sheaushuol-shu tsarng de shujuol chou.tih lii — biifang Shoeihuu Juann, Hornglou Menq, Rulin Wayshyy, Sangwo Jyh ...
Sometimes I secretly hid novels in my desk drawer — books like Water Margin, Dream of the Red Chamber, Informal History of Literary Men, and the Story of the Three Kingdoms.
Scene 3 Howlai woo ruh le dahshyue, woomde gwowel jiawshow budann bu jinnjyy woomen kann sheaushuol, hair jiaw woomen na ta danq gongkeh niann ... tzay dah-tyng-goang-jonq, gongran de kann.chii shyanshu lai, nii chyau jeh dwo guoh-yiin a!
Later, when I entered college, our Chinese professor not only did not prohibit us from reading novels, but even assigned them as lessons ... and we could openly read those once-forbidden “idle books” in public — what a thrill!
Scene 4 Woo sh feicharng tzanncherng jeyg Bairhuah Wen Yunndonq de.
I am very much in favor of this Vernacular Literature Movement.
Scene 4 Wey chujyi jiawyuh, huohjee dahjonq jiawyuh, huohjee bairhuah shiangyi i-deal, dannsh ruguoo yaw jeang gaushen i-deal de shyuelii, koongpah koouyeu meiyeou wenyan nemm joenchiueh ha?
For elementary education, or perhaps mass education, the vernacular may be more suitable, but if you want to discuss more advanced learned theories, I am afraid the colloquial is not as precise as the literary language.
Scene 4 Tarngchaur de Forjiaw de yeuluh a ... Songchaur liishuejia de ... tongshyng de geh-joong gongwen a ... tyauiue a, faaliuh de tyauwen a ... shangwuh de hertorng a ... bawshanq de goanggaw torng shinwen, shennjyhyu pyngcharng ren shiee-shinn ... rengjiow sh yii wenyan wei juu ...
But the records of Buddhist lectures in the T’ang, the Sung philosophers, current official documents, treaties, legal clauses, contracts, advertisements, news, and even ordinary letters still take literary language as their main support ...
Scene 5 Ay, jeyg wenntyi yawsh chehdii de taoluenn.chiilai, jiowsh i-yeh shuo daw tianlianq yee shuo.bu-wan de lo ... woo daangj nii shianqlai sh tzanncherng bairhuah de, bush ma?
If we really discussed this thoroughly, we could talk from night till dawn and still not finish ... I had always thought you supported the vernacular, didn’t you?
Scene 5 Woo buguoh jeajuangl de jann tzay faanmiall de lihchaang gen nii shia-liau jiowshle. ... Ch! woo shanq le nii nemm g dah-danq! ... Che.shanq herngsh mei shell gann, jao g tyimuh sweibiann tarn, shiaumo shyrjian jiowshle.
I was only pretending to take the opposite position so I could chat with you, that’s all. ... Hah, you really made a fool of me! ... There is nothing to do on the train anyway, so I just found a topic and talked at random to kill time.
Closing Leang-wey shiansheng jiaw deal sherm deanshin ha? ... Woo chiing nii chy shiauyeh.
What refreshments would the two gentlemen like? ... Let me treat you to a midnight supper.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Public accidents reveal social language quickly

dueybujuh
yuan.lianq
mianntz
rau
  • The opening is full of apology, blame, comfort, and negotiated forgiveness.
  • This is excellent beginner material because emotions are clear and speech acts are concrete.

2) Literary taste begins in rebellion

sheaushuol
shyanshu
toutoulde
  • The speaker’s love of novels begins as something hidden and forbidden.
  • This gives emotional force to the later support for vernacular literature.

3) Canon and pleasure are contrasted, not simply opposed

Menq Tzyy
Tzuoojuann
Lii Bor
  • The speaker does like some serious texts, but not all of them.
  • The lesson avoids a simplistic “classics bad, novels good” position.

4) Institutional legitimacy changes what counts as worthy reading

dahshyue
jinnjyy
gongkeh
  • Once at university, novels move from hidden leisure reading to official course material.
  • This is a powerful statement about how institutions define value.

5) The Vernacular Literature Movement is framed as educationally useful

chujyi jiawyuh
dahjonq jiawyuh
bairhuah
  • One strong argument for vernacular writing is that it better serves elementary and mass education.
  • This gives the movement a democratic logic.

6) The counterargument is about precision and prestige

gaushen
shyuelii
wenyan
  • The opposing voice says colloquial language may not be precise enough for advanced theories and established written domains.
  • This is a serious argument, not a straw man.

7) Real language use is mixed and transitional

gongwen
tyauiue
goanggaw
shiee-shinn
  • The lesson points out that official documents, contracts, ads, news, and correspondence still rely heavily on literary forms.
  • This makes the reform movement feel historically unfinished.

8) Debate can be sincere and playful at once

jeajuangl
shanq-danq
shiaumo shyrjian
  • The reveal that one speaker was partly pretending does not erase the debate’s substance.
  • Instead, it models intellectual play, friendship, and argument as social practice.

High-value classroom move

Run a live two-side debate

Split the class into a vernacular-writing side and a literary-language side.
  1. One side argues from education, accessibility, and living speech.
  2. The other argues from precision, legal/official use, and established literary forms.
  3. At the end, ask students whether the lesson itself seems to favor one side while still taking the other side seriously.

This turns the page into an actual intellectual event for students.

A key structural system

Forbidden → legitimized → debated

Forbidden

shyanshu, busheu woomen kann, char.jaur

Novels are hidden, disapproved, and punishable.

Legitimized

na ta danq gongkeh, gongran de kann

University transforms novels into public and legitimate study.

Debated

bairhuah wen, wenyan, shyuelii

The question becomes not whether literature matters, but what kind of language literature should use.

Teaching payoff

Students can see how personal taste, institutional power, and language reform connect.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — What kind of reader was the speaker?

Students describe which texts the speaker liked, disliked, or secretly read, and what that reveals about him.

Task B — Why were novels threatening?

Students explain why teachers labeled novels “idle books” and why hiding them in a drawer matters symbolically.

Task C — Summarize the two sides

Following the page 7 exercise, students summarize the arguments for and against the Vernacular Literature Movement.

Task D — Extend the ending

Students write two more turns after the midnight-supper invitation: does the debate continue, or does it dissolve into food and friendship?

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) What is the strongest argument made in favor of the Vernacular Literature Movement inside the lesson?

Correct. The lesson explicitly connects vernacular writing to elementary and mass education, making it a broader social issue rather than only a literary preference.
Not quite. The pro-vernacular argument in this lesson is strongly tied to accessibility and education.

2) Why is the reveal that A was partly pretending to take the opposite side important?

Correct. The lesson ends by showing debate as both meaningful and social, with teasing layered over serious ideas.
Not quite. The playful reveal does not erase the debate; it changes how we understand the tone of the exchange.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Name two kinds of books the speaker enjoyed and two kinds he did not care much to read.
  2. Explain why novels once had to be hidden.
  3. State one argument for bairhuah and one argument for wenyan.
  4. Explain, in English, why university changes the meaning of reading novels in this lesson.
  5. Describe the function of the midnight-supper ending.
