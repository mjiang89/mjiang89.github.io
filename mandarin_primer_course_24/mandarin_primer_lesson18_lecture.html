Mandarin Primer — Lesson 18

To the Minsheng Works

Lesson 18 continues the Indusco storyline, but now the focus shifts from discussing the movement in general to physically reaching and visiting one of its factories. The lesson combines asking for directions, dialect misunderstanding, factory reception, introductions, product lines, war-related production, the Three Principles of the People, popular education, public opinion, and self-government. It is one of the richest early lessons for showing how language, place, politics, and industry all meet in one real-world visit.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so the page stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow the sequence of arriving at a factory: asking directions, handling dialect misunderstanding, entering, meeting the engineer, and touring the site.
  • Understand route language such as turn left, turn right, go past the second bridge, crossroad, T-junction, and sign on the door.
  • Recognize how the lesson uses Chungking dialect as a teaching moment about regional variation inside Mandarin.
  • Learn vocabulary for factory products, including towels, blankets, sheets, alcohol, soap, ink, uniforms, shoes, rifles, cartridges, batteries, radio receivers, kerosene, and gasoline.
  • Understand the ideological structure of the Three Principles of the People as reflected in factory names: Minsheng, Mintzwu, and Minchyuan.
  • See how the lesson links popular education, public opinion, and self-government to a printing press rather than a manufacturing shop.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 18 teaches how a factory visit becomes a lesson in language variation, industrial specialization, and civic ideals. It moves from practical wayfinding to a broader explanation of what different factories are for and why their names matter.

For high-school students, this lesson is engaging because it contains humor, confusion, and discovery. They hear an unfamiliar dialect, mishear directions, meet an engineer, learn about toy airplanes and real weapons factories, and see how language is tied to larger political ideas.

How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source’s “To the Student” pages emphasize utterances, echoing, and grammar-through-exercises. Lesson 18 works especially well when students can see both a route map and a factory network diagram while hearing the dialogue.
  1. Draw the route step by step on the board as the direction-giving unfolds.
  2. Use two colors for Standard Mandarin and Chungking dialect features so students can see what changes and what stays stable.
  3. Separate the lesson into three layers: directions, factory tour, and civic-political explanation.
  4. Use real objects or pictures for product vocabulary: towel, soap, ink, battery, radio, gasoline.
  5. Teach the Three Principles segment as meaning-by-naming: why each factory or press has that name.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students may think the main difficulty is vocabulary, but the deeper challenge is that the lesson keeps changing frames: first directions, then dialect, then factory products, then political ideals.
  • Chungking dialect can look intimidating, but the notes make the sound correspondences quite systematic.
  • shanq ... chiuh vs. daw ... chiuh is corrected inside the lesson itself by speaker B.
  • wanyell can mean “toy” but also “trifle,” which explains why speaker B misreads the joke about airplanes and tanks.
  • Minsheng, Mintzwu, and Minchyuan are not just names; they organize the ideological logic of the conversation.
  • tzyhjyh is explained in the notes as a literary-style compound meaning “to practice self-government,” not just a transparent word-by-word phrase.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 18 as route, reception, and civic explanation

This lesson becomes much easier when grouped into three scenes.

Scene 1 Laujiah, Nin a! Chiing wenn shanq Minsheng Chaang sh tzeem tzoou d'a?
Excuse me, sir, may I ask how to get to the Minsheng Works?
Scene 1 Enq? Nii sor sahtz a? ... ta bu doong niide huah. Ranq woo lai wenn ta.
Huh? What did you say? ... He does not understand what you said. Let me ask him.
Scene 1 Tsorng jey-tyau luh wann tzuoo joan ... tzoou-guohle dih'ellg chyau, tzay shianq yow tzoou, tzooule leanglii luh de yanqtz, jiow yeou ig syr-tzyh-luhkeel ...
Take this road and turn left ... after going past the second bridge, turn right, walk about two li, and then there will be a crossroad ...
Scene 1 Tzay tzoou-guoh.chiuh ideal, yow daw ig din-tzyh-luhkeel. Tsorng din-tzyh-luhkeel joan.jinnchiuh, nii jiow kann-dao i-tzuoh yang-farngtz, menshanq yeou g sanjeaul-shyng de jau.pair, nah jiowsh Minsheng Chaang le.
Go a little farther and you reach a T-junction. Turn in there, and you will see a foreign-style building with a triangle sign on the door; that is the Minsheng Works.
Scene 2 Liou Jinglii lai le ba? ... Nii sh bush jao Liou Gongcherngshy a? ... Ta hair mei lai, dannsh jiow yaw lai le.
Has Manager Liou come? ... Are you looking for Engineer Liou? ... He has not arrived yet, but he will be here soon.
Scene 2 Ranq woo jiehsha i-wey perngyeou. Jehsh Shyu Ryhshin Shg., gen woo ikuall lai tsanguan de.
Allow me to introduce a friend. This is Mr. Shyu Ryhshin, who came with me to visit your factory.
Scene 2 Beenlai juan tzuoh jey-i-harng. Howlai baa fannwei jiannxde kuoh-dahle. Shianntzay chwule shoou.jin a, taantz a, beydal a ... yow tianle i-men huahshyue yonqpiin, baukuoh jeoujing a, yitz a, mohshoei a ...
Originally we specialized in this line. Later we gradually enlarged our scope. Now besides towels, blankets, sheets, and similar things, we have added a department of chemical products, including alcohol, soap, and ink.
Scene 2 W oomen jeh.lii chwule jyh!wu, dahchaang, pyishye jy way, bu tzuoh jiunyonq-piin de. Yeou shie sheau-wanyell, haobii feiji le, jannche le, harngkong muujiann le ...
Here, apart from uniforms, overcoats, and leather shoes, we do not make military goods. There are some little toys, such as airplanes, tanks, and aircraft carriers ...
Scene 2 Nah buguoh doush geei sheauharlmen wal de mushyng jiowshle. W oomen chu de dongshi dahduoshuh sh puutong sheng.hwo bihshiu de yonqpiin.
Those are only models for children to play with. What we produce are mostly articles needed for ordinary life.
Scene 3 Li jeh.lii buyeuan hair yeou i-jia jiaw Mintzwu Chaang de. Tamen neng tzaw chiang-paw, tzyydann, diannchyr, wushiann-diann shouinji, binqchiee hair neng liann meiyou gen chihyou.
Not far from here there is another factory called the National Works. They can make rifles, cartridges, electric batteries, radio receivers, and can refine kerosene and gasoline.
Scene 3 Inwey yaw baotswen mintzwu de tzyhyou, jiow deei yeou gwofarng, suooyii jyhtzaw wuuchih de gongchaang jiow jiaw Mintzwu Chaang lo.
Because national freedom must be preserved by national defense, the factory that manufactures weapons is therefore called the National Works.
Scene 3 Ting shuo tamen jenq tzaynall yuhbey yaw kaibann ig Minchyuan Yinnshua Suoo ne ... Jeyg yinnshua-suoo sh yuhbey tyichanq pyngmin jiaw.yuh, gen fabeau renmin de yanluenn yonq de.
I hear they are preparing to set up a Democratic Press ... This printing establishment is intended for promoting popular education and expressing the people’s public opinion.
Scene 3 Inwey ruguoo yaw renmin tzyhjyh me, torngshyr jiow deei baa renmin de jyshyh cherngduh tyi-gaule tsair shyng a; suooyii tsair jiaw Minchyuan Yinnshua Suoo.
Because if you want the people to practice self-government, then at the same time you must raise the level of their knowledge before it will do; that is why it is called the Democratic Press.
Closing Shianntzay daa ha-dean le, chaang.lii kai-gong le. Lao Ding! Lao Ding! ... Ruguoo yeou ren lai jao woo, nii jiow shuo woo peirj leang-wey laibin daw chaang.lii tsanguan chiuh le.
It is eight now, work has begun in the factory. Lao Ding! ... If anyone comes looking for me, tell him that I have gone with two guests on a tour through the factory.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) Asking directions is a real communicative task

chiing wenn
tzeem tzoou
joan
  • The lesson begins with a practical question and unfolds as a real navigation problem.
  • That makes spatial language memorable and meaningful.

2) Dialect variation is systematic, not random

Chungking dialect
retroflex → dental
n/l merger
  • The notes on page 5 explain the main sound correspondences in Chungking Mandarin.
  • This lets students see dialect difference as a pattern they can learn, not just “strange pronunciation.”

3) Speaker B acts as a bridge between systems

shanq...chiuh
daw...chiuh
  • B has a smattering of the dialect and repairs the communication problem.
  • This is a strong example of linguistic mediation inside the dialogue itself.

4) A factory can widen its scope over time

juan tzuoh
fannwei kuoh-dah
tianle
  • The factory originally specialized in textiles, then later expanded into chemical products.
  • This makes the lesson a good place to teach development and change over time.

5) Humor helps manage sensitive topics

wanyell
feiji
jannche
mushyng
  • The joke about airplanes, tanks, and aircraft carriers being “little toys” creates surprise and lightness.
  • The notes explain why the ambiguity of wanyell matters.

6) Names encode political purpose

Minsheng
Mintzwu
Minchyuan
  • The lesson explains why one factory is named for people’s livelihood, another for national freedom, and a press for democratic rights.
  • This makes naming itself part of the knowledge content.

7) Education and opinion are prerequisites for self-government

pyngmin jiaw.yuh
fabeau yanluenn
tzyhjyh
  • The Democratic Press is justified as a tool for popular education and public opinion.
  • The lesson argues that self-government requires a raised level of public knowledge.

8) Administrative speech remains highly practical

laibin
peirj
tsanguan
  • Even at the end, the lesson returns to practical factory routine: telling the attendant what to say if someone comes looking for the engineer.
  • This anchors the ideological discussion in ordinary workplace interaction.

High-value classroom move

Use a route-and-purpose simulation

One group gives directions, one group acts as factory visitors, and one group explains why each factory or press has its name.
  1. Students first solve the route problem on a map.
  2. Then they role-play the introductions and product questions.
  3. Finally, they explain Minsheng, Mintzwu, and Minchyuan in simple English or Mandarin.

This preserves the lesson’s three-part logic: getting there, entering, then understanding what the place means.

A key structural system

Name → function → principle

Minsheng

ordinary living goods

People’s livelihood: articles needed for everyday life.

Mintzwu

weapons + fuel + defense

National freedom: preserving the nation requires defense.

Minchyuan

education + public opinion + self-government

People’s rights: democracy needs knowledge and expression.

Teaching payoff

This lesson shows students how institutional names can encode a larger political philosophy.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Give the route

Students guide a classmate to the Minsheng Works using only the lesson’s route expressions.

Task B — Dialect detective

Students identify two or three Chungking dialect features from the notes and explain how they would differ in Northern Mandarin.

Task C — What does the factory make?

Students sort products into livelihood goods, chemical goods, toy models, defense goods, and printing/education functions.

Task D — Why that name?

Students explain why Minsheng, Mintzwu, and Minchyuan are appropriate names for those institutions.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to the notes, what is one major sound pattern of the Chungking dialect described in Lesson 18?

Correct. The notes on page 5 explicitly list this as one of the main Chungking dialect correspondences.
Not quite. The notes describe several systematic sound correspondences, and the retroflex-to-dental shift is one of the clearest.

2) What is the lesson’s main reason for calling the planned press Minchyuan Yinnshua Suoo?

Correct. The dialogue explicitly links popular education and public opinion to the possibility of self-government.
Not quite. The name is justified by the press’s civic function, not by location or convention alone.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Give two steps of the route to the Minsheng Works.
  2. Explain one Chungking dialect feature from the notes.
  3. Name one product made at the Minsheng Works and one at the Mintzwu Works.
  4. Explain, in English, why a Democratic Press belongs in the lesson.
  5. Say what has to be raised before self-government can really work, according to the dialogue.
