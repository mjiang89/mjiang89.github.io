Mandarin Primer — Lesson 22

Studying

Lesson 22 moves into biography, schooling, literary education, regional speech, and the emotional meaning of study. A casual tea-ordering conversation on a train becomes a long reflection on ancestral origin, northern and southern speech, family-school education, the Four Books and Five Classics, Tang poetry, chanting, homesickness, and the transition from traditional study to modern middle-school subjects. It is one of the richest lessons in the book for showing how language learning, literature, family history, and educational identity come together.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so the page stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow how the lesson develops from a tea order into a long autobiographical story about language and schooling.
  • Understand the difference between place of origin, birthplace, and place of upbringing in the Chinese cultural frame of yuanjyi.
  • Recognize contrasts between northern upbringing and southern dialect heritage.
  • Learn vocabulary for family schooling, early literacy, classics, poetry, chanting, middle school, and modern school subjects like English, history, geography, physics, and chemistry.
  • See how the lesson links linguistic identity to emotional experience, especially homesickness and poetic memory.
  • Notice that studying is presented not only as academic work but as a cultural inheritance shaped by family, region, and genre.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 22 teaches studying as life history. The speaker’s educational path is not just a school transcript. It includes home dialect, private tutors, primers, classics, poetry chanting, parental influence, migration, and later modern schooling.

For high-school students, this lesson is especially strong because it shows that “what subject do you like?” can be the endpoint of a much larger story about identity, family, place, and memory.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Tea-car opening
Begin with the waiter, the tea order, the train whistle, and the relaxed opening that makes the later biography feel natural.
2
Origin and dialect
Teach yuanjyi, Changchow, northern upbringing, and why the speaker cannot really speak the southern dialect of the family.
3
Traditional education
Teach family school, learning characters, the Three-Character Classic, Hundred Family Surnames, Thousand-Character Text, Four Books, and Five Classics.
4
Poetry and chanting
Teach Tang poems, poetic genres, chanting, and the two famous poems quoted in the lesson.
5
Modern school subjects
Teach the move south, entry into middle school, and the expansion to English, history, geography, physics, chemistry, mathematics, and Chinese.
6
Identity and interest + quiz
End with the question of why the speaker studies Chinese now and the unresolved “opposite reason” cut off by coughing and the whistle.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The “To the Student” pages emphasize utterances, repetition, echoing, and exercise-based grammar learning. Lesson 22 works especially well when students can see a timeline, a dialect map, and a traditional-versus-modern schooling chart at the same time.
  1. Use a two-column board: traditional family-school education on one side, modern school subjects on the other.
  2. Create a life timeline for the speaker: ancestral home → northern childhood → southern tutor → family school → poetry with mother → move south → middle school → Chinese department.
  3. Project or hand out the two poems and read them aloud with contrasting tone: regulated verse and antique-style verse.
  4. Treat the quoted poems as emotional evidence, not decorative quotation.
  5. Keep asking students what the speaker’s educational experience says about identity, not just what books he read.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

Students may think the lesson is just a list of books and school subjects, but the real thread is how place, language, and education do not line up neatly.
  • yuanjyi means ancestral or registered place of origin, not simply place of birth. The notes explain that a person may be called a native of the ancestral place even if raised elsewhere.
  • Charng.jou is the Kiangsu Changchow here, not the Fukien place with the same common romanization.
  • shufarng, syshwu, and shyueshiaw do not all mean the same kind of school.
  • The poetic terms shy, tsyr, ge, and fuh are carefully distinguished in the notes.
  • The page 6–7 musical notation matters: the lesson is not only about reading poems, but about chanting them in different melodic traditions.
Lesson walkthrough

Lesson 22 as train talk, schooling story, and literary memory

This lesson becomes easiest to teach as five connected movements.

Movement 1 Leang-wey shiansheng chy sherm char? ... Longjiing. ... Yeou jiuhual meiyeou? ... Miengl tian i-lianq jiow daw.
What kind of tea will you two gentlemen have? ... Dragon well. ... Do you have chrysanthemum? ... We arrive first thing tomorrow morning.
Movement 1 Ryhshin ah, woo renn.shyhle nii tzemm jeou, woo tsornglai mei wenn.guoh ni yuanjyi sh naal.
Ryhshin, I have known you for so long and never asked where your place of origin is.
Movement 2 Woo beenlai sh Charng.jou-ren; keesh woo isheaul shengjaang tzay Beeibian, budann buhuey shuo Nanbian-huah, lian ting dou ting budah.
Originally I am from Changchow, but I was brought up in the North, and not only could I not speak the southern dialect, I could hardly even understand it.
Movement 2 Woomen sheauharlmen i-kai-meng de shyrhowl, woo tzuufuh jiow jiow ye Nanbian chiing le i-wey shuo jiashiang-koouin de shiansheng lai jiau woomde shu.
As soon as we children were ready to begin school, my grandfather invited a teacher from the south who spoke our home dialect to teach us.
Movement 3 Woo tsorng syh-suey chii jiow chii-tourl renn fangkuall-tzyh; wuu-suey chii-tourl jiow niann Santzyh Jing, Baejia Shinq, Chiantzyh Wen ...
Starting at four years old I began learning characters; at five I started reading the Three-Character Classic, the Hundred-Family Surnames, and the Thousand-Character Text.
Movement 3 Howlai jiow niann Dah.shyue, Jong.iong, Luen.yeu, Menq .Tzyy. Syh Shu dwu-wanle me, jiowsh Wuu Jing.
After that came the Great Learning, the Doctrine of the Mean, the Analects, and Mencius. After the Four Books came the Five Classics.
Movement 4 Woo shian-muu tzuey ay shy tsyr ge fuh ... meei-woanshanq jiau woomen tzyy.mey jiig ren niann Tarng Shy Sanbae Shoou, shooux dou niann daw bey.de-chulai.
My mother loved poetry, lyrics, songs, and rhapsodies most of all ... every night she had us brothers and sisters read the Three Hundred Tang Poems until we could recite them from memory.
Movement 4 Niimen dwu-shy heng bu heng a? ... Heng a. ... 'Yueh luoh u tyi shuang maan tian ... Yehbann jongsheng daw kehchwan.'
Did you chant poetry when you read it aloud? ... Yes ... “The moon goes down, a raven cries, frost fills the sky ... At midnight the bell reaches the traveler’s boat.”
Movement 4 'Chwang-chyan mingyueh guang, Yi sh dihshanq shuang, Jeu-tour wanq mingyueh, Di-tour sy guhshiang.'
“In front of my bed is moonlight / I thought it was frost on the ground / I raise my head and gaze at the moon / I lower my head and think of home.”
Movement 5 Howlai me, woomen chyuan-jia ban-hwei daw Nanbian, woo jiow tzay Nanbian jinnle jongshyue. Nah shyrhowl woo jiow kaishyy shyue Ing'wen, lihshyy, dihlii, hair yeou wuhlii, huahshyue ...
Later our whole family moved back south, and I entered middle school there. At that time I began to study English, history, geography, and also physics and chemistry.
Movement 5 Woo sheang woo hairsh diing shiihuan dwu gwowen. Suooyii woo shianntzay tzay gwowenshih a. ... Bush jeyg yuanguh, huohjee keeyii shuo sh inwey ig chiahx shiangfaan de ...
I think I still like Chinese literature best, which is why I am in the Chinese department now. ... That is not the reason, or perhaps you can say it is for an exactly opposite reason ...

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) “Place of origin” is socially deeper than birthplace

yuanjyi
ancestral registration
  • The notes explain that people can be called natives of the ancestral place rather than the place where they were born or raised.
  • This is central to the lesson’s opening misunderstanding about identity.

2) Language inheritance and language competence can diverge

jiashiang-koouin
Beeibian
Nanbian-huah
  • The speaker’s family is from the south, but he grew up in the north and could neither speak nor really understand the southern dialect at first.
  • This makes language background more layered than students may expect.

3) Traditional schooling begins before modern school

kai-meng
syshwu
fangkuall-tzyh
  • The lesson shows literacy beginning in the family school before entry into any modern institution.
  • That matters for understanding what “studying” means in this world.

4) Canonical texts shape the speaker’s early mind

Santzyh Jing
Syh Shu
Wuu Jing
  • The progression from primers to Four Books to Five Classics gives the lesson its traditional educational backbone.
  • It is not just bibliography; it marks an educational worldview.

5) Poetry enters through family affection

shian-muu
Tarng Shy Sanbae Shoou
bey.de-chulai
  • The mother’s love of poetry turns literary education into an intimate household practice.
  • This is why the poetry section feels emotional rather than merely scholarly.

6) Chanting joins literature to melody

heng
liuhshy
guushy
  • The notes on pages 6–7 and the musical notation show that poem reading is also a sonic practice.
  • This is one of the richest multimodal moments in the book so far.

7) Modern education expands the curriculum

jongshyue
Ing'wen
wuhlii
huahshyue
  • Once the family moves south, schooling broadens into English, history, geography, physics, and chemistry.
  • The lesson quietly contrasts older and newer educational systems.

8) Loving Chinese may come from a paradox

gwowenshih
gendii
shiangfaan
  • The friend assumes the speaker studies Chinese because he has a strong foundation and interest.
  • The speaker begins to say the real reason may be exactly the opposite, then the scene cuts off. That unfinished ending invites reflection.

High-value classroom move

Use a schooling comparison activity

Ask students to compare the speaker’s early education with their own schooling.
  1. What did he memorize early that modern students usually do not?
  2. What modern subjects appear only later?
  3. Where do family and school overlap in his education?

This makes the lesson feel historically alive instead of distant.

Study

Teaching payoff

Students can see how subject preference grows out of a complicated personal history, not a simple talent label.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Retell Ryhshin’s language history

Students retell the speaker’s early linguistic experience, following the page 7 exercise prompt and varying the places and conditions.

Task B — Traditional versus modern curriculum

Students sort all named texts and subjects into family-school, classical study, poetry training, and modern middle-school study.

Task C — Poetry as emotion

Students explain why the train vendor’s cry about tea eggs triggers homesickness right after the poetry recitation.

Task D — The unfinished reason

Students propose what the speaker may have meant by saying that the real reason for studying Chinese is “exactly the opposite.”

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to the notes, what does yuanjyi most closely mean in this lesson?

Correct. The notes explicitly explain that this is a social and ancestral place-of-origin concept, not simply birthplace.
Not quite. In this lesson, the whole point is that “where you are from” is more complicated than where you happened to be born.

2) What is the best summary of the lesson’s final unresolved twist?

Correct. The lesson deliberately leaves that explanation incomplete, which makes students think harder about the speaker’s educational identity.
Not quite. The ending works because it withholds the full explanation and leaves a suggestive paradox hanging in the air.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Explain how the speaker can be from Changchow and still grow up not speaking the home dialect.
  2. Name two traditional texts and two modern school subjects from the lesson.
  3. Describe how the speaker learned poetry at home.
  4. Explain why the cry of the tea-egg vendor creates homesickness in the scene.
  5. Give your own interpretation of the unfinished “opposite reason” at the end.
