Mandarin Primer — Lesson 14

Conversation with the Doctor

Lesson 14 continues the hospital storyline, but now the conversation is directly with the doctor. The lesson moves from immediate survival, through crisis assessment, internal-organ status, blood transfusion, whole-body injury survey, infection risk, modern medicine, and professional modesty. It is one of the richest early lessons for combining medical reasoning, anatomy, register, and cause-and-effect explanation.

This page keeps the book’s original romanization system so it stays aligned with the scanned lesson.

Learning goals

What students should learn

  • Follow a doctor’s structured update about a seriously injured patient.
  • Understand language for crisis, pulse, heart strain, blood loss, transfusion, organs, infection, and early treatment.
  • Recognize how Mandarin expresses clinical caution, reported judgment, and probability.
  • Learn anatomy vocabulary across ordinary and medical registers, including the table on page 5 for internal organs.
  • See how modern medicine and recently discovered drugs are discussed in accessible spoken Mandarin.
  • Notice the role of politeness and modesty at the end of the conversation.
Teacher lens

Why this lesson matters

Lesson 14 teaches explanation after danger has passed. The rescue is over, but now the speakers need a deeper account: what was dangerous, what was checked, what was treated, and why the outcome is hopeful.

For high-school students, this lesson is especially valuable because it shows how language can organize a complex update clearly. It is also a strong lesson for teaching how Mandarin handles technical material without losing human warmth.

Two-hour lecture flow

Suggested lecture sequence

1
Doctor visit hook
Begin with the greeting and the central question: is the patient out of danger now?
2
Crisis and survival
Teach fever, pulse, heart strain, injections, and the idea that the critical stage has passed.
3
Internal vs external status
Work through organs, bowels, urine, breathing, throat, thirst, and the absence of internal injury.
4
Blood and injury survey
Teach blood transfusion, plasma, and the doctor’s whole-body review of abrasions, burns, and spared areas.
5
Modern medicine and timing
Focus on infection risk, early treatment, sulfanilamide, penicillin, and how the doctor frames medical progress.
6
Professional tone + quiz
End with modest refusal of praise, polite thanks, and an understanding check.
How to teach it well

Effective teaching moves for beginners

The source’s “To the Student” pages emphasize utterances, echoing, repetition, and grammar-through-exercises. Lesson 14 becomes especially teachable when the doctor’s update is broken into a clinical report structure instead of treated as one dense block.
  1. Use a doctor’s report board with sections: crisis, organs, blood, wounds, burns, infection, prognosis.
  2. Put the internal-organ table from the notes on the board and compare everyday words with medical terms.
  3. Separate “what is wrong” from “what is surprisingly okay.”
  4. Make the timing logic visible: high fever yesterday, crisis passed now, early treatment prevents infection.
  5. Teach the ending as a cultural moment: compliments, modest denial, and polite thanks.
Likely beginner confusions

Where English speakers may stumble

This lesson is dense because it combines medical facts, technical vocabulary, and subtle interactional tone. Students need the big picture of the patient’s condition kept visible throughout.
  • dawsh does not simply mean “upside down” here; the notes explain its discourse force as something like “contrary to what one may fear” or “I am glad to say.”
  • sh de can correspond to English “no” in responses, depending on the logic of the question.
  • Ordinary vs medical anatomy terms differ: for example, page 5 gives separate columns for everyday body-part words, food words, and medical terms.
  • taangruohsh can suggest counterfactual “if,” but Chinese does not mark counterfactuality in the same formal way English does.
  • Compliment responses differ culturally: the notes explain that direct “thank you” for praise would sound immodest here.
Dialogue walkthrough

Lesson 14 as a structured doctor’s report

This lesson is easiest to teach as a sequence of clinical updates, each answering a different concern.

Stage 1 A: Jintian binqren hao.deal le ma?    D: Jintian dawsh keeyii bawgaw — ta hao de duo le.
A: Is the patient better today? D: I am glad to report today that he is much better.
Stage 1 B: Shianntzay shinq.minq dahgay meiyeou wenntyi le ba?    D: Sh de, shianntzay weishean shyrchi keeyii shuo sh yiijing guohchiuh le.
B: There is probably no question about his life now, is there? D: Yes—you can say that the critical period has already passed.
Stage 2 Woo tzwotian suoo pah de jiowsh ... fa-shau fa de nemm gau, may tiaw de yow nemm kuay, bujydaw tade shintzanq chydejuh chybujuh.
What I was afraid of yesterday was that the fever was so high and the pulse so fast that I did not know whether his heart could take it.
Stage 2 Suooyii geei ta daale jii-jen chyang-shin de yaw. Howlai kann tade shau jiuran mannmhalde tueyle.shiahlai le, woo jiow jydaw ta yiijing pyng-anxde jingguohle jeyg weiji le.
So I gave him a few injections to strengthen the heart. Later, when I saw the fever actually subside gradually, I knew he had safely passed the crisis.
Stage 3 Woo geei ta liixwayxde shihshide char-guohle i-biann. Woo kann ta bu shianq yeou sherm ney-shang de yanqtz.
I examined him carefully, inside and out. I do not think he seems to have any internal injuries.
Stage 3 Fey a, charngtz a, wey a, gan a, shenntzanq a, dou hair haohaulde.
The lungs, intestines, stomach, liver, and kidneys all seem to be in good condition.
Stage 3 Dahbiann sheaubiann dou tong. Hu.shi yee heen yun de, saangtz yee bu joong. Jiowsh lao kee, lao nawj yaw he-shoei.
His bowels and urine are normal. His breathing is even, and his throat is not swollen. The only thing is that he is thirsty all the time and keeps asking for water.
Stage 4 Suooyii i-ruhle yuann, woomen touri-jiann shyh jiowsh geei ta shu-sheue.
So as soon as he entered the hospital, the very first thing we did was to give him a blood transfusion.
Stage 4 Buyonq jao-ren d'e, iyuann.lii tswenj jiow yeou shianncherngde sheuejiang, sweishyr keeyii na.chulai yonq.
There was no need to look for a donor; the hospital already had stored plasma ready to take out and use as needed.
Stage 5 Yawsh lian ching-shang yee suann.chiilai me, tzoong yeou syhshyr-jii-chuh ne — duo de jeanjyr shuu.bu.ching le.
If we count even the light injuries, there must be more than forty places—so many that one simply cannot count them exactly.
Stage 5 Tour-pyi mo-poh le, shinqkuei tour-guu mei-shyh. Yow-eeltou, yow-lean ... gen byitz, dou tsa de heen lihhay; haotzay yeanjutz mei huay.
The scalp was abraded, but fortunately the skull was all right. The right ear, right side of the face, and nose were scraped badly; fortunately the eyeballs were not damaged.
Stage 5 Yowshoou de dahjyy, lian elljyy, jongjyy, dou geei huoo shau de heen lihhay ... Yowtoei de ker.shigall, yowjeau de jeauwanntz yee shau-shang de diing lihhay.
The right thumb, index finger, and middle finger were burned very badly ... the right kneecap and right ankle were also burned most seriously.
Stage 6 Kueide shiannday de ishyue, jinnbuh de jemm kuay, youchyish yeoule tzueyjinn fashiann de jii-joong heen ling de yaw ...
Fortunately modern medicine has advanced so quickly, especially because of several very effective drugs discovered recently ...
Stage 6 Jyyshiu jyh de gow tzao, keeyii bau nii nenggow wanchyuan bihmean chwanraan dwu-jiunn de weishean de.
So long as treatment is early enough, one can guarantee complete avoidance of the danger of infection.
Stage 7 Ah, yawsh woomen mei jao.daw Niou Dayfu, nah yee bujydaw nengbuneng yeou tzemm lianghao de jyeguoo ba?
If we had not found Dr. New, I do not know whether we could have had such a good result.
Stage 7 Ae, haoshuo vx! naal lai de huah! Woomen yee buguoh jiowsh jinn woomen shyng-i de inggai jinn de tzer.renn jiowshle.
Oh, please do not say that. Where do such words come from? We are only carrying out the duty our profession requires of us.

Core concepts

What the lesson teaches, concept by concept

1) The crisis is framed as a danger to the heart

may
shintzanq
chydejuh
weiji
  • The doctor explains that the central danger was not just fever in the abstract, but whether the heart could withstand the strain of high fever and rapid pulse.
  • This gives the lesson a clear logic of cause, risk, and intervention.

2) Passing the crisis is described as a process

jiuran
tueyle.shiahlai
jingguoh
  • The doctor does not merely announce improvement; he explains the evidence that allowed him to conclude the patient had safely passed the crisis.
  • That makes the lesson a strong example of reasoning from observed change.

3) Internal-organ language is layered by register

ordinary
food term
medical term
  • The chart on page 5 distinguishes everyday body words, food words, and medical terminology for organs such as lungs, stomach, liver, kidney, intestines, heart, and brains.
  • This is especially useful for showing students that vocabulary choice depends on context and register.

4) Bodily functions are reported matter-of-factly

dahbiann
sheaubiann
hu.shi
  • The doctor checks bowel movement, urination, breathing, throat swelling, and thirst in a calm clinical sequence.
  • This helps students see how a condition report is organized around systems, not just injuries.

5) Blood support becomes a key turning point

shu-sheue
sheuejiang
  • The doctor explains that blood loss made transfusion the first priority once the patient entered the hospital.
  • The stored plasma detail makes the scene feel concrete and modern.

6) The whole-body injury survey is anatomically detailed

tour-pyi
tour-guu
yeanjutz
ker.shigall
  • The lesson names many body parts in order to explain where the injuries were serious, where they were superficial, and where damage was avoided.
  • This makes it one of the richest early lessons for concrete body vocabulary.

7) Medical progress is explained through timing

jinnbuh
fashiann
jyyshiu
bihmean
  • The doctor argues that modern medicine and newly discovered drugs matter, but early treatment matters just as much.
  • The lesson repeatedly emphasizes “early enough” as the key condition for preventing infection danger.

8) The ending teaches cultural modesty

ae
naal lai de huah
laujiah
bukeh.chih
  • The notes explain that one does not directly accept praise with “thank you” in this context.
  • Instead, the proper response is modest denial, followed by polite closure.

High-value classroom move

Turn the lesson into a doctor’s briefing

Put one student in the role of the doctor and give them a structured checklist to report from.
  1. State whether the patient is out of danger.
  2. Explain what was dangerous yesterday.
  3. Report on internal organs and body systems.
  4. Summarize major injuries.
  5. Explain why the outcome is hopeful now.

This helps students hear the lesson as organized professional speech rather than as a random list of medical facts.

A key structural system

Danger, intervention, and prognosis

Danger

fa-shau fa de nemm gau, may tiaw de yow nemm kuay

The high fever and rapid pulse create the crisis.

Intervention

daale jii-jen chyang-shin de yaw, shu-sheue

The doctor strengthens the heart and supports the patient with transfusion.

Prognosis

weishean shyrchi yiijing guohchiuh le

The critical stage has passed.

Teaching payoff

This lesson models how experts explain not just what they did, but why the situation is now safer than before.

Guided practice

Practice tasks for a two-hour lesson

Task A — Crisis summary

Students explain what the doctor was most afraid of yesterday and what evidence showed the danger had passed.

Task B — Organ table

Students match ordinary terms, food words, and medical terms for the internal organs from the chart on page 5.

Task C — Injury map

Students label a body outline with abrasions, burns, spared fingers, and untouched eyes and skull.

Task D — Modest doctor response

Students role-play praising the doctor and answering in the culturally appropriate modest style.

Quick understanding check

Two multiple-choice quiz questions

1) According to the doctor in Lesson 14, what was the main danger yesterday?

Correct. The doctor explicitly says that what he feared was whether the heart could take the strain of the high fever and rapid pulse.
Not quite. The lesson makes the crisis very specific: the patient’s heart might not have been able to withstand the strain.

2) What cultural point do the notes make about responding to compliments at the end of the lesson?

Correct. The notes explain that directly thanking someone for praise would imply immodest acceptance of one’s merits; modest denial is preferred.
Not quite. The ending is a cultural lesson: praise is usually softened or denied rather than accepted directly.
Exit challenge

End-of-lecture spoken challenge

  1. Say whether the patient is out of danger now and why.
  2. Name one internal organ the doctor says seems to be in good condition.
  3. Describe one major injury and one fortunate non-injury from the lesson.
  4. Explain, in English, why early treatment matters so much in the doctor’s explanation.
  5. Give the culturally appropriate type of response to the visitors’ praise of the doctor.
